]]> By: Chris ../#comment-1364 Chris Thu, 09 Sep 2010 20:07:00 +0000 ../../../../../?p=146#comment-1364 Really great and thanks for being brave enough to put actual (in my opinion real world) numbers on things. One thing to add to this equation of "what will I make as a composer" is of course back end (residuals, royalties, whatever you wanna call them). And although you mention in your article that you are dissecting up front costs, you can get a much better sense of what you will inevitably make if you factor in some of your back end expectations-up front-so to speak. An indie film in my experience often doesn't make a ton (if any) back end unless the producers are able to get the film good air play on cable (good) or network (even better but unlikely). If you want to make money as a composer, you simply have to get your music on TV, somehow, somewhere, as often as possible. So what started out as about $15k in profit can end up being many thousands of additional dollars if you are able to get good air play from your indie film. Remember you won't see a dime from DVD sales, rentals and there are no residuals for theater play in the US. Foreign territories are different and do pay for theatrical performances although every territory is different. In addition, another important thing I would add to your list of considerations is "can I own this music"? Meaning, can I take this music I write for this film and exploit it, wether that means in a music library or license it to other clients throughout my career. This language would have to be included in the contract you sign with the producer of the film. If you are able to own your music, you will then have the power to control your music and continue to make money from the licensing of your music. Not to mention doubling your residuals for all airings of the indie film as publisher of the music. This can be a major revenue stream. The major caveat with respect to Dean's example is that using a union string section will very likely limit your options in exploiting the music to generate future income since every reuse will require an additional payment to the musicians and in the real world you'll have a tough time making the math work. Unless of course your licensing clients are paying big bucks (like trailer usage) and then kudos to you. If your score is all synths and samples or non-union then you will have more options to exploit your music for additional income. I'm not against using union guys in recording music, I'm simply stating a fact that using them will have an impact on your freedom to place your music in other projects. I think since budgets are so tight these days, that thinking about alternate ways to exploit and control and profit from music you have already written is more important than ever and especially important for young composers to consider as they start writing music. Great to see you here.

Maybe I should clarify a bit. I know you are a pop producer more than a film composer, and in that case, yes… samples work just fine. When I produce sessions for people, we always use samples and nine times out of ten those samples make it all the way to the final mix.

However, in film work, it is pretty widely held that samples don’t stack up in the face of an actual orchestra, which is what I’m referring to in this article. You can get away with sampled drums all day long, but a sampled clarinet, viola, or trumpet? Not so much. In most film score situations, the desire is to replace the sample with a live instrument.

I agree that manipulating samples is an art in and of itself. Even still, samples are samples. There’s only so far you can go with them before you simply run out of material to manipulate. They are only as good as their source recordings, and in an orchestral situation, that just isn’t ever good enough. I could be a master of sample manipulation, but an instrument in the hands of a seasoned player will quickly put those skills into perspective.
