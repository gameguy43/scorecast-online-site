Heather Fenoughty: One-Man-Band

June 24, 2009 Written by Heather Fenoughty Writing

As I approach the big 3-0 (yikes…), and put pen to paper (well, fingers to keyboard) to write this first instalment of my column on this month’s topic of diversification, I’m finding myself reviewing the last 10 years of my career with a resounding “Yes, as a composer it is soooo important to diversify!”

Image by the awesome Tom Jones

Admittedly this is my point of view, a conclusion drawn of my life experiences so far. They may change in the next ten years. But, hey, so far being a bit of a ‘One-Man-Band’ has worked for me, so it might just work for you
;-)

FOCUS

Focusing on music and composition, and film music in particular, has never really been a problem for me. In fact, it’s practically borderline obsession. I cannot remember a time when I didn’t want to write or be involved somehow with the music of film.
This led me to learn various instruments and then onto two music degrees, the latter in scoring picture, an invaluable experience, as it was here where I learned the similarities between composing music pieces and sound designing an entire soundtrack, using found or recorded sound to augment music or even be the basis for it.
I took my narrow focus on just scoring film to the wider possibilities of, and the fun to be had with, film sound.
After university I found even more so that diversifying my skill set led on to more contacts and opportunities. Recording sound on low budget short film sets led to an appreciation of well-recorded sound when I then brought them into the post-production studio… and also the necessity of learning ways of cleaning up less than optimal sound (that I vowed to record better the next time!).
Using organizational and basic office skills as a production assistant in a local, small production office meant I also ended up being first choice for the recording and post-production sound, simply out of convenience for the producers, and they could pay me as a package deal, probably ending up cheaper for them than sourcing it out to separate facility houses.

SKILLS

Even if your office skills are pretty limited, the fact that you’re a composing for film means that you’ve got a pretty broad skillset in other areas as it is. In the beginning it’s vital to use them all to get you to that holy grail of composing for film (or theatre, television, commercials, whatever floats your boat).
You’re probably pretty good with computers and related kit – get work in kit hire, and find out about all the films that are hiring kit perhaps by making friends with your regular clients.
If you’ve been freelance or self-employed for any length of time, you’ll develop some pretty awesome motivational and organizational skills (if you don’t you won’t be in work very long!). How might you put those to good use in some line of work that may put you in the line of fire for a composing role? Or even pay the bills so you have enough free time to compose music and build up your credits?
I found that working in music sequencers and timelines actually translates well to working in Macromedia Flash, which led to designing my own fancy website and then a few more for friends and friends of friends. After realizing that Flash websites get no love from search engines, some of the coding skills used in Flash translate over to working in html etc., which led to a little more web design. Maybe you could design websites to fund your addiction to writing music for film?
You probably play at least one instrument. Whether it’s through teaching or playing at functions, there’s easy cash to be made there, with very little time investment involved. You’ve already put in all the hard work learning to play the damn thing…
Honestly… at the very least being able to offer not only composition but sound design, foley, and mixes on short films and contemporary theatre really has gotten me more work. If I can offer it as a package, I save the client hassle and quite a bit of dosh. It’s gotten me more credits, more contacts and lots more word-of-mouth recommendations than if all I could do was crank out the tunes.
Or at the very least, it’s kept the roof over my head. And, let’s face it, more importantly, my studio. :-)

JACK OF ALL TRADES?

Does diversifying mean you become a ‘jack of all trades, master of none’? This really depends on you: how far are you willing to go to get the opportunities to put your foot on the ladder, or even later down the line, to continue to work in the industry when the credit crunch finally hits your neck of the woods (though this has yet to happen in mine, and I’m keeping fingers crossed it’ll continue this way. However, you never know what the future may bring – it’s always good to have contingencies!)?
The trick is to not worry too much about being the greatest in the world at every single one of the skills you offer, but instead aim to be the best you can be at the ones you love and at least pretty competent at the ones that pay.
Face it, even if you work only on composition, out of the billions of people on the planet there will always be someone who’s better than you, after all!
Embrace this knowledge, work hard at getting better and enjoy the journey.
Focus 100% on your role in the project at hand, learn everything you can from the experience, give it all you’ve got and you’ll come away from the experience better at that role than you were before. You may also come away feeling like you never want to do it again… but that’s a whole other post waiting to be written…
FULL CIRCLE

It’s even got to the point now where I have so much work on, I’m able to outsource a little of the basic stuff like tracklaying and simple foley… which means I have more time to focus on the music, the sound design, the experimentation, the fun stuff, and so the client still gets more bang for their buck.
Which (hopefully!) leads to even more word-of-mouth recommendations!
I started with composition and will probably end my career there, for that’s where my true passion lies, but I would never have got there had I not diversified and got as much mileage out of the associated skills as I have.
And I certainly wouldn’t have had as much fun along the way. Variety is, after all, the spice of life.
Sometimes it’s good to be a one-man-band.

    Great article, Heather! I did a lot of website coding as well, back in the day… and the design stuff I learned really helped me shape up my promotional materials and demos. Recommended for anyone and everyone.

    The idea of offering one-stop shopping for sound (and beyond!) is something that a lot of my colleagues are trying… I think that for certain types of projects and clients it's a smart move.

    A buddy of mine has been doing a *lot* of 5.1 album remixing for labels in the last few years, for example, and it's been a winner all around: he gets to utilize his gear to its fullest in between composing gigs; he learns more about the gear, about mixing, and about the latest audio technology with each new project; and he forges relationships that often lead to composing work.

    Once you hop the fence, the playground just isn't that big—and doing SOLID work, regardless of the job assignment, is a reputation-builder.

    Thanks for sharing a lot of really good ideas here, Heather! You're a great entrepreneur, and a good model for all of us!

    And I strongly agree that one isn't likely to get very far in this business without developing a lot of different skills — not all of which are “musician” skills, in the strict sense of the word.     In previous SCOREcast Online comments, I've often argued in favor of maintaining a steady focus on one or a few things, sufficient to become really good your passion — rather then getting spread too thin. I have to remind myself about this every day. But at the same time, I know things don't always go according to even the best plan. Thus, even though I try to keep myself focused on my top-priority goals, I maintain a list of ideas of how I can make income if I have to change course. These are things that come to me at odd times, like when I'm washing the dishes or just poking around on the Internet. When an idea comes, I write it down immediately, as if my life depended on it — because, some day, it might. I now have about 200 different ideas up my sleeve.

    One day, when it rains, I'll pull them out.
