"My Perfect 3": Lee Sanders

Posted by SCO Staff in Uncategorized | 9 comments

Editor’s Note: In the spirit of December’s theme (Gear and Software), we asked our contributors to weigh in with their Top 3 gear finds and why they like them, and we are bringing them to you in a series that we are calling “My Perfect 3″. Here are the three that Lee Sanders chose, and a short rundown on why he picked them.

Digital Performer
I imagine a lot of us will include our sequencing app on this list—and that’s probably appropriate. If you write music at the computer, your sequencing platform informs everything else you do. Its importance is paramount. It has to be solid, flexible, and powerful… and above all, it has to serve any need you might conceivably have in your composing life. Digital Performer delivers for me on all counts.

For me this application has, over the years, become a transparent filter. I’ve worked with it for thousands—tens of thousands—of hours, and my fingers just know where to go. If you’re a user of Logic, Cubase or any of the other major platforms out there, you may have the same feeling about your work canvas… at some point it’s just a matter of what you “grew up” using. For me, it’s Digital Performer—since 1989, when it was plain old Performer. For twenty years and counting, and I don’t see myself switching anytime soon.

Omnisphere
On the other hand, Omnisphere is one of the newest additions to my arsenal, and it’s just a sick powerhouse of a sound module. The variety and sound quality of what you’ll find here is unparalleled, in my experience… and the playability and “inspiration” factor are downright dangerous. I’ve caught myself spending hours on end in enjoyable tone patrol, just seeing what this monster can do. And when you add the processing capabilities they’ve added onto this beast, the entire thing steps up yet another level. Seriously… it’s unbelievable.

I’m not nearly done scratching the surface of what Omnisphere will bring to my writing. I’d sum it up this way: sometimes you buy a new piece of gear because of its range: it does many things you need (or that you discover you need once you’ve made the purchase). Other gear you buy because it does one thing incredibly well. Omnisphere gives you both these functions… and that’s just plain wrong. Spectrasonics is a company of absolute lunatics for letting this thing go at the current price point. There. I said it.

M-Audio keyboards (Keystation Pro 88 et al.)
For me there are two camps of controllers: the Really Expensive, Really Nice Controller, and the Really Cheap, Readily Replaceable Controller. If you have true keyboard chops, you probably want to purchase the former and keep it around for a long, long time. The exquisite action of the hammers… the glorious reproduction of an actual piano “feel”… all these things make a difference to you.

Me, I’m a hack. I can play just well enough to enter the information in when I’m sequencing… and that’s it. It’s simply not a skill I’ve developed. Fully-weighted keyboards actually make my hands ache after a few hours of hard sequencing. So I buy a new, cheap, semi-weighted keyboard controller every year or two, and I just abuse that sucker. Inevitably it bites the dust, and I buy a new one. But that’s a strategy that works for me. And M-Audio offers lots of bells and whistles on their 88-key range (and I’d recommend 88 keys for you—if you haven’t worked with the full 88, you honestly don’t know what you’re missing!), at a price that makes it possible for me to get by with my (lack of) performance technique. Problem… solution. Simple.

(P.S. I’d include my trusty Mac Pro on this list, but A) someone else is almost sure to do so, and B) Digital Performer is a Mac-only product, so I’m sort of able to cheat and include it by default—!)

  • http://daviddas.com daviddas

    It'd take too long to figure out my TOP 3, so I'll just chronicle three that come to mind first…

    1. Kontakt. A somewhat unintuitive design doesn't dissuade me from this king of samplers. Kontakt has really gone where no samplers have gone before and continues to innovate. It is a bottomless pit of possibilities (made more bottomless by the number of libraries that are released in its format) and it's far ahead of all its competitors (especially since it's former chief competitor, Giga, bit the dust). I might do an SCL clinic in 2010 about sound design in Kontakt!

    2. The UAD-1/UAD-2 card. These guys make the most amazing high-end plug-ins. They're not cheap, but they are worth their weight in gold. The LA-2A emulation finds its home in nearly every mix I do, as does the Neve 1073. And it adds no CPU load to your computer. For someone like me who uses a native (non-Pro Tools HD) DAW, this is a welcome bonus. I couldn't live without my UAD plugs for mixing.

    3. Metric Halo interfaces (I currently have a ULN-8 and ULN-2). My first interface was a MOTU 2408 (mk1). It worked fine. Then I stepped up the MOTU line to the 2408 mk2, and to other, newer MOTU interfaces, which got progressively better through the early 2000's. Then I took a chance on the first Metric Halo interface (2882) and INSTANTLY experienced a huge leap up in clarity. It changed the way I listen and mix. To this day, clients notice and comment about it. I'm sure there are other excellent interfaces out there (I'm sure MOTU's have continued to improve too), but I find it hard to give up my x-factor. :) The ULN-8 has some of the finest mic pre's I've ever heard in my life. Beautiful, clear, clean, and perfect for everything I throw at them.

    I might have to follow this up with 3 more because I can think of plenty more to write about…

  • lhsanders

    To the issues raised by “I b in da mand” earlier re: Digital Performer: As I've stated above, my experience with the software has been considerably different (and better) than what you describe in your post. My Mac, running DP, is typically as stable as I could hope… and I push that thing pretty hard. There are, of course, issues with any app that has to serve such a diverse user base (and with MOTU, the 64-bit issue is ongoing; see http://tinyurl.com/nfk8x9 for more info)—but I've found the MOTU crew to be pretty accommodating on the (admittedly, rare in my case) occasions when I've had a problem or an issue. So I can't agree on that one. But there's a whole lot more in that post that I do dig. :-)

    I like your idea of blending many sound libraries, and particularly adding the custom sounds you've accumulated over the years to create something extremely unique and personal—that's a crucial component of what sets us apart, ultimately! Also—and here's somewhere my studio needs some investment—the hardware you mention is fairly stellar! My philosophy has been to make my system as simple as possible, while still maximizing audio quality. That criterion demands a certain level of complexity, but with the power and depth inherent in this latest generation of techno-goodies, things are actually getting simpler all the time.
