Gateway Scores: Men in Black

By SCO Staff  /   July 2, 2009  /   1 Comment

When Jai asked us to write a Blog this week on the score that got us into the business, or at least opened our eyes to the industry, I was reminded of something I was once told in an interview I had done with a composer called Andrew Sigler. I keep coming back to this time and time again, as I think Andrew really hit the nail on the head:

“…I always wonder if [people] confuse their influences with their favorite composers, which are not always the same thing. I’ll put it this way, influences run more deeply and subconsciously than ‘my fave five’ composers. For instance, I love Stravinsky. And I love to say that I love Stravinsky; it’s a win-win. However, the reality is that going to see ‘Back to the Future’ when I was a kid had a much bigger impact on me musically. Let’s just say I REALLY wanted to play Johnny B. Goode as quickly as possible. In the long term, Igor has sustained me, but Marty got me on the road.”

I think the “Men in Black” score “sustained” me, but also “got me on the road”. I’m showing how young I am here, as most others would probably cite older films as their gateway, but I think this was the first film score I had heard that captured my imagination and showed me the possibilities of music in film. I don’t know what it was in the score that struck a chord (pardon the pun) somewhere inside me, but it definitively still resonates the same special feeling to this day when I listen to it. I think I was 11 when I first heard it, and I immediately got the soundtrack for my birthday – the first CD I ever had! I couldn’t put it down and just kept listening to it. I couldn’t believe the sounds Elfman was creating and I was hearing. The orchestral colours he used, the mixture of styles and sounds, the epic and whacky mood all mixed into one.

What’s funny is, I created a track about a year or two ago and subconsciously had written a section of music which was nearly identical to a part of the score of Men in Black – even though I hadn’t listened to the MIB score in about 10 years! I was sitting there scratching my head thinking….”why does this sound so familiar?” I couldn’t believe that something I had heard a decade ago had influenced me so deeply, that I could subconsciously write music based on it.

From those first four deep notes on the cello, we know we’re in for a ride. In my opinion, Elfman’s music for this opening sequence is close to perfection and is a prime example of excellent film scoring – every time I hear that opening sequence, I can see the dragonfly buzzing along the highway, swooping up and down, then getting splattered all over the windscreen as we see on screen. And that in my opinion is one of a number of things, that makes excellent film music – when you hear that cue, you immediately remember what it was referring to in the film.
