It's not the most glamorous subject compared to our core interest, and quite frankly most composers rightfully shy away from telling anyone about a day job for fear that it might be interpreted as \"not being serious\" about film composing. The main misstep was thinking that if I had just \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eany \u003c/span\u003ejob that paid \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ethis \u003c/span\u003eor \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ethat \u003c/span\u003eamount of money then I'd be able to leverage the after-hours time (and money) to make the rest \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ehappen \u003c/span\u003eby force of will. That's because (among a million other variables) everyone comes to the marketplace with different background and experience - as well as unique talent, aspirations and ambitions. That also means that there's no sure-fire skill set, tool set or any other kind of \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eset\u003c/span\u003e that automatically translates to success. This somewhat echoes Randy Knaub's great article from earlier this month – but it bears repeating – it’s a mental trap to think \"if I only had *this* [insert name of gear/software/contact/meeting/audition/job here]\". I’ve been guilty of that on all fronts, not the least of which is the job front – and since I just landed a really great day gig I thought I’d take a moment to share some perspective.\u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eTime \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eis \u003c/span\u003emoney – but the inverse is \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003enot \u003c/span\u003etrue\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eMoney can’t buy you love, and it can’t buy you \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003etime \u003c/span\u003eeither. The biggest mistake I’ve made in previous day jobs was to underestimate the time outside of “core business hours” that a job can demand. This varies from the simple logistics of an extended commute to the more complicated situation of a corporate culture that expects salaried employees to give up their personal lives for the company. This seems kind of obvious as I’m writing it, but it can be a slippery slope for a well-paying job turn into life-swallowing drudgery. In at least one case, an employer (part of a major film studio) actually hid that little nugget from prospective employees until they were hired on – and then were pulled aside for “the talk” from one of the supervisors shortly after joining. Not cool.\u003c/span\u003e \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003eIn another case, there were “social” and “volunteer” events that an employer expected everyone to participate in. And while it can be fun to socialize and goof off with co-workers to build professional bonds and friendships – it’s another thing to get the stink eye from the boss on Monday morning because you didn’t participate in a paintball competition with co-workers over the weekend. My point is that you have to guard your studio time like it’s pure gold, because, well – it \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;\"\u003eis \u003c/span\u003epure gold. Whether you’re writing music for your big break or simply sharpening the saw, it is time you’ll never get back. And like directors and film producers, many employers will try to get as much of that as they can whether or not they pay for it. In all cases \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eit’s up to \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003eyou\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/span\u003e to decide if it’s time well spent.\u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;font-family:arial;\" \u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eTaxing vs. Toxic\u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eOur relationships profoundly impact the way we perceive the world. It doesn’t matter if it’s the influence from a family member, significant other, or friend – or even arm’s-length interaction with a boss or co-worker – we are influenced by people we meet every day. As composers we’re prone to think of ourselves as particularly strong-willed and resistant to unwanted influence. But at the end of the day we’re all human beings. Given that a day job can force us to spend a significant portion (and sometimes a majority) of our waking hours with a relatively arbitrary social group, it can take some time to understand how that steers us personally. And generally speaking just because a relationship is toxic doesn’t mean it started out on a bad note. It’s like the cliché of being in a popular rock band where it’s all fun and games until it all goes sideways as the drummer goes into rehab the same weekend that the lead guitarist finds his girlfriend in the bass player’s bed. While not as dramatic, a toxic vibe at the day gig can sometimes mean too many after-hours happy hours that lead to poor performance at work and in the studio. It can also stem from a negative or frustrating work environment that you “take home” to stew on and mentally starve yourself of precious time to prep your demo reel or set up that next industry meeting. \u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eAnd while it’s too much to ask for a gig that requires \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eno \u003c/span\u003eeffort, the next best thing is to have a job that keeps you busy and engaged. Time flies when you’re having fun, and in this case, time flies when you’re working hard. It’s even better when the day gig taxes the part of the brain that’s unrelated to producing music. At one time I thought that I’d enjoy a day job that was closer to the film or audio business, and I was dead wrong. Even though my current day job is with a firm that does music production, I’m not in a studio during the day. So when I get home my place seems “fresh”, which is partly psychological but it helps to feel like “revving up” for a session. And aside from the self-hypnosis there is the actual physical advantage of not having burned ears from dealing with fixing other people’s projects all day. I’m still adjusting to my new routine, but I like it. Sometimes it means clearing my head by going to the gym after work, which seems counter-intuitive to exert yourself even further – but it works for me. \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eBe your own expert on this.\u003c/span\u003e Like many of the most important activities in life, if it doesn’t feel good you’re probably doing it wrong. \u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThe first (and last) three rules\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eLocation. That’s not to say that you need to be \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;\"\u003ehere\u003c/span\u003e, per se, but that you need to be \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ewherever \u003c/span\u003eyou need to be to take the next step on \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eyour \u003c/span\u003ecareer. Again, this is a lesson that I have learned in stages, and to varying degrees of effectiveness. When I first moved to Los Angeles, I lived in Westwood but commuted to Culver City for work. The lesson I learned is that it doesn’t do you any good to be near industry events if you’re returning home too late (or too tired) to participate. When I found another job on the edge of Los Angeles County I thought it would be easy to maintain active contacts and continue working on projects. Again, I was wrong – dead wrong (a recurring theme for this article). When I relocated to Studio City, things changed noticeably for the better – and now that I’m working a day gig in Hollywood “proper”, I literally see what I’ve been missing. The difference between being in Hollywood and being in the Valley is, in a word, dramatic. It’s just a few miles’ difference, but the change is unmistakable. I’ll explain by example: \u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI met with a producer (who has become a good friend) in a Hollywood restaurant after work one day last week. It was the first time in several weeks that we’ve been able to find a time and place to get together and catch up. She told me of an LOI (letter of intent) just signed with a film company and was setting up to do a short film as a proof-of-concept for the larger project she’s developing. She also dropped a few names of the actors that were going to be in it, which was all exciting to hear. One of the phrases that popped out was “and you’ll be doing the music for it” which caused me to do a double-take. She \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003edidn\u003c/span\u003e’t say that they’d be holding auditions – she didn’t ask me to submit a demo – she said I was\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003e the one\u003c/span\u003e. This is called “the catbird seat”, which for me is a bit of an odd place to be. And of course – I tried to talk her out of it. I know. I feel like a moron as I type this, but it’s true. But hear me out... when actors of a certain caliber are bandied about, the assumption is that the principals are going to find an equally “sized” composer name (or one of their proxies/ghosts) to do the music. It has happened to me more than once, and I thought that this would be no exception. But when it comes down to moments like these - \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;\"\u003egeneralizations don't matter.\u003c/span\u003e This is *her* project, and well, it was *her* decision. After I backtracked and we laughed it off, a person sitting at an adjoining table leaned in and said they’d never seen a composer try to “un-audition” for a gig. I responded with “it’s a gift” as I handed over one of my business cards. That night I got an email that he wanted to license a track from my online demo reel. I didn’t recognize the person’s name but I did recognize the company he works for. And, no – I didn’t try to talk him out of it.\u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThis anecdote directly relates to my day gig in this way: the meeting took place in a restaurant that’s in the bottom floor of the building I now work in. In that part of Hollywood it’s not uncommon to strike up a random conversation with someone while in line for the morning shot of java, only to turn around and recognize that person behind you as a character you see on television every week. (I thought his voice sounded familiar…) Never underestimate the power of being in the right place – because if you can stick around long enough the right time will come along. That’s why aspiring actors prefer to wait tables or sling lattes in Hollywood rather than make several times the salary in an office “outside the beltway”. One step off the beaten path is as good as being a world away. This stuff \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ematters \u003c/span\u003ewhen considering the right day gig as sporadically working composer. For me, it goes back to\u003c/span\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-family:arial;\"\u003e a famous quote from Albert Einstein: \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003e\"As far as the laws of mathematics refer to reality, they are not certain, and as far as they are certain, they do not refer to reality.\" \u003c/span\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eDealing with \"the day gig issue\" is very much the same, and even affects composers when spanning scoring gigs.
