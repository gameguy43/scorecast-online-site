Jim Well: Sample Libraries, Meet WIVI

July 15, 2009 Written by SCO Staff Technology

In last month’s Inside The Outside column, I explained that as a composer-in-the-making, I’m hard at work in the School of Chops, learning about writing for traditional orchestra. I want to know how I can create various timbres, textures, and effects – that communicate emotions and moods – using combinations of instruments and playing techniques. By studying scores, listening to performances, and emulating, I aim to unlock the secrets of great composers who, either long ago or recently, have written music that moves my soul. At the same time, I’m striving to perfect the methods I use to mock up complete orchestras and scores, using my DAW, studio gear, and collection of virtual instruments. These methods are an important part of my overall composing workflow – this month’s theme.

An Alternative to Samples
[In the following discussion, I assume you have some familiarity with DAWs, virtual instrument (VI) plugins, MIDI, and the use of sample libraries.]

You’ve heard it’s important to make your MIDI mockups as realistic as you can. Perhaps, when you hear realistic, you think sample libraries. What VI could sound more realistic than one based on recordings of an actual acoustic instrument?

It’s a good thought, but sampling technology has its limitations. In this article, I discuss some of these disadvantages and explore an alternative technology, based on a form of additive synthesis, that might better suit some of your needs. Sample-based VIs play back instrument waveforms that were previously captured verbatim and stored in a library. In contrast, additive synthesis is a particular way of mathematically modeling the acoustic waves produced by a real instrument and the instrument-dependent manner in which these waves change in response to input (e.g., breath, bowing, fingering, etc.). When you need to hear the instrument’s sound, the model is used to compute a waveform on the fly. This waveform doesn’t purport to be identical to the waveform the real instrument would have produced, but does aim to be perceptually very similar.

An effective variant of this modeling technique is the basis of the Wallander Interactive Virtual Instrument (WIVI) family, offered by Wallander Instruments. Existing WIVIs are explicitly oriented toward those composing for traditional instruments (though you can invent non-traditional ones). The combination of features offered by WIVIs is certainly intriguing, if not unique in the current marketplace. Instrument rendition is better than skeptical sampling addicts might expect, and in my own case, is often closer to my expectations than what I get from my samples. If I don’t like what I’m hearing, I can probably improve it, because WIVI sound is highly malleable. If you’ve ever tried tweaking MIDI mockups to sound more realistic, you know how important this can be, but may have noted that sampling technology offers limited adjustability at a detailed level. Most striking is that when you combine multiple WIVIs into orchestral ensembles, the result doesn’t sound “phasey”, organ-like, or otherwise unnatural, but rather convincingly realistic. Wallander attributes this to slight randomization of several attributes of each instrument’s sound, calling this feature performance randomization.

I’m actually using brass and woodwind WIVIs, in addition to sampling and several other technologies, in my studio right now. If you’re trying to decide how to spend your precious dollars and time, learning about WIVIs might aid your decision process.

Computational Issues, and System Cost
Let’s compare WIVIs with sampling-based VIs, beginning with computing requirements. Typical sample libraries contain many gigabytes of sample data. Most sample data must be read from disk, in real time, at the moment it’s needed. If you’re running many sample-based VIs (i.e., to mock up a full orchestra), you need one or more big, fast disks dedicated to your sample libraries. You likely need more than 4 Gbytes of RAM to buffer your sample data, and since a 32-bit CPU can address a maximum of 4 Gbytes, you probably need a 64-bit CPU and OS. Your computer needs a fast, wide datapath to move huge amounts of data from disk to RAM quickly enough. These requirements mean increased system expense and probable support headaches. But WIVIs, unlike sample-based instruments, don’t move massive amounts of data from disk to RAM. They’re able to run on much more modest computers, even many laptops.

Sample-based VI plugins pre-load considerable sample data into RAM when they’re initialized. You see this when start up your DAW – if you load a project with a large number of sampled VI plugins, you may have to wait up to several minutes before all plugins are ready and you can start working. This doesn’t happen with WIVIs, since they pre-load relatively little data. You get near-instant gratification.

“But,” you say, “I’ve already invested in a big machine to run samples, and I just go for coffee when starting up my DAW. So why should I care?”

Achieving Realism
I’ve already explained that the performance randomization feature of each WIVI is crucial to the illusion of realism when WIVIs are combined into ensembles. This is not only a matter of mimicking the imperfections of human players, but also counteracts adverse sonic effects that occur when combining sounds are too highly correlated, effects you don’t hear when listening to real ensembles. WIVI-style randomization may be the most important single factor in the ability of WIVIs to achieve realistic orchestral sound.

But there’s much more. Consider any wind instrument, for example. With the real wind instrument, you know that when you blow harder, the sound gets louder but also changes in timbre. When playing expressively, it’s second nature for a musician to vary his or her instrument’s sound – in both amplitude and timbre – within single notes, as well as from note to note. Because WIVIs are based on a model that permits both amplitude and timbre to be varied at any time, in response to breath pressure, they can reproduce this behavior and thereby lend themselves to achieving more natural-sounding playing. Sample-based VIs don’t have this capability – you’re able to vary amplitude within a note (e.g., by varying CC11), but timbre doesn’t vary naturally along with it. Likewise, whereas a WIVI models the way a real wind instrument’s pitch varies as a function of breath pressure, sample-based VIs cannot do so. Vibrato? WIVIs let you add that, as well, with continuous control over several pertinent parameters. Breath noise, key noise, attack, release, and those all-important note transitions? Just about anything you’d like to be able to vary (or not vary), you can.

Whence Articulations?
You’re probably used to seeing sample libraries that offer many different “articulations” for a given instrument. That’s a big part of how they attempt to facilitate realism. WIVIs aren’t set up that way, because with their inherent control capabilities you can create just about any articulation you need. In contrast, if a sample library doesn’t have the articulation you need, or the variant provided doesn’t sound right, you’re stuck. Trills are a good example. A typical sample library provides canned, half- and whole-step trills. But you know trills can be played in different ways to enhance expression – imagine wanting to imitate the uneven fluttering of a butterfly. WIVIs let you design your own trills, and each one can be unique if you’d like. Same for any other articulation.

On the other hand, if you want to speed your workflow, a WIVI lets you create your own library of re-usable articulations, using what are called Smart Sequences. You can then set up a keyswitch to call up any articulation at any time, just as you can with many sample libraries. Taking advantage of this and other WIVI flexibilities requires work and skill – but the point is: you can do it. That’s often not true with sample libraries.

Ensembles
I haven’t mentioned the unique interactive interface you get when you purchase any WIVI instrument bundle (brass, woodwind, and sax bundles are currently available, with more planned). Besides letting you adjust any WIVI parameter (many of which can also be set in real time via MIDI), the interface graphically depicts a performance space (such as a hall, studio, church, or anechoic chamber) in which you can place either a single WIVI instrument or an ensemble of like or different instruments relative to an imaginary listener. The position of each instrument relative to the listener establishes stereo pan, of course. But since acoustics of the depicted performance space are modeled, too, position also affects the ratios of direct sound, early reflections, and late reflections, and therefore the depth of each player within the perceived acoustic space. And if this reverberation implementation doesn’t suit your purposes, there is a way to bypass it and eliminate the pertinent computations, without losing the panning setup. I’m doing this because I need to use my WIVIs along with sample-based VIs from other vendors and I want all these instruments to run through a common reverb setup.

If you prefer, you can put each instrument instance in its own plugin. But if you like to streamline your workflow by using instrument ensembles, you may find WIVI ensembles to be a great asset. First, you can design your own polyphonic ensemble. By comparison, sample libraries usually offer only a limited selection of ensembles. Second, you can put an ensemble into a single plug-in instance if you like, which optimizes computational efficiency. Each instrument can be assigned to a different MIDI channel for individual control (subject to the usual 16-channel limit), or they can all be assigned to the same channel, or anything in between. Third, if several instruments are assigned to the same MIDI channel, you can take advantage of Wallander’s polyphonic mode to automatically assign parts of chords to different instruments in the ensemble – potentially saving you a lot of work. Controls in the interface allow you to specify the rules by which this is done. Fantastic.

If you’d like to see polyphonic mode and many other WIVI features in action, check out the Polyphonic Mode demo on the Wallander home page. Scroll down to the bottom of the page to find the demo. For a more intimate experience that lets you try out all of the WIVIs, I suggest the Wallander Instruments demo available at Try-Sound.com. And a free WIVI Trial Edition, downloadable from the Wallander site, includes the complete interactive environment and several (but not all) of the available WIVIs.

Scope of the WIVI Family
What about the range of WIVI instruments available? You’ve probably noticed I haven’t said anything about strings so far. That’s because there is no string product at the present time. Wallander is well aware of this, and is known to be considering adding strings to the WIVI family. Unfortunately, it seems doubtful that string WIVIs will appear soon, due to the complexity of the undertaking. For now, I’m using sampled strings – and percussion instruments, for that matter – from other vendors. They mix fine with my WIVIs, though I have to put up with the slow load times and realism disadvantages associated with sample libraries.

Coverage of woodwinds is excellent, as 13 different types are available (in three bundles) with three or more variations of each. In fact, in a project I’m working on, I needed an E-flat clarinet, for which a WIVI exists. I own many sample libraries from one of the major vendors, but none of them offers this instrument. Another major vendor’s sample library offers it, but the cost of adding it to my collection is high. This was one of the factors that pushed me to try using WIVIs, and I’m very pleased with the result. Similar situations exist with other “specialty” instruments.

There are two WIVI saxophone bundles, covering 9 different sax types. I don’t have these yet, so I can’t say much about them.

Four WIVI brass instrument types are available at present, in the Brass 1 bundle. However, if you take account of all the muted variations of the basic types, the WIVI Brass 1 bundle can actually be thought of as including 23 distinct instrument types. Adding another bundle of brass instruments is said to be high in Wallander’s priorities.

By the way, you can buy a WIVI Epic Bundle which includes all of the WIVIs currently available, at a large discount relative to the sum of the separate bundle prices.

WIVI in the School of Chops
I’m putting my WIVIs, sample libraries, DAW, computer, and skills to the test with a large project: sequencing Stravinsky’s The Rite Of Spring. Great for my School of Chops objectives. I’m reaching for the Holy Grail: being able to have the entire orchestra running in one, unified environment (one computer running Cubase 5), with everything available to be edited at any time – i.e., no tracks frozen. So far, so good. At the moment, my orchestra incorporates 38 woodwind and brass WIVIs, as well as sampled strings. I haven’t added percussion yet. Although I have a 64-bit computer and OS (Vista Ultimate), I’ve found it advantageous to run the orchestra under the 32-bit version of Cubase 5. This is because all presently-available WIVIs operate in 32-bit mode. They also run under 64-bit Cubase 5, but then Steinberg’s VST Bridge comes into play, creating significant overhead. Fortunately, by using relatively little RAM, my WIVIs help keep the memory used by my 32-bit plugins below the 4 Gbyte limit. Right now, until I add more plugins, I’m using about 25% or less of all eight of my CPU cores, and about 3.2 Gbytes of RAM out of 16 Gbytes available. So there’s still headroom.

Bottom Lines
Of course, you want to know how my WIVIs sound. Overall, I’m very happy with them. Most of the instruments sound very good. The jury is out regarding a few, which seem to sound more “breathy” and less resonant than I expect. I’ve been working around this problem with EQ – but there are a lot of WIVI parameters I haven’t mastered yet, and it may be that I can get closer to what I need by adjusting them. WIVIs should sound realistic, because the parameters of the model on which each one is based have been derived by automatic analysis of real instrument waveforms. And Arne Wallander is a smart guy.

Though I’ve focused on WIVIs in this article, my purpose has been to get you thinking about alternatives to sampling, with due recognition that there are worthy model-based products other than WIVIs on the market, and that sampling still has its place.

There is probably a lot more to talk about, but I think it’s best to leave that for the comments I hope many of you readers will post. I’ll join in as needed!

  • Jim Well

    I really appreciate hearing your views, William! I, too, am expressing opinions, and we don’t have to end up with the same view.

    In my opinion, the sound is great right out of the box for at least some of the instruments if you use breath control mode. Here’s how I’d explain that:

    When you use breath control, the amplitude and timbre change continuously with the controller value, rather than only at the note boundaries. If you don’t use breath control, and use velocity instead, what happens is more or less equivalent to setting the breath value at the beginning of the note and holding it constant for the duration of the note. I.e., more or less like a synth played without any continuous controllers. (It is a synth … but one that, theoretically, at least, can sound exactly like the real instrument that was analyzed when creating the model, if you give it equivalent input. The input is crucial!) I agree that any synth sounds pretty bad if you play it this way, and WIVIs are no different. In fact, even the real instrument would also sound pretty awful, and very “synthy”, if played this way! To get something that sounds right, you need to use breath control and sometimes other continuous controllers — the equivalent of what you’d do if playing the real instrument.

    It then sounds even better when you combine instruments into ensembles. I think this is aptly demonstrated by the “Polyphonic Mode” demo I mentioned (though you don’t have to use polyphonic mode to create WIVI ensembles).

    No question that Symphobia is a great product, and got even greater with Version 1.1. I’m using it myself. (I have to say, however, I often feel guilty when I use it, like I’m cheating …). I think there’s a good argument to be made that if you’re happy with the level of flexibility Symphobia gives you and you’re not too worried about your orchestrations sounding like those of every other Symphobia owner, using it can save you a lot of time. That’s valuable when you’re trying to earn a buck.

    In fact, my views are somewhat colored by where I am in my development as a composer. Right now, I’m very interested in combining individual instruments and seeing what happens. WIVIs let me do that. But I have to do a lot of tweaking at the level of individual instruments. Can I work effectively as a commercial composer that way?     William — thanks for your comment!

    I almost turned away from WIVIs early on, after listening to the MP3 demos Wallander has on its site. I didn't think they sounded very good. That was before I actually started spending significant time working with them.

    It took some work before I got used to the way WIVIs work and became adept at getting good results with them. For one thing, you really have to use the breath controlled mode to get what WIVIs are capable of delivering, and not the velocity-controlled mode (which is the default). That's not to say you have to use a hardware breath controller (although you can). You can edit the control contours directly in your DAW. That's what I'm doing. Along with the breath control trajectory, I usually also edit aftertouch (which, by default, controls vibrato depth), another CC I've assigned to control vibrato frequency, and yet another CC that controls note transitions. By playing with these things I can get very good results, so it's a matter of developing a workflow and a skill.

    And the amazing thing is, you can throw together an ensemble of WIVIs just like you can throw together an ensemble of real musicians, and the combined result comes out sounding good. Try that with samples and you're likely to be disappointed.

    As I mentioned in the article, while I'm happy with most of the WIVIs, I have some reservations about a few — but I'm not ready to draw my final conclusions on those yet, because I still have many features I haven't tried yet, and I think one or more of them might solve the problem.     Following up on a few things …

    (1) In my exchange with William Arnold, I had said I thought recalled that WIVIs defaulted to using velocity as means of controlling playing amplitude. William said he thought they defaulted to CC2 — breath control. William was correct.

    By the way, we're talking here about a WIVI parameter called “Breath CC”. You find it in the “General Settings” group of WIVI controls, and it defaults to CC2. You can set it to any continuous controller (CC), and you can also set it to VEL (velocity). If you set it to a CC, WIVI note amplitude and timbre respond to changes in the CC value at any time. If you set it to VEL, the amplitude and timbre of notes you play will respond to key velocity instead — but, true to the concept of key velocity, the amplitude and timbre are set at the beginning of the note and don't vary thereafter, so you lose an important component of expression.

    Now, if you don't know about the WIVI's “Breath CC” parameter or don't change its setting from the default, the WIVI will be expecting you to vary CC2 as you play.

    This is actually a very significant issue for someone who is trying out WIVI for the first time! If you don't realize what's going on, the CC2 default will likely lead to a poor first impression of WIVIs. Let me explain why.

    CC2 is the default because WIVIs are intended to be used with breath control. If you're playing “live”, it's best to be using an actual breath controller, like a Yamaha BC3. (If you're not playing live, you can edit breath control contours in your DAW.) But many people don't use breath controllers, and do most of their composing work with just a keyboard — the norm when working with sample libraries.

    The question is: How does a WIVI behave if you hook a keyboard up to it, don't have a breath controller, have left “Breath CC” set to CC2, and just start playing?

    The answer is: You'll have no dynamics! You touch your keyboard lightly, and you get a pretty hefty sound with more or less constant amplitude and pitch. You bang hard on your keys, and you get the same thing! That's because unless “Breath CC” is set to VEL, the WIVI pays no attention to key velocity! Beware!

    Deane, this might have happened when you did your initial WIVI evaluation. If so, it would have caused you to think WIVIs were pretty crude.

    Bottom Line: If you're playing a WIVI “live”, with just a keyboard, set “Breath CC” to VEL. This setting will give you some control over note dynamics. You won't have the fantastic expressivity you can get with a breath controller — but what you get is much better much better than no control.

    (2) I mentioned in the article that the WIVI environment provides built-in reverb which is highly controllable. I won't go into all the details here. But I would like to point out that, when using an ensemble of WIVIs to play orchestral material, I found that the built-in “Symphony Hall” reverb can muddy the sound more than I was comfortable with — at least when left at its default settings. For various reasons, I opted to disable the built-in reverb and use a combination of WaveArts MasterVerb and Voxengo Pristine Space reverbs. This gives me a cleaner, more defined sound and lets me use the same reverb for non-WIVI parts of my orchestra. (It's likely that by tweaking the WIVI reverb parameters I could also have improved the result, but this is something I haven't fully explored yet.)

    Bottom Line: If you're just doing a casual demo, the default built-in reverb will come into play. So I'd suggest being wary of what this reverb might do to the instrument sound during your evaluation.

    (3) You might not notice that, by default, WIVI vibrato depth is controlled by MIDI aftertouch messages. Thus, if you have a keyboard that supports aftertouch, then when playing “live”, you can add vibrato simply by pressing harder on your keys.

    And by the way, if you don't like the default sound, you can adjust various parameters to change it.

    (4) Another detail that will almost certainly escape notice during an initial evaluation is the control WIVIs offer over note transitions — i.e., more or less legato, portamento, etc. This is another area where, if you're doing a quick initial evaluation and don't know how to control note transitions or even that they can be controlled, you might hear things that don't meet your expectations and conclude that WIVIs don't sound very good.

    In practice, I've been finding that, for my taste, exerting control over note transitions is quite important. At the moment, however, I'm using far less of the note transition control capabilities than are available. I simply assign the “Transition CC” parameter to be controlled by a MIDI continuous controller of my choice, and then set a CC value according to my taste in any given musical passage. (I'm generally doing this in my DAW.) If you don't alter the “Transition CC” parameter, your WIVI defaults to making transition speeds respond to key velocity. My experience suggests that, for me, anyway, that's not such a good choice.

    The relevant controls are found at the bottom of the “Control Parameters” section of the WIVI controls.

    (5) Deane's most recent comment (above) includes specific mention of sample libraries he likes to use, and why he likes them. I virtually worship Deane, but in trying to accomplish what I've been interested in doing in recent months, I came to more or less the opposite conclusions. It was disappointment with various aspects of those libraries that drove me to explore WIVI. I don't yet know where I'll end up.

    So, keep the comments coming — not only on this article, but on all of them. Let's try to get this thing right!

    Deane asks, “Is it starting to not matter anyway what a *real* orchestra sounds like since people don't really know to begin with?”

    I think the answer is a little tricky. My first inclination is to answer that it's mattering less than it used to. But from the point of view of a composer who both uses MIDI mockups and writes for traditional orchestra with the intention of recording the real orchestra, I think it matters a lot.

    If you work with MIDI mockups all the time, and don't get re-calibrated by listening to real orchestras, you start thinking as if the MIDI sound is a reliable reading on what the real orchestra will sound like when you record it playing your score. Then, when you actually record the orchestra, you find out that the balance of the real orchestra (and maybe even the sounds of the individual instruments) is different. If the balance is out of whack, the timbres and textures will be out of whack as a result. What worked in MIDI might not work with the real orchestra. Scary prospect.

    It seems the antidote is that a composer needs to make it a priority to stay calibrated on the sounds of real orchestras. Maybe even on the sound of a particular real orchestra, playing on a particular scoring stage.

    William -

    I don't have an expert answer to this question yet. However, here's what I think:

    (1) As I've maintained consistently, using breath control (or, at least, something that you can vary continuously) is very important if you want to play expressively. You can set a WIVI flute to give you faster attack on every note, but then you're stuck with that articulation and have discarded an important means of expression.

    If you're playing live or entering a musical line into your DAW by playing in real time, breath control is especially great as the principal thing you vary, because it's feasible to vary it with each note — e.g., to get a sharp attack on one note and a gradual attack on the next. In theory, you could do that with, say, the mod wheel, but you'd have to be awfully fast and accurate to get close to what you can do with breath.

    (The above breath control advantage, of course, is in addition to the fact that breath control varies both the amplitude and the timbre of the currently-sounding note — at any time.)

    OK, now I'll stop beating incessantly on that point!

    (2) Next, let's assume you're playing the WIVI from your keyboard, are not using a breath controller, and just want to set your WIVI so that all notes have a faster (but not necessarily equal) attack.

    First, remember to set Breath CC to VEL in the General Settings. That way, at least you have some expressivity control, because in this mode, various properties of notes and note transitions will vary as a function of note velocity (key velocity). In addition, if you have a keyboard that generates aftertouch messages according to how much pressure you exert on the keys after pressing them, you can use this as a very easy way to control vibrato depth.

    Now, given the above setting, attack will get faster as key velocity increases. However, there will be some maximum attack speed (minimum attack time). If that maximum attack speed isn't fast enough for you, you can change it.

    To increase the maximum attack speed (or, equivalently, decrease the minimum attack time), select the Control parameters panel by clicking on the rightmost button just below the place where you select instruments. Then use your mouse to grab the control panel and scroll it down until you see the area with the heading “BREATH ENVELOPE”. In this area, you'll find a set of parameters that affect things WIVI will do automatically to the breath signal as you play. (I.e., for each note, the WIVI generates for you a puff of breath having one shape or another, given that you're not using a breath controller to create this puff yourself — and the parameters let you influence the shape that the WIVI gives to that puff.) As you can see, there are several things you could modify to influence the maximum attack speed. For example, if you increase the value of the “Key attack spd.” parameter, you'll be able to get faster attacks on your flute notes. You can set the value very high and get very fast attacks — but then, of course, you'll get a sound that's different from a real flute. If that's what you want, great. If not, be more moderate in your setting.

    I'd suggest experimenting around with the various parameters in this area, to see what they do. I'm by no means an expert on all of them.

    TIP: If you haven't discovered this yet, the WIVI interface has built-in documentation in the form of “tool tips”. For example, if you want to see the documentation for the “Key attack spd.” parameter, double-click in the field where you set the value for this parameter, and the documentation will appear as a “tool tip”. Click once on the tooltip window to make it go away.

    (3) One problem with many of the parameters in the BREATH ENVELOPE area (specifically, from “Breath speed” down to “Key release spd.”) is that you can't assign a MIDI CC to them. If you could, you'd be able to vary their values in real time as you play. But you can't.

    <>However, though it may not be obvious, MIDI CCs can be assigned to another group of these parameters. Just above the “Breath speed” parameter you'll see a window that graphically depicts the shape of the breath envelope, and above that, a field where you select the name of a parameter that you want to adjust. If you're coming here for the first time, this field will say “Attack time”. You set its value via the three controls just below the breath envelope graphic display. Now, click once on the arrow button to the right of the parameter name field. If the field initially said “Attack time”, it will now say “Attack time CC”. I.e., this is how you can assign a continuous controller (CC) to the Attack time parameter. If you haven't assigned a CC before, I think this CC will be set to VEL — meaning that attack time is controlled by key velocity. Thus if you want to direct control over Attack time rather than live with the automatic linkage of Attack time to velocity, assign a CC and control it yourself.

    (4) I suspect you might also be able to change the note articulations of your WIVI flute to be more to your liking by adjusting the controls in the TRANSITIONS, PORTAMENTO, and LEGATO areas, which are further down in the Control parameters panel. These controls also have the advantage that you can assign MIDI CCs to them. For example, when working on a musical line in my DAW, I find it can be very helpful to set the “Transition CC” parameter to assign a CC for note transition control. Then, if something doesn't sound right, I have a control I can adjust (by hand-editing the control contour in my DAW) — and voila!

    Well — enough for now. As you can see, there's a whole world to explore here. Imagine being an expert on all of this, and developing the chops to work all of these controls as fast as you can think!
