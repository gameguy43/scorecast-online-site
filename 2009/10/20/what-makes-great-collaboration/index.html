Deane Ogden: What Makes a “Great” Collaboration?

Posted by Deane Ogden on Tuesday, October 20, 2009 · Leave a Comment 

This last weekend, a good friend of mine from northern California stayed with us for three days as he finished business here in LA on a little festival film that I scored for him earlier this year. It dawned on me this weekend — as we sat around, drinking endless amounts of CocaCola Slurpees, and talking movies — just how much fun it is to collaborate with someone you also have a solid friendship with. Many great conversations were had, but one sticks out the most, and is an interesting discussion starter here:

If we didn’t have our projects in common with our directors, would we still be friends?

As for me and my friend Scott, we really couldn’t answer that question. Who knows? The fact is that we do have film in common, so the point is sort of moot. But that got me thinking about us as collaborators. What makes it work?

Scoring “Bad Faith” for Scott was a near perfect experience. In fact my last three or four films have been some pretty awesome times. But I’ve also been a part of filmmaking teams when the process was an exercise in utter agony. Sometimes the shoe just isn’t the right fit, and even though you may make it through the storm this time as a team, it is likely that the next go’round will involve a different group of storm-chasers.

All of this reminiscing leads me to examine some of the collaborations that have proven successful throughout the history of film, and even a few that have sparked up as of late. Hitchcock and Herrmann. Burton and Elfman. The Coens and Burwell. Singer and Ottman. Spielberg and Williams. Lucas and Williams. Anybody and Williams??

You could say that Hitchcock and Herrmann were cut from the same cloth. It might be said that Spielberg and Williams are visionary icons that found each other at just the right time and place, and a perfect partnership was forged. Bryan Singer and John Ottman met in film school and have been creative partners ever since, save for one film where schedules conflicted.

Not just in the film industry, however, great collaboration is a cross-compatible phenomenon. Mick Jagger and Keith Richards. Larry Page and Sergey Brin. Coco Chanel and Pierre Wertheimer. Bill Hewlitt and David Packard. The Beatles and George Martin. Will and Mike on Big Brother 2. (I’m not kidding about that last one!)

What is it that really makes a collaboration great? Do you need certain non-negotiable variables to be in place in order that an incredible and perfect working relationship can occur, or do they just naturally happen as the Universe sees fit? Is a little bit of tension important, or is an environment free of friction and conflict the best way to work?

1. When you really put some thought into it, what have been the variables to your own successful collaborations?

2. And… conversely, what things will kill a potentially great collaboration?

Inquiring minds want to know… in the COMMENTS below!

Deane Ogden

SCOREcast founder and editor-in-chief, Deane Ogden, is an American composer for film, television, video games and the concert stage. Deane is also an accomplished studio and live drummer, and has recorded and performed live with acts as diverse as Tina Turner, Seal, David Foster, Rhianna, Britney Spears, and more. Deane was commissioned to write the orchestral themes for Michael Phelps’ record-breaking gold medal run during the 2008 Beijing Olympic Games—the most-viewed event in American television history.

As an advocate for professional creative arts education, Deane speaks to creative and cultural organizations worldwide on the convergence of art and business. You can hear Deane’s music and follow his most recent globe-trotting happenings at DeaneOgden.com.
