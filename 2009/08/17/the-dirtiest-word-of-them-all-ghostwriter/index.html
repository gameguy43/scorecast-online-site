Deane Ogden: The Dirtiest Word of Them All: Ghostwriter

Posted by Deane Ogden on Monday, August 17, 2009 · 3 Comments 

Oh…. we HATE to say it! That “dirty phrase” in composing that we prefer to avoid, yet it comes up in conversation almost daily. Every successful composer says they will not do it, but most of them do it anyway. Most composers, working or not, condemn the idea, but there seems to be no way around it and it almost feels like a necessary evil if you want to be successful at this gig.

I recently attended a panel where a group of popular TV and film composers were assembled to talk about their work and answer questions from a hungry crowd made up mostly of new people wanting to break into the industy. The talk turned to how many shows a person could handle writing for at one time. A question from the audience came up for one of the composers who is currently writing for several successful shows in network TV.

“Do you do it all?”, the audience member asked.

“I do it all myself,” said the composer, “and it’s difficult, but I just have to discipline myself and it all works out.”

Unfortunately, I know for a fact that that was a bald-face lie. That composer does NOT do it all themselves.

They have “ghosts”.

Let me offer this honest (and quite common) scenario, and you tell me what’s right and what’s wrong:

You just landed the gig of your life — you are composing for a new show on one of the big TV networks and the show is a runaway sensation… And so are you — in fact, you’ve never seen weekly checks like this since you started composing, and your projected backend royalties in nine months are looking pretty damned amazing (You might even be able to pony up the down payment on that house you’ve had your eye on!).

All of this success has now put you on the radar of many execs, and you are getting job offers left and right. The thing is though, if you were to let the world see you sweat in your little project studio, you are barely treading water and it’s everything you can do to just keep up with the pace of the show you are on now.

At the same time, you know it’s Business Smarts 101 to “take every gig”, and just because you are now writing for a weekly smash hit, that rule doesn’t stop applying.

The choice is simple: You need a ghost if you are going to take more on. Simple as that.

But how do you feel about that concept?

Honestly? EVERYONE does it. I mean, they just do. I’m not kidding. All the big boys do it, and I just know this because I’ve hung around with many of them and they’ll just eventually tell you in secret that they couldn’t do it all if they didn’t employ a ghostwriter. Simple. Back in the day, several classical composers even had ghosts, and nobody seems to be mad at them for it, so what’s the big deal?

So here it is:
Is having a ghost a necessary evil to enjoying a long career as a composer? If so, do you tell, or is it ethical to just say you do it yourself? What about backend? Should your ghosts get theirs, or should they simply be paid as an employee and that’s that? Is there another alternative to getting all the work you can and still being able to go it alone? If so, what are some of those scenarios?

Jump in on the COMMENTS below, and let’s hear your thoughts… and, let’s be honest about it. No hurt feelings… no opinions that aren’t valid, yada, yada, yada.

It IS what it IS, and it’s something that, sooner or later, you are going to have to confront one way or another. At least, we hope so, right?

Deane Ogden

SCOREcast founder and editor-in-chief, Deane Ogden, is an American composer for film, television, video games and the concert stage. Deane is also an accomplished studio and live drummer, and has recorded and performed live with acts as diverse as Tina Turner, Seal, David Foster, Rhianna, Britney Spears, and more. Deane was commissioned to write the orchestral themes for Michael Phelps’ record-breaking gold medal run during the 2008 Beijing Olympic Games—the most-viewed event in American television history. As an advocate for professional creative arts education, Deane speaks to creative and cultural organizations worldwide on the convergence of art and business. You can hear Deane’s music and follow his most recent globe-trotting happenings at DeaneOgden.com.
