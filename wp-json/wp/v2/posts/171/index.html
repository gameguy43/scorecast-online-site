I’m about to embark on an experiment of sorts, and I’d like to share my progress with you as I go. We all know how tough it can be sometimes to make enough money from composing – in particular when starting off. Some of us have a part time job while starting off, some have a full time job – others have no side jobs – just composing.<\/p>\n

So… how can we make more money from our music, even after it has been used for its original purpose of a film score, etc.? We sometimes forget that the money making process doesn’t always have to stop there – we can keep licensing our music (depending on the original license we signed with the director\/creator) after the original project. It’s not all about the money, but let’s not fool ourselves – we all need money to survive, and the more the merrier. \ud83d\ude42<\/p>\n

I currently sell some of my music on stock music libraries and make between $200-$350 per month – which is nice pocket money for paying the bills, or buying whatever. However, that money is generally only 50% or less, of the net profit that my music makes – the websites I sell on can take from 50% – 75% of the money my music earns, which can often be quite disheartening.<\/p>\n

Therefore, I looked into ways to make 100% profit on selling my music – then it hit me – I sell a collection of about 12 piano tracks on a website about 4 or 5 times a month, and it sells for $100 a pop (of which I earn 50%). So…if I could sell a CD for $100, why not sell it on my own website for $100 for licensing purposes or $20 for personal listening, and earn all of the profit? So I contacted some CD duplication companies to see how much it would cost to get 50 CDs created, and started looking at the sums:<\/p>\n

Expenditure:<\/span>
\n<\/a>50 CDs = $283 (CDs include colour artwork printed on CD, jewelcases, album cover and back)
\nDelivery = $24
\n__________
\nTotal = $307<\/p>\n

If we then take a look at the total possible income, the money starts to add up nicely. Lets say I sell 30 CDs for personal listening (I’m sure I could find enough friends and family to buy them! LOL) and then 20 CDs for licensing purposes.<\/p>\n

30 x $20 = $600
\n20 x $100 = $2000
\n_____________
\nNet Total = $2600
\n______________________________
\nGross Total Possible Income<\/span>= $2293<\/span>
\n_____________________________<\/p>\n

OK – so the above sums may <\/span>be wishful thinking, but it certainly looks like it could be a profitable venture, so why not try? My CDs are being made this week and should be ready in a few days time. I’ll share my progress with you as I go – I think it will be a worthwhile experiment.<\/p>\n

The next questions is – how do I sell them\/market them? That’s where things get interesting… I want to make 100% of the profit so I won’t be using services such as CD Baby<\/a> to sell my CDs for me – at least for now. I’ll be trying a few different marketing ideas and I’ll let you know which work, and which don’t.<\/p>\n

In Part 2 of this article (coming soon), I’ll show you how to license music on your website, rather than relying on other sites to do it for you.<\/p>\n
