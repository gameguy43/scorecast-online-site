]]> By: Deane Ogden ../#comment-238 Tue, 20 Oct 2009 23:04:53 +0000 ../../../../../?p=171#comment-238 Probably the number one rule in music retail is “Add Value to Every Sale.” It seems that the music business is in such a state that it is no longer good enough – in fact, it's expected – that the customer get more for their 10 or 15 bucks than just a program's worth of music. The boundaries of this concept are being broadned yet again by Apple's recent introduction of “iTunes LP”, which is pretty amazing if you haven't already checked it out.

Mike brings up an interesting point above with his explanation of how DiscMakers handles you waiting awhile between orders, and their choice to dump your material, presumably to make space on their servers for people who are making them money right this second.

Why not take a cue from the labels that are still left (all whom are struggling, by the way) and use your initial order run of 100-200, or even 1000 (f you have that kind of a fanbase), CDs and make them “limited-edition signed” copies. When they run out, they are gone. Just MovieScore Media, ScreenArchives, or Varese does. Make it clear that after those run out, the only places left to purchase that album are view the digital download sites that you use.

Seems like a solid biz plan for adding urgent measurable value to your physical CDs.

]]> By: jamesolszewski ../#comment-232 Tue, 20 Oct 2009 19:46:34 +0000 ../../../../../?p=171#comment-232 I put out my first (and so far only) CD in mid-March 2008, through CD Baby. I did a short run of 100 CD-Rs through DiscMakers, and I couldn't have been happier with the experience. I gave away about 20 or so, and sold the rest. I have about 15 left, scattered around- 8 still at CD Baby, 3 in my studio, the rest in a shop in my home town being sold locally- sort of like consignment.

I broke even pretty quickly, the rest being gravy. The best thing about a company like DiscMakers, they retain all the “information”- the images, the song files, specs on the CDs, etc.- so if i ever need to do another run, I just let them know how many more I want and they spring into action (once the credit card clears)!

I'm working on my second CD now, and I'm struggling with whether to sell physical CDs. My particular demographic (thank you ReverbNation) for my solo work is women 40+, so I think maybe I will. But even so, I plan to offer hi-res downloads, along with PDFs of all the artwork, plus more, in the download package. This will justify my charging a little bit closer to the physical CD price for the download, and folks will still get the artwork. I'm thinking of throwing in images of my handwritten music in progress. That's the beauty of downloads- you can throw in extra stuff people will value with virtually no extra cost.

This next CD I'll probably print 200, or I may make the leap to 300, replicated, not duplicated.

(Sidebar: CD Duplication means CD-R. The company burns your CD as you would at home. CD Replication is “the real deal”. They make a glass master of your CD, and press the CDs physically, instead of using lasers and special ink to “burn” them. This is how CDs from your favorite artists are done. Easiest way to tell duplication from replication (from what I've found), duplicated CDs have that purplish or greenish tint to their undersides. Replicated CDs are silver. Replicated CDs tend to last longer (probably something to do with oxidation), and (sadly) some people consider duplicated CDs to be “amateur”.) Sorry for the tangent, figured I'd throw it out there.

]]> By: George Shaw ../#comment-246 Tue, 20 Oct 2009 10:37:36 +0000 ../../../../../?p=171#comment-246 Cool experiment, I've been running a similar experiment for the last 4 years, and I've barely broken even to cover my expenses. It's really hard to sell film music CD's especially if you're not a big name like John Williams or Danny Elfman, and don't have a publicist marketing your music.

I've self released 4 CD's via CD Baby, and had another released by moviescoremedia.com (which was awesome to not have to cover any costs for duplication or marketing). My first 2 CD's, I bought really nice printable CD-R's, and printed nice covers and basically created the CD's myself, and have sold 70 physical CD's to date between the two.

My 3rd CD, I decided I wanted to make it look nice, and ordered 200 copies via Oasis. It was ridiculously expensive, so I charged slightly higher for CD's. And I've only sold 11 in the past year.

My 4th CD, I decided I didn't want to spend at all on CD's, and made available only as a digital download. It's only been around for a few months, but it's about recouped it's costs (about $55 for CD Baby fees).

On top of selling CD's, about 50% of income from my sales is from digital downloads, mostly from iTunes (and most of that is from iTunes-Europe), but I'm generating income from various other places like Napster and CD Baby's own digital sales.

That said, through CD Baby, I probably make about $20-30 a month in CD Sales, and that's through a minimal amount of self marketing when I'm not busy composing. Though in the last year, I've had two opportunities to have my music performed in live concerts, and each time I was able to sell about 40 CD's, and making those live appearances has been much more effective than trying to sell my CD's online.
