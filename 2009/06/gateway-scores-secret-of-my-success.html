Gateway Scores: The Secret of My Success

by SCOREcast Team · No Comments · in Composition

When Jai asked me to talk about my Gateway Score — the score album that really grabbed me as a child and convinced me that scoring films is what I HAD to do for a living — I sort of froze at the thought of the assignment.

“A real composer would talk about John Williams, Jerry Goldsmith, or Bernard Herrmann,” I thought to myself. “When everyone hears my pick (insert Adam Sandler’s famous little-boy-sad-face here)… They’re all gonna laugh at me!“

While I love action films and I flip out for science fiction on a pretty regular basis, I have always been the drama guy. I love a great drama, especially one that contains strong writing for the characters coupled by great performances. “Jerry Maguire” might be my favorite film of all-time, simply because of Cameron Crowe‘s ability to create characters of incredible depth which allowed each actor to carve out a performance of incredible depth. The story device used in “Jerry Maguire” — rags-to-riches followed by life-lesson-learned — is not new by any stretch of the imagination, yet is presented in a fresh and unique way, almost in reverse. “Jerry Maguire” is fundamentally an old-fashioned dramedy… a little bit of comedy, a lot bit of drama.

For me, the dramedy was perfected in the mid-to-late eighties by a guy named John Hughes. Hughes somehow brought the irreverence of Reagan-generation punk attitude to his filmmaking, creating characters that I could completely identify with. “Pretty In Pink”, “The Breakfast Club“, and of course, “Ferris Bueller’s Day Off” immediately come to mind. But I didn’t become acutely aware of film music until my formative years teaching myself piano in the mid-to-late 1980′s. By then, everybody was trying to be John Hughes, and the dramedy was THE genre to make if you were a filmmaker. Needless to say, I was digging most of them.

I was also really taking notice and studying up on who was scoring all of them. Ira Newborn was huge back then, and the concept of the pop soundtrack album was really starting to take off. This all started to happen at about the same time a school buddy of mine who was also into film (future director Bryan Curb, who I would later work with here in Hollywood) gave me a cassette tape of David Foster’s “The Symphony Sessions”. That album contained a track called “Water Fountain”, which was the love theme from the 1987 Michael J. Fox dramedy “The Secret of My Success“. Foster was hired to write the score for the film after director Herbert Ross heard his music for the smash hit “St. Elmo’s Fire“.

David Foster’s “Water Fountain” theme really knocked me over, both on the soundtrack album to the film as well as the re-recording from “The Symphony Sessions”, which featured Foster on piano backed by the Jeremy Lubbock conducted Vancouver Symphony. Back when thematic writing was still a practice that involved notes rather than “patches”, Foster’s melody is a beautifully crafted representation of a major modal theme that is complex in its simplicity.

The rest of the score is motivic of both the “Water Fountain” theme and a secondary theme that Foster called the “Gazebo” theme, which is also just as lush and romantic as the former.

The fact the a lot of people don’t know that David Foster scored films as well as produced pop records in the 1980′s is dwarfed only by the number of people who would even be aware that “The Secret of My Success” had any kind of actual score in it to begin with. However, I’ve spent the majority of my career trying to write music that made me feel like I did when I first heard that “Water Fountain” theme.

Just when I think I’m getting close, I listen to it again and realize I have so far to go.
