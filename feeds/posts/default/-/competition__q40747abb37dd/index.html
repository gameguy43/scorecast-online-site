(Yes, there are standards and an expected high level of expertise). But, as mentioned here on SCOREcast several times, people skills and delivering as promised are always the bottom line. One finds that you will never stop learning and growing if you keep your attitude straight. As soon as you think you know a lot, it can be revealed that you may have a lot to learn. Watching and learning about ‘each person’s unique way’, can reap huge results in the long run.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThere are two kinds of ‘people managers’- one that feels the need to surround him or herself with people who are as good as or better than them, elevating EVERYONE; and others who need to be the “top dog”, surrounded by lesser talent, and thereby no threats. I have observed some of the most successful people in our business over the last 29 years, and the common thread has been--collaboration. When the horses are allowed to “run the race”, letting artists “do what they do”, projects seems to move along in a smoother, happier way. Yes, there has to be a “mommy or a daddy”, someone to make the ultimate decisions and wrangle the project responsibly. When done in the spirit of cooperation and respect, each project is the better for it. A healthy ego and confidence are so important, but they thrive in a collaborative environment.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eSo, how does this relate to competition? Well, one thing I am hoping for is the chance to reach out to the musical community, finding common ground, and see what we can all do to make our projects more successful. I think anyone reading this can relate an experience where they arrived at the studio, or the mixing stage with one perception of a game plan, only to find others have a completely different view. Game planning, pre planning, laying out ideas in advance, can only benefit the creative choices that are coming. In a perfect world, everyone on a project would know in advance where music will take the lead in a scene; where sound effects will be leading the way, or, where silence will tell the story. Many times, we arrive on the stage, only to find that everyone has done the same work to tell the story, and then, serious editing and/or compromises are made. Too late. I am an advocate of meeting with the music department and the director early in a project, so that I can be apprised of thoughts and concepts, and help the film makers realize their story. This does not happen often enough. This can even relate to the technical side with sample rates, bit rates, and track numbers as well. All this requires a bit of extra legwork, of course. Pre planning for post may seem like a bit too much work, too early on. However, it can also save a score by allowing it to be “heard” to its fullest (and I don’t mean volume). Coordinating with the other creatives on a project can bring continuity, and reduce surprises later. Of course, the creative process of mixing will affect all the elements brought to a stage, and new ideas can flourish in the right environment. But I would encourage all of us to think a bit about how we can share common goals, and rise above the “competition”.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eFor example: many composers have been burned by a mix of their score somewhere down the line. Music was buried, or pared down, or not woven cleanly around dialog and effects. It does help to have a musical mixing crew who understand the “language” of music. Many times, music is handled as “audio wallpaper”..the final touch to the soundscape. (ouch). As a result, a few composers and editors will only deliver a composite track, so that their “mix” can not be altered. There is value in understanding that we on the dubstage should first “do no harm”. We shouldn’t be automatically eq-ing, or rebalancing because we “can”. However, if the goal is the story, then planning splits and track assignments in advance can HELP music to be played, rather than just lowering or raising an overall mix. If a melodic line is playing during a dialog scene, control over pads, and bg’s while softening another orchestra section can actually help the thread of music throughout a film. I will save discussion on splits and tracks and mixing in surround for another column, but this idea of preplanning and collaboration is essential to smart film making. Especially in these days of shrinking budgets with less time allotted for mixing. Although it may seem like expanding track counts delivered to a stage could possibly create MORE work, it actually helps in the long run. A bit of preplanning can go a long way to making a polished product.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eMany times, it may not seem practical to work early-on with your post mixing crew. Like anything else in life, we are dealing with people. I think people LIKE to be included in the process. If we use the “KISS” rule (“Keep It Simple Stupid”), a tremendous amount of stress and concern can be relieved. Everyone wants the projects we work on to be the best they can be. Maybe adapting some simple rules of engagement can provide us all with smoother sailing and fewer surprises. Wouldn’t that be nice??? There have been countless times where we get surprised on the mixing stage unnecessarily. Valuable mixing time can be lost to simply setting up and patching! (Yes, we still have to “patch”).\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI personally would rather plan in advance as best we can, and hit the ground running on the first day of a mix. (especially with a room full of clients, editors and crew waiting to get going, at whatever rate they are paying) While we can not eliminate all surprises, and change is just about the only thing we can really count on (!), reducing these things only HELPS the creative process, and the hard work of dubbing.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIf we think of the mixing console as a musical instrument, and a mixer as a conductor (for the director), all these concepts apply. Decisions are constantly made; some planned, many spontaneous, but always with purpose. There is not a conductor out there who does not plan what he or she wants to create. They can't. If they did, they'd be out of business tomorrow. The pace of the major studio runs at least ten times faster than that of the independent filmmaker. Studios require certain things of their composers that the independent world couldn't care less about. Studio execs place expectations on their composers that independent producers frankly don't ever need to, nor want to, think about.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIf you really do desire to make this jump — out of the indie market and into a life of studio features — you'll first need to take the mental leaps necessary to start training yourself for a different kind of competition. \u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eAgain, I'm NOT putting down indie composers! The vast majority of my work is in the independent world. But when I am booked on the Lot of a major motion picture studio and am working on a studio project, my habits and my understanding of the game need to elevate to their level, or I'm in trouble.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eLet me run you through a handful of those \"understandings\", in an effort to outline the expectations you'll need to address before you can expect to be at all competitive in the studio realm.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eTHOUGHT 1: A SENSE OF URGENCY\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIn my dealings with major studios, certainly one of the most important things I've learned is that when the iron is hot, you HAVE to strike. You cannot wait, and you cannot hesitate. When you find out that there is a television show that is looking for a new musical solution, if you have an \"in\"... you need to get a package through the door, and you need to do it NOW. When you receive knowledge that the studio is unhappy with the way things are going musically with their current composer, and the rumblings of replacement have already leaked to you, it's time to act and act fast.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIn the independent world, you might have the luxury of a few days before a decision is made on who gets the job. Therefore, if you get the demo in today or tomorrow... it's not going to make that big a difference. However, on a studio project — particularly in TV — you might have a window of as little as a few hours to get something turned in, and if you are not prepared ahead of time to make that happen, you might as well forget it.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eHave your demos ready to rock at ALL times. If I called you right this second and told you I needed a demo sent over ASAP, would it take you a day to put a general reel together for me? You need to have general reels ready to go out the door right now, as well as reels containing specific genres of your material in case you get a request for a \"Dramatic Reel\" or an \"Action Reel\", and so forth. Not being caught with your pants down in regards to demo preparedness is sort of \"Studio Feature Film Composer 101\", and is a primary reason why people lose out on gigs at studios.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eYou also need to be able to take meetings when you are called to. Needing to rely on the bus schedule or the Metro to get you to Fox at 4pm on a Friday is going to be a problem if you are coming from the Valley. You need wheels, pure and simple. I recently heard of a guy who is a composer in his mid-30's and he still doesn't know how to drive a car. To me, that's ridiculous. How could you even \u003cu\u003e\u003ci\u003ethink\u003c/i\u003e\u003c/u\u003e you can compete if you cannot even drive yourself to meet with a table of people who want to hand you a job?\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eTHOUGHT 2: CLEAR AND FREQUENT COMMUNICATION\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIn the independent world, you might not hear from your director or producers for a few days/weeks, as they are most likely wearing several hats: director, picture editor, post-production supervisor, color corrector, sound mixer, title designer... you name it, they're doing it. By contrast, in the studio world, everyone has a job (usually protected by a union), and everyone does that job, and THAT job only. Therefore, your principals are not running like crazy people as much as they would be if they were handling every minor detail personally. They have a lot more time on their hands to \"manage by walking around\" then they would if they were producing an independent feature.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eTake it upon yourself to lead the way in making calls to check in as a team. Make sure that your principals don't have to ask you twice if you \"spoke with the editor about that change in the picture\" or whether or not you uploaded the file you promised to the night before. Don't make them repeat themselves. Make promises sparingly, but keep them faithfully. If you cannot pull something off in the time you know you have to do it, don't commit to doing it during that time frame. But no matter what, pick up the phone and communicate.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eAnd another thing: Stop \"texting\". There is nothing more unprofessional than receiving a text message from someone that contains information that would be better communicated in person or voice to voice over the telephone. \"I'm five minutes away,\" is a text. \"I'm going to run 5 grand over budget,\" is a phone call. :-)\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eHere is my personal code of conduct when it comes to communicating with higher-ups in the studio realm: \u003ci\u003e\u003cb\u003eLess is more, accuracy is imperative, and person to person is always better.\u003c/b\u003e\u003c/i\u003e That last part is important for several reasons. First, it gives you the opportunity to either be heard audibly or seen physically — which is always a good thing. Secondly, texts, emails, and voicemails get buried. Voice to voice contact or face to face meetings (if it is time-appropriate) make YOU a priority — you aren't on some list somewhere... you are standing there fully engaged with the person. Way better.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eTHOUGHT 3: PROBLEM-FREE PARTICIPATION\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eStudio executives are some of the busiest people I've met in this industry. There is a misconception out there that if you work at a studio, you spend the majority of your time sitting around and watching the clouds roll by.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eNot true.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI've also heard frustrated composers say things like, \"I could do that job — be a studio executive.\" Umm... no, you couldn't. You don't have it in you. The fact that you are saying that is only the tip of the iceberg as to why you'd never have the chutzpah to work on the Lot. Trust me.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI've become close to several studio people in the years I've been doing this and let me tell you… there's no group of people who work harder under more pressure. The Studio Lot is a place of ridiculous intensity where ungodly expectations are being put on people day in and day out. I'm sure that you have at one point or another experienced the pressure of working for an employer who is concerned for his or her \"bottom-line\". Studios are no different, except that when the bottom-line isn't met every quarter, heads will literally roll. It is a pressure-cooker atmosphere that chews up and spits out the very best people in Hollywood on a fairly regular basis. \u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIt is because of this already intense pressure to achieve the numbers that you, as a third-party freelance contractor, should never present a problem without a solution. Studio people simply do not have the time for extra problems, let alone ones that YOU are creating. You want to get in with the people who are the movers and shakers of the industry? Be a Solution. You've heard it said before that there really is no such thing as \"writer's block\". I mean, there IS... everyone gets stumped every once in a while, yes. But there truly is, however, no such thing as the \u003ci\u003eexcuse\u003c/i\u003e of it. You simply cannot show up to an approval session with no cues in hand and blame it on the fact that you couldn't think of anything to write that day, right?\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eWell, being a problematic composer on a studio film is the exact same thing. If you are a pain in the ass, you'll literally \u003ci\u003enever work with them again\u003c/i\u003e. That phrase is not just a cliche. I see it all the time.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThe opposite approach is best. I remember the first opportunity I had to provide additional music for Disney on a project. At the orchestral sessions, on a break, I overheard one of the executives from the music department asking the music editor if I was \"playing ball\" with them; meaning, was I being cool, getting my work done, and not causing headaches. \u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThe editor answered with a smile, \"Oh yeah! He's done everything we've needed him to do, without fail. He's great!\" \u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\"Good,\" said the executive. \"I like when it's easy.\"\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eHearing that from someone I respect like that studio executive made my decade. To know that I was being regarded as a team player and not someone who was creating drama or friction on what had already a bit of a roller-coaster project for them made me feel like the king of the world. The sessions were incredible, my music sounds great in the film, and both Disney and the director were happy with the job I did. Big BIG win.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eAnd that's how you want to be. You want to be the person that comes in, does the job, encourages others to be their best, and pushes the production along as best you can with no controversy, drama, politics, or bullshit. No grandstanding, no ego, no crap — just pure, raw, energy that is excited to be there and thankful for the opportunity to prove that you've got the goods.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003ci\u003eConfident humility\u003c/i\u003e. That's what it's all about.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eTHOUGHT 4: LET THE STUDIO SYSTEM WORK\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eMaking the leap from independent films to studio features takes some time. Just as you didn't work your way from $5k indie features to million dollar indie features overnight, you're also not going to be handed a theatrically-released studio feature to score right out of the gate. It's nothing personal, you just aren't worth the risk yet. You are still an unproven entity to any major studio. With hard work, perseverance, and a small amount of magic, you'll get your chance, but you have to let the studio system do its work for you. It will, if you let it. And here's a tip: The sooner you start thinking of the studios as potential \"partners\" on gigs and not as potential \"suppliers\" of gigs, the quicker the stars will align for you.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eBe patient... and more importantly, be respectful. If you are lucky enough to get in the door so far as to be told \"we'll let you know\", do not call the next day if you still don't \"know\". If you do things like this, you are already considered a problem.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI know a guy who called the studio that had already informed him that he didn't get a gig for weeks after, with the intention of \"keeping his name in circulation\". I've heard people refer to these types of folks as \"tail waggers\". The moniker sounds silly, but it is accurate. Susie and I had a dog once and this is how he acted when he wanted \"treats\". If you come across that way as a composer, imagine how a studio executive would view you as a potential business partner. Studio: 1. You: 0.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eBeing persistent has its advantages, but being a pain in someone's ass never does. It takes a lot to run a major studio, and the film you are gunning for is one of hundreds in production. Just because people aren't falling all over themselves to return your phone calls doesn't mean they aren't working to get you on the gig. Let them do their job and be patient.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eCONCLUSION\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThese are only a few thoughts I've had this month as we've beat this topic senseless around here.
