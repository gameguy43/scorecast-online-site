]]> By: Jim Well ../#comment-273 Sun, 19 Jul 2009 22:16:32 +0000 ../../../../../?p=82#comment-273 William –

I don't have an expert answer to this question yet. However, here's what I think:

(1) As I've maintained consistently, using breath control (or, at least, something that you can vary continuously) is very important if you want to play expressively. You can set a WIVI flute to give you faster attack on every note, but then you're stuck with that articulation and have discarded an important means of expression.

If you're playing live or entering a musical line into your DAW by playing in real time, breath control is especially great as the principal thing you vary, because it's feasible to vary it with each note — e.g., to get a sharp attack on one note and a gradual attack on the next. In theory, you could do that with, say, the mod wheel, but you'd have to be awfully fast and accurate to get close to what you can do with breath.

(The above breath control advantage, of course, is in addition to the fact that breath control varies both the amplitude and the timbre of the currently-sounding note — at any time.)

OK, now I'll stop beating incessantly on that point!

(2) Next, let's assume you're playing the WIVI from your keyboard, are not using a breath controller, and just want to set your WIVI so that all notes have a faster (but not necessarily equal) attack.

First, remember to set Breath CC to VEL in the General Settings. That way, at least you have some expressivity control, because in this mode, various properties of notes and note transitions will vary as a function of note velocity (key velocity). In addition, if you have a keyboard that generates aftertouch messages according to how much pressure you exert on the keys after pressing them, you can use this as a very easy way to control vibrato depth.

Now, given the above setting, attack will get faster as key velocity increases. However, there will be some maximum attack speed (minimum attack time). If that maximum attack speed isn't fast enough for you, you can change it.

To increase the maximum attack speed (or, equivalently, decrease the minimum attack time), select the Control parameters panel by clicking on the rightmost button just below the place where you select instruments. Then use your mouse to grab the control panel and scroll it down until you see the area with the heading “BREATH ENVELOPE”. In this area, you'll find a set of parameters that affect things WIVI will do automatically to the breath signal as you play. (I.e., for each note, the WIVI generates for you a puff of breath having one shape or another, given that you're not using a breath controller to create this puff yourself — and the parameters let you influence the shape that the WIVI gives to that puff.) As you can see, there are several things you could modify to influence the maximum attack speed. For example, if you increase the value of the “Key attack spd.” parameter, you'll be able to get faster attacks on your flute notes. You can set the value very high and get very fast attacks — but then, of course, you'll get a sound that's different from a real flute. If that's what you want, great. If not, be more moderate in your setting.

I'd suggest experimenting around with the various parameters in this area, to see what they do. I'm by no means an expert on all of them.

TIP: If you haven't discovered this yet, the WIVI interface has built-in documentation in the form of “tool tips”. For example, if you want to see the documentation for the “Key attack spd.” parameter, double-click in the field where you set the value for this parameter, and the documentation will appear as a “tool tip”. Click once on the tooltip window to make it go away.

(3) One problem with many of the parameters in the BREATH ENVELOPE area (specifically, from “Breath speed” down to “Key release spd.”) is that you can't assign a MIDI CC to them. If you could, you'd be able to vary their values in real time as you play. But you can't.

<>However, though it may not be obvious, MIDI CCs can be assigned to another group of these parameters. Just above the “Breath speed” parameter you'll see a window that graphically depicts the shape of the breath envelope, and above that, a field where you select the name of a parameter that you want to adjust. If you're coming here for the first time, this field will say “Attack time”. You set its value via the three controls just below the breath envelope graphic display. Now, click once on the arrow button to the right of the parameter name field. If the field initially said “Attack time”, it will now say “Attack time CC”. I.e., this is how you can assign a continuous controller (CC) to the Attack time parameter. If you haven't assigned a CC before, I think this CC will be set to VEL — meaning that attack time is controlled by key velocity. Thus if you want to direct control over Attack time rather than live with the automatic linkage of Attack time to velocity, assign a CC and control it yourself.

(4) I suspect you might also be able to change the note articulations of your WIVI flute to be more to your liking by adjusting the controls in the TRANSITIONS, PORTAMENTO, and LEGATO areas, which are further down in the Control parameters panel. These controls also have the advantage that you can assign MIDI CCs to them. For example, when working on a musical line in my DAW, I find it can be very helpful to set the “Transition CC” parameter to assign a CC for note transition control. Then, if something doesn't sound right, I have a control I can adjust (by hand-editing the control contour in my DAW) — and voila!

Well — enough for now. As you can see, there's a whole world to explore here. Imagine being an expert on all of this, and developing the chops to work all of these controls as fast as you can think!

]]> By: Jim Well ../#comment-288 Sat, 18 Jul 2009 01:41:46 +0000 ../../../../../?p=82#comment-288 Deane asks, “Is it starting to not matter anyway what a *real* orchestra sounds like since people don't really know to begin with?”

I think the answer is a little tricky. My first inclination is to answer that it's mattering less than it used to. But from the point of view of a composer who both uses MIDI mockups and writes for traditional orchestra with the intention of recording the real orchestra, I think it matters a lot.

If you work with MIDI mockups all the time, and don't get re-calibrated by listening to real orchestras, you start thinking as if the MIDI sound is a reliable reading on what the real orchestra will sound like when you record it playing your score. Then, when you actually record the orchestra, you find out that the balance of the real orchestra (and maybe even the sounds of the individual instruments) is different. If the balance is out of whack, the timbres and textures will be out of whack as a result. What worked in MIDI might not work with the real orchestra. Scary prospect.

It seems the antidote is that a composer needs to make it a priority to stay calibrated on the sounds of real orchestras. Maybe even on the sound of a particular real orchestra, playing on a particular scoring stage.

]]> By: Jim Well ../#comment-283 Sat, 18 Jul 2009 00:46:22 +0000 ../../../../../?p=82#comment-283 Following up on a few things …

(1) In my exchange with William Arnold, I had said I thought recalled that WIVIs defaulted to using velocity as means of controlling playing amplitude. William said he thought they defaulted to CC2 — breath control. William was correct.

By the way, we're talking here about a WIVI parameter called “Breath CC”. You find it in the “General Settings” group of WIVI controls, and it defaults to CC2. You can set it to any continuous controller (CC), and you can also set it to VEL (velocity). If you set it to a CC, WIVI note amplitude and timbre respond to changes in the CC value at any time. If you set it to VEL, the amplitude and timbre of notes you play will respond to key velocity instead — but, true to the concept of key velocity, the amplitude and timbre are set at the beginning of the note and don't vary thereafter, so you lose an important component of expression.

Now, if you don't know about the WIVI's “Breath CC” parameter or don't change its setting from the default, the WIVI will be expecting you to vary CC2 as you play.

This is actually a very significant issue for someone who is trying out WIVI for the first time! If you don't realize what's going on, the CC2 default will likely lead to a poor first impression of WIVIs. Let me explain why.

CC2 is the default because WIVIs are intended to be used with breath control. If you're playing “live”, it's best to be using an actual breath controller, like a Yamaha BC3. (If you're not playing live, you can edit breath control contours in your DAW.) But many people don't use breath controllers, and do most of their composing work with just a keyboard — the norm when working with sample libraries.

The question is: How does a WIVI behave if you hook a keyboard up to it, don't have a breath controller, have left “Breath CC” set to CC2, and just start playing?

The answer is: You'll have no dynamics! You touch your keyboard lightly, and you get a pretty hefty sound with more or less constant amplitude and pitch. You bang hard on your keys, and you get the same thing! That's because unless “Breath CC” is set to VEL, the WIVI pays no attention to key velocity! Beware!

Deane, this might have happened when you did your initial WIVI evaluation. If so, it would have caused you to think WIVIs were pretty crude.

Bottom Line: If you're playing a WIVI “live”, with just a keyboard, set “Breath CC” to VEL. This setting will give you some control over note dynamics. You won't have the fantastic expressivity you can get with a breath controller — but what you get is much better much better than no control.

(2) I mentioned in the article that the WIVI environment provides built-in reverb which is highly controllable. I won't go into all the details here. But I would like to point out that, when using an ensemble of WIVIs to play orchestral material, I found that the built-in “Symphony Hall” reverb can muddy the sound more than I was comfortable with — at least when left at its default settings. For various reasons, I opted to disable the built-in reverb and use a combination of WaveArts MasterVerb and Voxengo Pristine Space reverbs. This gives me a cleaner, more defined sound and lets me use the same reverb for non-WIVI parts of my orchestra. (It's likely that by tweaking the WIVI reverb parameters I could also have improved the result, but this is something I haven't fully explored yet.)

Bottom Line: If you're just doing a casual demo, the default built-in reverb will come into play. So I'd suggest being wary of what this reverb might do to the instrument sound during your evaluation.

(3) You might not notice that, by default, WIVI vibrato depth is controlled by MIDI aftertouch messages. Thus, if you have a keyboard that supports aftertouch, then when playing “live”, you can add vibrato simply by pressing harder on your keys.

And by the way, if you don't like the default sound, you can adjust various parameters to change it.

(4) Another detail that will almost certainly escape notice during an initial evaluation is the control WIVIs offer over note transitions — i.e., more or less legato, portamento, etc. This is another area where, if you're doing a quick initial evaluation and don't know how to control note transitions or even that they can be controlled, you might hear things that don't meet your expectations and conclude that WIVIs don't sound very good.

In practice, I've been finding that, for my taste, exerting control over note transitions is quite important. At the moment, however, I'm using far less of the note transition control capabilities than are available. I simply assign the “Transition CC” parameter to be controlled by a MIDI continuous controller of my choice, and then set a CC value according to my taste in any given musical passage. (I'm generally doing this in my DAW.) If you don't alter the “Transition CC” parameter, your WIVI defaults to making transition speeds respond to key velocity. My experience suggests that, for me, anyway, that's not such a good choice.

The relevant controls are found at the bottom of the “Control Parameters” section of the WIVI controls.

(5) Deane's most recent comment (above) includes specific mention of sample libraries he likes to use, and why he likes them. I virtually worship Deane, but in trying to accomplish what I've been interested in doing in recent months, I came to more or less the opposite conclusions. It was disappointment with various aspects of those libraries that drove me to explore WIVI. I don't yet know where I'll end up.

Finally … Thanks to everyone for the time taken to participate in this discussion! As someone else (probably Deane) has said, the real action on SCOREcast Online is in the comments — i.e., you!.

So, keep the comments coming — not only on this article, but on all of them. Let's try to get this thing right!

When you use breath control, the amplitude and timbre change continuously with the controller value, rather than only at the note boundaries.
