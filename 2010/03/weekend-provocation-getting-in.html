Lee Sanders: Getting In

March 12, 2010 Written by Lee Sanders Process

For the next few weeks I’m going to use this column to dig into the three primary technical aspects of spotting: entrances, exits and function. There are three Fridays left in the month, so I suppose it’s good I thought of doing this now! First up: getting in.

Let’s start with when to enter a scene. And right up front, a disclaimer: none of this is comprehensive. I sort of think that it couldn’t be. Each film is a different animal, calling for a different set of tactics. And for every rule there’s a brilliant exception. There are, however, some things that I think you ought to consider pretty much every time out.

One of the most important ones, to me, is that choosing your entrance point is a decision that should always be taken with the entire score in mind. Consider the context, in other words.

This is one reason you must prep for a spotting session: you want a consistent approach. Entering a scene either relatively early, or noticeably later than you might, can be a valid choice, but it’s a decision that should flow from your overarching plan for the score as a whole.

If you go in without a master plan, just taking it as it comes, you’re likely to make decisions on a moment-to-moment basis. Those decisions will probably be inconsistent with each other, which just about guarantees to destroy any underlying sense of unity the finished product might possess. You’re sinking fast, and you haven’t even written the first note.

Here’s an example: I was spotting a project recently, and after a couple of viewings I decided on an overall approach: I wanted to come in as late as possible with each cue. A really minimal approach, in other words.

Here’s why: The performances were there on screen, and there was going to be a ton of sound design to contend with anyway… plus, I already knew that for many cues the music was going to have an austere character. The sparse spotting played into that. And waiting… for… it… also plays with the audience, which I wanted to do. They’re smart: they know when that music’s supposed to be coming in. To deny them what they’re listening for creates a subtle atmosphere of uncertainty. Another bonus for the kind of show this was.

At the end of Act 3, there was a tense scene between two characters (a single mother and her teenage son). It might have been an obvious decision to pour some music on that scene to ‘punch up’ the conflict in their conversation (and in their deteriorating relationship).

You know what? That probably would have worked fine. The obvious choice isn’t always the wrong choice. It’s obvious for a reason, after all.

But as I watched the scene prior to spotting, I came to the opinion that the acting was so strong that I preferred not to score the scene at all. If I’d been laying in the cues early and often beforehand, the lack of music would be too much for this final scene of the act… it would have felt too empty. As it was, I knew it would work because I’d already pre-conditioned the listener to expect a sparse score.

And so it all worked together. In my opinion, it actually worked really well. You could cut the tension of that final Act 3 scene with a knife—and a big reason why is that there was no sappy score behind the stinging words of mother and son. Nothing to mediate, or soften, that experience.

(Incidentally, “act-outs” are one of the more delicate moments for television—producers often want to REALLY! PUNCTUATE! that final moment of the act. Negative accents of the kind I wrote for this project are often a tough sell… and a producer who isn’t afraid of them is a valuable friend indeed. I’ll have more to say on this next week, as we discuss getting out, but just keep it in mind for now).

Now, a few thoughts on how to get in.

If you listen to a score album—almost any score album—you’ll hear a lot of cues that begin with a string line fading up. And you know the reason why: it makes for a smooth, almost imperceptible entrance. That’s often the goal. Often, but not always. Cues that enter with a bang can be fun. Our job, of course, is to match the intensity and the brashness with which the music announces itself to the emotional and narrative content of the scene. And a big part of the perceived intensity and brashness is choosing precisely, to the frame, the perfect moment to break the silence.

If you need to enter unobtrusively, it’s easy to sneak in under a sound effect, or a line of dialog (although the music is often best when the entrance reacts to a significant line, after a dramatic beat for the audience to process what they’ve just heard). Which line of dialog is a subjective decision: do you want to enter after the game-changing line of dialog? Or on the reaction shot? Do you want to precede (i.e., foreshadow) the consequential line? That last one is usually not a popular choice… it’s a bit like announcing the punch line before the comedian has finished the joke.

Another aesthetic choice is to match entrance to action. If characters are getting ready to do something, then they actually start doing it, you might justify the entrance of the score that way. Yet another choice is to allow the music to reflect a changed psychological state—here the music should, understandably, be less visceral… more removed.

Alan Silvestri tells the story of spotting Cast Away: He and Robert Zemeckis sat in a room in silence as the first 15 minutes of picture went by. Zemeckis asked him: “You not hearing anything yet?” Silvestri replied, “Not yet, no.” Another 10 minutes of picture passed. Zemeckis: “Nothing?” Silvestri: “Sorry, Bob… nothing yet.” 10 more minutes. “Still nothing?” “Nope.”

This went on for a long, long while. And it was at a crucial psychological and dramatic moment—Tom Hanks’s character looking back at the island that he’s now managed to escape—that we finally, finally hear the first strains of the score. It’s a bold choice, and as entrances go, an incredibly moving one.

Next, let’s talk for a moment about cuts. Entering precisely on a cut makes that cut structural—it makes the audience notice it. That can be a good thing, or a very, very bad thing. The good news here is that it’s generally pretty obvious if a cut is intended to be a CUT, or to disappear seamlessly into the flow of the continuity editing. So it’s pretty easy not to screw this one up, if you’re taking anything like a reasonable amount of care with your entrance.

I hope this is enough to get the conversation flowing… spotting is such a personal, subjective and important part of the entire process that I would love to hear your own approaches. What do you find yourself doing? What has worked for you in the past? What hasn’t? Hit the COMMENTS and let’s talk some more.

  • lhsanders

    Yup, on what you're saying about the spotting session not being the first meeting, let me add/amplify by saying: Generally one or two meetings take place with the filmmakers before you're officially brought on board the project (espec. if it's a new relationship). Might even be more, depending.
    These are broad-strokes sort of meetings, creatively speaking, and they often take place a little before your deal gets done (if you have representation; if not, you'll still probably talk a bit about the picture before you get down to the brass tacks of making a deal). Everyone involved is sort of feeling each other out to see if they're even in the same zip code as far as their ideas for the project at hand are concerned.

    So these meetings are all within the context of you and the filmmakers having a *prospective* relationship with regards to the project. The spotting session is typically the first time after the deal gets done where you're looking at this thing as the composer who WILL BE doing the show (at least, unless your score gets dumped!).

  • randythom

    Hi, All!

    I just discovered this group, and it's a goldmine.
    Some of you may know I'm a sound designer. I'm obsessed with finding ways for real collaboration to happen between sound designers and composers. My own bias is that decisions need to be made early in each project about which sequences are going to be music driven, with sound effects as a supporting character; and which scenes will be sound effects driven, with music as a supporting character. In my opinion it's almost always disastrous for both departments to attempt to cover the same ground at the same intensity, yet that is exactly what many directors want us to do.

    I sound designed Cast Away, and all of Bob's movies since Gump. Zemeckis is very much a believer in passing the baton, so to speak, from one department to another over the course of a film, rather than setting the stage for a gang fight between dialog, music, and effects like so many other directors seem to relish. Alan Sylvestri and I appreciate Bob's approach.

    A joint spotting session, involving effects and music, can sometimes help in this regard.

    Randy

  • randythom

    Thanks, Dean!

    I'm sure you know that there are almost always spotting sessions for sound design and dialog too. In the dialog spotting session they talk about questions like what needs to be replaced by ADR, what needs to be handled in loop group, etc. The sound design spotting session is very similar to a music spotting session in the sense that the questions that arise are similar… where sounds should begin and end, what dramatic and emotional character should they have, etc.

    Unfortunately, when a director does three separate spotting sessions his/her tendency will often be to imagine that it is 100% the job of that discipline to sell each scene. So, for example, in the dialog spotting session a director will often be tempted to call for lots of off-screen dialog, add grunts groans and breaths for principal characters, and so on, forgetting temporarily that music and sound effects will be there to help tell the story. Likewise, sound effects tend to proliferate during the sound design spotting session, and music during the music session. The director often as a vague notion that it can all be sorted out in the final mix. You and I know that “sorting out” is hard as hell, and almost never winds up being satisfactory to anyone, including the director.

    I wouldn't propose that all spotting sessions try to focus on all three disciplines, but I do think that on many projects a session attended by composer, sound designer, and dialog supervisor can be extremely helpful in avoiding the “wall of incoherent noise” tendency in many final mixes.

    Randy
