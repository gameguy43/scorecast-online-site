Jim Well: The Journey

by SCOREcast Team · View Comments
Share

This monthly column, Inside The Outside, takes you inside one man’s ongoing journey to a composing career. I’ll be thinking out loud about challenges as they hit. I can’t say for sure I’ll arrive intact. But if you’re a fellow traveler, we’ll confront the same demons. May The Farce be with us.

“Who is this Jim Well guy?” you wonder.

I didn’t start out to be a composer. As a kid, I longed to be a music guy. Dad said, “Son, be an engineer instead. Do music on the side.” Taking his advice, I went to the Dark Side, while Muse took a nap. Can’t say I regret my eons as an engineer. It put serious bread on the table, rooted me in Northern California, gave me options. I even liked it.

But the life I didn’t have wouldn’t die. I had to get music back. So I got myself a keyboard. A wind controller. Put up some music apps. Started arranging, composing, sequencing, performing. Found a ready audience near home. Saw I was still the little engine that could. Only, it was just a hobby – not enough. Sound familiar?

I cut the corporate cord in 2002. Thought about Muse, but couldn’t find the road. With other unmet needs in control, I zigged and zagged. Tried being a nurse. No. Software entrepreneur. No. Well, yes — I did want to be a businessman. But Muse still hunted me.


Then, in 2007, three forces converged: (1) I realized I had overlooked composing as a way in; (2) virtual instrument technology had come way farther than I ever thought; (3) I came to despise the Almighty Car, and looked for a way to work without one, staying put.

The … car?

Yeah. When these three forces fermented with my entrepreneurial lust, it emerged that I would build a car-free, home-based, independent business around composing for media, right here in Nor Cal, leveraging the Web. That has became my manifesto. Maybe not so different from yours?


“Not so fast,” a voice says. “That’s not how the industry works. There’s a long-established culture, a way of working. Based on proximity, centered in LA and New York. Besides, you gotta have chops. And if you wanna get into this business, you gotta come up the ladder like everybody else.”

You have just met Demon #1. Do pay attention …

I haven’t got all the answers, but I’m not moving from Nor Cal just yet. One day, though, you might see me apprenticed to an established composer, if I get the needed chops first. No chops, no work.

My first response to Demon #1 was like any chicken’s: I looked to my strengths. I knew I had to get to the School of Chops, but for me, getting couldn’t mean going. I thought, what I have is some cash, a technical background, and my so far hobby. So I took what I knew and focused on creating a first-class project studio. With two goals: become my classroom, and be industry-ready. Now I’ve built it … and I’ve come.

I’m sitting pretty and along comes Demon #2. “You’re still just playing around,” it says. “Toys do not a composer make.”

Holy cow. You have actually be able to do something that somebody needs. Like, write really good music. On a moment’s notice. And, you have to be able to show that you can. So now, it’s all about the School of Chops – and that demo reel. Write, write, write.


Demon #3: “Too much to learn. Without a mentor, you’ll tear yourself to bits!”

Some truth here. Hafta sharpen my focus. What I most want to do is write for full orchestra. Make goose bumps. The way I feel when I hear a good film score. The closer I come to being “classically trained”, the better – as long as I can do it on my own, in the School of Chops. Add that to the manifesto, please.

Why classical? Listen to the Wagners, Stravinskys, Ravels, Coplands … doggoned if they don’t sound like the scores I wanna write. These guys were masters of how to get that sound, wring that emotion from the tools of the classical orchestra. They left lots of scores to study. And there’s no shortage of good books to guide the way. Even some great online courses, like Peter Alexander’s Professional Orchestration. It boils down to discipline.


Demon #4 can’t stop laughing. “Even if you write it, who’s gonna play it?”

I’m from the Dark Side, I say. You can’t stop me. But yes, good point. First of all, MIDI will play a lot of what I write. When that’s not good enough, the next step is that some parts will be played by real musicians. From where, you ask? Look, I know LA probably has the best people to do this. But I live in the Bay Area, near San Jose. We have a lot of talent here, too. I’ll draw on local resources whenever I can. Why couldn’t we do here – on a smaller scale – what LA’s done? Time for some fresh thinking.

Not to mention, I’ve discovered SCOREcast. Much more next month …
