Caveats of Convenience: Pt. 1

By Deane Ogden  /   August 10, 2010  /   13 Comments

Things film composers are doing wrong lately, and how to avoid developing the same habits.

I’m going to stray from the recent focus on “Working as a Team” and devote a few posts to some of the things I’ve observed film composers doing wrong lately. Of course, that statement in and of itself is highly subjective, but I think there are some habits that are starting to trend that, while possibly not evident right away, will prove down the line to be detrimental to doing business as a film composer in our continually evolving market.

I’m not talking about poor business acumen: not returning phone calls, showing up late, under-delivering or running over-budget. To be honest, if you are doing any of those things you are already dead in the water! Instead, what I’ll be referring to are things that July’s theme of “Marketing and Branding” got me thinking about, and I’ll split them up into a series of posts I’m going to call “Caveats of Convenient Film Composing”. I see this list of choices as “temptations of convenience”. This series might also be called “The Seduction of The Easy Way Out”. In this economy and composing climate, I see a lot of composers taking these routes. While none of them are necessarily “bad”, I’ll try to make the case as to why I believe most of them should be avoided.

Caveat #1: Catering To The Crowd

We’ve said this before at SCOREcast (and I’m sure you’ve it heard elsewhere, too): you aren’t going to get rich being a film composer. Moreover, you certainly aren’t going to get “noticed” or get famous being one… save for the film music lovers in the world. And there are enough of them to make you think you might!

In fact, I am hearing more film scores come out that sound like they were written with film music fans in mind from the start. I’m not talking about CD soundtrack albums—those *should* be sequences for a good listen. I’m referring to the actual music in the film. As film music becomes more and more popular (the result of increased academic attention on film scoring combined with the glut of composers trying to fight their way onto the playing field) we are seeing the value of film music being placed more on the “soundtrack release”, and the hopeful (if not fruitless) rewards reaped thereof rather than the accomplishment of a score being perfectly tailored to the action onscreen.

Likewise, social media has introduced new frontiers never before discovered in how film music people are connected around the world. New film music festivals such as the incredible Ubeda International Music Festival, SXSW, and even the Soundtrack Film Music Festival raise awareness of our craft in a way that was never really possible before the level of connection that social media tools like Facebook, Twitter, LinkedIn, and MySpace provide. However, that connection of convenience comes with a small price tag: composers writing for the “admission” into those events instead of writing the best score they can for the film. I was asked to be a guest adjudicator at a festival last year where one of the awards given out was a “Hardest Working Composer” award of sorts, and the criteria by which it was awarded was based on how many scores that composer had produced in a single year. I didn’t vote for the person who ultimately won the award, and I won’t mention how utterly wrong many of this person’s scores were for the films in which they “accompanied”. The award was obviously given for sheer “output” rather than any strict adherence to quality, function, or craft standards.

As a result, we might be hearing more and more music that sounds unoriginal and unfinished. John Debney recently invited some lively discussion by posing the question, “Why do so many scores sound absolutely the same these days?” Of the 128 respondents, most blamed the studios/producers for the lack of originality in film scoring today. I believe, however, that the studios want just as much to hear something creatively original as those of us doing the composing long to bring it to them. These days, composers are considering more than simply servicing the film when they write their scores, and it might be showing.

Danny Elfman has been criticized over the years of turning out scores that do not necessarily provide for a great listening experience independent of the film they were scored for. To that I say, “Perfect!” Do you think Mr. Elfman gives a hoot about whether or not his music works outside of the picture? I assure you, he doesn’t. And you shouldn’t either. No accolades, awards, reviews, or journalistic opinions could ever substitute for a well-written score tailor-made for the story by which it intends to serve.  To be a “good” composer (I’m not even talking great here… just good) you must come to the realization that you are there to do one single thing: service the film. That’s it. If you are aiming for any more than that in your duties then you are not fulfilling your duties at all. In fact, you are wasting your client’s, the studio’s, and your team’s valuable time. You are hunting for something more than what the job entails… you are hunting for a trophy. At that point, not only are you out of the flow of creativity, but you are also self-servingly seeking to gain something without truly putting your heart and soul into the work required to attain it.

My challenge? THROW yourself into your next project with reckless abandon. Don’t settle. Write music that you never knew was in you. Do not take your job lightly. Do not cut corners or take shortcuts. Do not rely on old, tired, and familiar devices, progressions, melodies, or motifs. In short, don’t make it easy on yourself. Challenge yourself to write bigger, better, and smarter. Orchestrate from a passionate place, not from an educational one. Voice your music with a new clarity and not with the palette you voiced from the last time or two. Do something different. If your principals are not sold on it, get off your ass and sell them on it! Convince them that this is the way to go if you know in your heart that it is what is best for the film. Instead of writing like it is the first score you’ll ever be recognized for, write as if it is the last score you’ll ever write. Everything else will take care of itself.

Thoughts? Hit me up in the COMMENTS!

  • http://profiles.google.com/noisyblocks Emlyn A

    For starters, might I suggest that more composers simply abandon the orchestral format? [Hushed silence, followed by loud booing.]

    All done? hehe Hear me out:

    I adore the texture of Baroque music; Purcell’s “When I Am Laid In Earth” passacaglia makes me tear up, I’m emboldened by “Bolero” (!), Reich’s “The Desert Music” is beautiful, and there’s a special place in my brain for the works of Satie, Bartok and Glass.

    But the medium is tired, tired, tired. (I feel the same way about the 4-piece rock band…and just about every “acoustic” instrument.) What once passed as the only reliable means of creating and reproducing “new sound”–carefully written orchestral scores–and of experimenting and formulating new timbral qualities and textural combinations has been gently and progressively eroded by the reality that computer technology brings to music-makers. (And I don’t speak to the “realism” of computer-generated music or of its performance; these are irrelevant in context of the art of devising and controlling sounds.)

    I don’t ever expect the orchestra to be replaced wholesale, but to deny the world of spectral possibilities–and raw beauty–that computers afford the composer is to bury one’s head in the sand. Oh my hard-won education! they cry. Oh my beloved violas! they wail. Well, I have an education in music theory & composition too, and violas make me positively swoon, but I have come to see computer-enabled sound crafting as just a natural extension of a very, very long history of human-created musical devices. For ultimately it will come down to what the music says, and not how it’s said; the emotive content will never stray far, but from Von Bingen to Aphex Twin we do so love to fiddle with the way it’s delivered.

    Yes, there’s a learning curve with computer instrument- and music-making; it’s often a steep curve, fraught with difficulties, challenges, arcana and sublime revelations.

      Hi Emlyn
      DIY (Do It Yourself) is definitely today’s trend – in many things. There are DIY shows all over television so why not DIY film music?
      DIY is usually attempted for one of two reasons: saving money and/or ego gratification – a feeling of accomplishment.   Most DIYers would agree,  however, that if it’s really important to have a professional job done, hire the professionals.

      Studio musicians, music editors and scoring mixers are the professionals.  Most composers, including myself are amateurs at everything other than composing and perhaps orchestration.  I don’t hire out as a mixer or (God forbid) a player on anyone else’s session.

      The orchestra does not symbolize a type of bygone music but rather a commitment to a professional job with a serious budget appropriate  for the level of work a professional film composer does.

      Electronic scores are appropriate for many films but not all.  And just as you wouldn’t use an orchestra to emulate electronics, using electronics to emulate an orchestra is a DIY solution.  It makes you the best cellist, French Horn player and harpist on the session.  Is that an “A” list orchestra?

    Perhaps an analogy would be a costumer working on a film and simultaneously trying to create pieces that could hit the runways in Paris next season?

    I definitely, passionately agree with your challenge, and I too would love to see more composers finding their unique voice and creating work that is fresh, but most of all, a lot less copping the very tired trends. On the other hand, I do think it is worthwhile to consider the quality of your work apart from picture. Music raises the picture, but it goes the other way – the picture can raise less-than-excellent music, blurring out poor/lazy writing.

  • http://www.davidpencil.com David Pencil

    There are two factors I’d like to bring up.
    First, every industry experiences a wide plane of people spinning out the same thing, with just a few who, through their innovation, change the face of what is “common” and continue pushing the envelope forward. For us, unlike our concert composer brethren, we are called into collaboration with directors, producers, and executives. I think that if we can find ourselves in (or better yet, create) situations where the people we are working with are bold enough and confident enough in our creativity to allow us to push the paradigm, then we would be foolish not to jump at the chance. Unfortunately, as some composers take the easy way out, so do some directors, asking for the same sounds heard on the big market so they can feel “like” everyone else.
    Secondly, though we may not agree with the “soundtrack style” being over-employed, I think it’s worth looking into some other possible reasonings for it besides just trying to produce more quickly or make more money. I’m speaking primarily to when the composer plans to separate the music from the film after completion either because he finds the video too distracting (either due to poor quality, overblown sfx, etc.), or to prove for pride’s sake that the music can stand on its own without the film supporting it. Neither of these are particularly amiable reasons for writing differently than your creative best for the specific project, but they are reasons which can be reached even outside of a juvenile or greedy mindset.
