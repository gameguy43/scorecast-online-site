Deane Ogden: Crappy Collaborations: A Blueprint for Success

by Deane Ogden · No Comments · in Uncategorized

Are you tired of being successful? Do you get sick of directors referring you to new filmmakers? Have you grown bored with watching your film music career speed ahead at a rapid pace?

Well, now you too can make it all go away by becoming a Horrible Collaborator!

That’s right! Now you can have the same broken dreams that thousands of flaky composers have, all due to your own self-sabotage! Just follow these ten simple rules, and your career as a film composer will be headed nowhere fast!

1. Never Communicate with Your Principals
The surest way to kill a collaboration is to stop talking to your bosses. Imagine how great it will make your director feel to work for months or years on his or her film, having total control over everything… that is until the day they have to hand it over to you, only to have you take it, disappear with it, and never give them any progress reports once you get it into your studio! I’m telling you, they LOVE THAT! In fact, by the third or fourth phone call or email that has gone unanswered by you, they’ll love you so much that they’ll already be talking to their producers about not using you the next time around. Suddenly, you’ll be the talk of the town!

2. Avoid Discussing Money At All Costs
Talking about money can be one of the toughest and most uncomfortable parts of negotiating a deal with a filmmaker. So I say JUST DON’T DO IT! This way, you don’t have to have that always awkward conversation when you are trying to get hired for the gig, and the producers don’t have to commit to a hard financial deadline — It’s a win-win for everybody! Essentially, you’ll be working for free until the conversation is finally had, and then you’ll be negotiating against work already performed. Love that! All in all, it’s good practice for when you eventually have to sell used cars because the “composing thing sorta didn’t really work out.” Just consider it “training for your future”!

3. Wait Until the Very Last Minute
Everyone loves a procrastinator, especially directors. Do yourself a favor and leave the planning, strategy, and discipline to the Line Producer! The composer has no business being organized enough to stay on schedule… besides, that’s no fun anyway. Who needs it? Instead, make sure that you spend your writing time playing on Facebook, rearranging the furniture in your studio, and checking out those new plug-ins on the Native Instruments website. Wasted time in the composing chair is the key to a happy director come delivery time!

4. Be as Uncompromising as You Can Possibly Be
Let’s face it: You’re the music expert, not the director. If the director was the expert, he or she wouldn’t have needed to hire you in the first place! See how that works? When a director says, “That just doesn’t work for me… Can we change it up a little?”, look at them like they are stupid and say, “I have it all under control. Maybe you should go see how the color-correcting is coming along!” This will put them in their place and guarantee that they never ask for another change again. And that’s what you want: A director that knows their place in your world!

5. Cut Corners on Originality
I have four words for you: Loops are your friend. I have four more words for you: Don’t tweak factory patches. Pay attention, kids… I’m throwing you pearls here! Anytime you can cut corners and skimp on quality, do it. Your director will love it when the same waterphone sound you used in his movie shows up a week later in another score. Another thing that is cool is to hear all of your film composer buddies talk about how you used a factory Taiko drum midi file straight out of SD2. And who knows, maybe you can parlay that into an endorsement from EastWest, to boot. After all, you’re just doing your part to put their little company on the map, right?

6. Backstab When It Benefits You
Since post-production generally takes it in the shorts on budget and time, it is customary for the dub crew, the mixers, and even the editors and SFX guys to badmouth the director and producers at some point during the post-production schedule. My advice? JOIN RIGHT IN! Those kinds of things NEVER get back to the victims, and if they ever do, chances are they’ll think you were just trying to be a comedian. If there is anyone in this business that checks their ego at the door, it’s the director and producer. They always have great senses of humor about those kinds of things and they never mind being ridiculed or complained about in front of their colleagues. Ever!

7. Make Sure Everyone Knows You Are Doing Them a HUGE Favor
Always remember this: It’s JUST a film! It’s not life or death, and it’s certainly not going to matter to any producer if you don’t stick to the terms set forth in your contract. If you are a couple of thousand dollars over budget, so what? If you are a month late on delivering, wah wah wah!! Everyone should just be happy that they are getting you, and shut their big fat mouths! Besides, this movie wouldn’t be what it is without your score in it, anyway. You are doing them a big gigantic favor by even working on this thing, so they need to give you your space. Since your so good that they are sure to hire you on all of their subsequent films, maybe next time you’ll throw them a bone and put a little more effort in. That is, if the budget is what it should have been this time, the cheapos! I mean, my God, what do they think you don’t need to eat or something?!?! Sheesh!

8. Be Unprepared for Meetings
Directors, studio executives, and producers have a lot of time to burn… Probably the most out of everyone on the planet. I mean, let’s be real: It’s not like they don’t have an army of people running around doing all their crap for them all day, right? So, big whoop if they get to your studio for playbacks and you are still finishing up the reel! The solution to this one is easy: First, make sure to ease their minds by telling them to “relax”… that “it will only be another twenty minutes or so” before you are ready to show them anything. Second, after the twenty minutes comes and goes, make sure to update them that you are “almost done… maybe another 10 or 15 minutes, tops.” Directors love having their time devalued and their schedules disrespected. In fact, I would encourage you to set the tone for the whole project by doing this from the outset. That way, there won’t be any surprises about how you are going to handle every approval session throughout the collaboration process. If it’s one thing directors hate… it’s surprises!

9. Show Up, Score It, and Move On
Go ahead and make it clear that anything extra that is asked of you is off the table. You do not go the extra mile! Once the film is over, move on to something else and forget about this one. Hey… you did your job! Leave the festivals, the junkets, and the promotion up to the PR guys. You fulfilled your contractual obligations and the check cleared, so you certainly don’t need to be spending your valuable time promoting something that you worked on a year ago. It’s not your fault the film just now found distribution! In fact, adopting an “as little as possible” attitude will get you farther down the path of career destruction than anything. Keep on taking the easy way out and you’ll be back living in your parents’ spare bedroom in a matter of weeks. Mmmm… Mama’s home cookin’!!!

10. Finish “Weak”
After years of planning, writing, re-writing, casting, scouting, shooting, editing, test-screening, and promoting, the last thing a director and producer want from you is a nice note or a gift letting them know how much you appreciate them and the opportunity they gave to you. Worse than that? A CD of the musical score. Oh Em Gee! I mean, how thoughtless could you be? Don’t you know that never calling them again until you see on IMDb that they are making another movie is what they really really want from you? Can’t you just please leave them alone? It’s not “Show Friends”, it’s “Show Business!” Geez!!!! Get it through your big dumb head!!!!!!!!

About The Author

Deane Ogden

SCOREcast's founder and editor-in-chief, DEANE OGDEN, is an American film composer, percussionist, author, and speaker who has scored dozens of feature films including The Sensei, Dreams On Spec, The Way Home, Surrogates, and the Cuba Gooding Jr. actioner The Hit List for Sony Pictures Entertainment. He has also written music for over 600 hours of network television, including the orchestral themes for Michael Phelps’ record-breaking gold medal run during the 2008 Beijing Olympic Games—the most-viewed event in American television history. As a top-call session and live-performance drummer for pop and rock radio, Deane has recorded and performed with numerous artists, including Tina Turner, Joss Stone, Seal, Chris Cornell, David Foster, Kenny Loggins, Gavin DeGraw, Jewel and Rihanna. You can find Deane’s extended bio, blog, and music at his official website, or connect with him personally at Twitter or Facebook.
