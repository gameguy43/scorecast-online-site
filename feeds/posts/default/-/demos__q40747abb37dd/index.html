Here's what I want you to be thinking about this weekend:\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eWho is your competition?\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003e\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003e\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003ca name\u003d'more'\u003e\u003c/a\u003e\u003cb\u003e\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIt's counter-intuitive, but in a way you face greater competition if you're just starting out than if you're somewhat established. Composers have become like lawyers… with more aspirants in training than practitioners making a living in the field. There are more composers on the D-list than the C-list; more on the C-list than the B-list, and so on. So the number of people targeting that $5,000 indie film is actually greater than the number gunning for the $200 million blockbuster. The major pictures are, of course, a much higher-stakes game, but the players at the table are fewer… especially considering that many of the filmmakers who work at that level already have their composer. C'mon, did you really think anyone but James Horner was gonna score \u003ci\u003eAvatar\u003c/i\u003e?\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eTaking a look at your \"level\" of gig—and don't get me wrong; I think going for projects that are a \u003ci\u003elittle\u003c/i\u003e over your head can be a good thing, if you approach it as a learning experience and a way to force yourself to stretch!—you can quickly assess the level of craftsmanship, experience, studio resources, etc. that you're likely to be facing in the competition. You can find out, in other words, who the competition really \u003ci\u003eis\u003c/i\u003e. That's something you need to know.\u003cbr /\u003eUse Google, social media of all sorts (searching Twitter is a great way to make some progress here)—seek out a couple dozen of your peers, whether you've ever made their acquaintance or not, and take stock. Figure out who the competition really is for the kinds of gigs you're trying to get. Hell, hit up IMDb and find out who got the gigs you didn't… if you're targeting your own demos appropriately, that alone should give you a pretty good view of the terrain.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eOnce you've determined that you're safe (for now) from having to compete with the Williamses and the Elfmen of the world, I recommend doing some \u003cb\u003eopposition research\u003c/b\u003e. We all put our cues on websites to be listened to; it's fair game for you to go and listen.\u0026nbsp;Compare your stuff with the competition.\u003cb\u003e\u0026nbsp;\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003ePart One\u0026nbsp;\u003c/b\u003eof this technique is to play a couple of cues from several people who are at your level… then play a couple of your own cues. A-B. Back and forth. Be critical: Are you doing as well or better in terms of finished musical product? Chances are you're at least close. Congratulations. But don't get too cocky. Focus on the stuff others are doing better.\u003cb\u003e\u0026nbsp;\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003ePart Two\u003c/b\u003e of this strategy is more painful, more humbling, but crucial. Play your stuff, A-B, with the composers at the top of the food chain. Producers don't care that you don't have rooms and rooms full of gear, with small armies of studio underlings, to make everything sparkle. They just want their score to KILL. So A-B yourself with the people you think are absolutely the best in terms of writing chops, production quality, dramatic sensitivity. And draw the appropriate conclusions about where you stand. You might even try specifically writing a few cues for your demo as \"\u003ci\u003ex\u003c/i\u003e-killers\" (where \u003ci\u003ex\u003c/i\u003e is one of your favorite film-score cues by a composer other than yourself). See if you can out-do your heroes. Benefit from the fact that they've already done a lot of the hard work by defining the parameters of the cue. Just be sure to do it in a way that conveys your personality. Filmmakers can smell a Thomas Newman-alike or a John Williams-alike a mile off, and they'll wonder if you have anything original to contribute to their project.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eBut creating amazing music, as we always emphasize around here, is only a part of the equation. You might do well to re-evaluate yourself as a competitor (my \u003ca href\u003d\"../../../../../2009/06/phases-of-game.html\"\u003eblog entry\u003c/a\u003e on this subject is still available, and this might be a good time to take the quiz, if you haven't already).\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eEvaluate the competition, too—see what you like about their websites… their online personalities… the way they're leveraging social media. Then simply do what it takes to make your situation better. Use your own taste and your own resources as a guide.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eDefining exactly who your competition is (by how far along you are, what your network of Industry contacts looks like, etc.) allows you to keep one eye on the fellow members of whatever list you honestly think you're on… and it allows you to keep the other eye on the folks at the top of the heap.
