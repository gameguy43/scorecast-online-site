And there's a pacing to the thing: it ought to be at a level commensurate with the gigs you're actually getting (and if you simply \u003ci\u003emust\u003c/i\u003e compare and compete, do so with those peers who are at your own level).\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI'd love to have a brand spanking new Euphonix or SSL console; who wouldn't? But I don't need it. It's way out of profile with where I am in my career. And the fact that (insert A-lister here) just bought one, or five, or whatever, shouldn't really bear on my decision. If I let it, I'm allowing my urge to compete over my head put the rest of my finances in jeopardy. And that's dumb. I don't need any more machinery to legitimize what I do.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eMy take on this: put the blinders on, run your own race, and unclench. When you need gear, get the best you can afford and no more. (it's enough to have to spend just doing that!)\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eLast thought for now: \u003c/b\u003eThere are all sorts of aspects of this biz in which thinking of the struggle as a competition can be healthy. It spurs us on to do more than we might be inclined to otherwise. It takes us out of the safe, familiar zone where no growth occurs. It gives us definitive, often-humbling feedback about how we're progressing.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eBut competing over who can go the longest without sleeping, or who has neglected their family and friends the longest, or even who can write the craziest number of minutes of music in a day? Somewhere in there is that oft-mentioned line between hard-ass and dumb-ass. Tread carefully. Run the right race. Don't let your hardcore composer status become an end unto itself.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eLurkers—here's a great chance to pitch in your own thoughts. I'd love to hear what you think on this one. What have I missed? Are there other areas where we're all guilty of getting too amped-up, \u003ci\u003etoo\u003c/i\u003e competitive? Your people skills are honed, crisp and extra-sparkly. Your phone calls are getting returned, and the gatekeepers seem ready to say the magic word—\u003cb\u003eyes\u003c/b\u003e.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThen, one by one, those gigs just… slip away. \u003ca href\u003d\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_swan_theory\"\u003eBlack swans\u003c/a\u003e start cropping up in your work life—unforeseen interlopers that threaten to swoop in and snatch the fruits of victory out of your hands.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eWhat—and who—are these \u003cb\u003eunexpected competitors\u003c/b\u003e? Knowing about them—and how to compete with them—should be an integral part of your game plan. So let's take a look, after the jump.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003ca name\u003d'more'\u003e\u003c/a\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003e1. The Budget Composer.\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThis person's a vulture—preying on productions whose budgets are tight. He swoops in fast and lowballs everyone else off the table. Sure, it's no way to earn a living in the long-term. Budget Composer is hoping to cement a relationship with the filmmakers, though, so that he'll be a lock for the next gig (and, through some sort of magical process, the filmmakers will decide he's worth a lot more money next time around).\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eTwo problems with this approach: first, \"lowball\" has come to mean \u003ci\u003ezero\u003c/i\u003e. And it takes real creativity to get below that (of course, some composers are now striking deals to give away their royalties, in effect literally paying for the privilege of writing music).\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eSecond, chasing an expanded music budget on the next gig is like chasing the horizon. It's out there, tantalizing you but never coming within reach. Take it from me. It's not going to happen. Not if you do a standout job with the budget you have. Think of it like a producer: Why would you pay more for the same product—especially when the person providing that product desperately\u0026nbsp;\u003ci\u003eneeds\u003c/i\u003e the gig?\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI think the best tactic against Budget Composer is to take the high line. Know what you cost. Don't deviate from it. And if you do deviate from it, make it clear up front that the filmmakers are getting a x score (where x is the rate you've chosen for yourself) for y dollars (where y is the amount you're agreeing to), and that this special one-time offer won't be repeated. You will lose gigs this way, but my take on it is that they're predatory gigs to begin with. If \"low cost\" is the sole criterion (or even the #1 criterion) for choosing a composer, then you have to wonder about the filmmakers' priorities elsewhere in the show. And you have to ask yourself if you want to spend the rest of your career on your knees, begging for every extra penny.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eBy the way, if this gig is for someone with whom you have an existing track record—i.e, if they already know your rate and have paid it in the past—you might be wise to take any offer of this sort to be a relationship-threatening insult. Unless everyone on board is taking a hit just to do the project (and the filmmakers are taking a bigger hit than anyone else!)… and even then I'd advise you to be wary.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIf your standing (social \u003ci\u003eand\u003c/i\u003e professional) with the filmmakers is solid, though, I still claim that you'll have some general protection because of the rule that states: people generally don't screw over their friends. Sure, it happens… but less often than you think. Keep those relationships right and tight, and you'll find out for yourself.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003e2. The Music Supervisor.\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eWhen the position of music supervisor was first imagined, it pertained almost exclusively to the domain of \u003ci\u003esongs\u003c/i\u003e in films. Now music supervisors have begun to assert jurisdiction over the score-selection process as well. In addition to presenting a \"short list\" of songs and/or artists to be considered for placement in a film or television project, they often recommend one or more composers for the gig. And their recommendations, increasingly, are carrying weight. Not just with individual filmmakers, but with networks and studios as well. The model is evolving: in a growing number of cases your demo will have to go through a music supervisor before it's even mentioned to any other gatekeepers.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThis additional middleman can save the filmmakers an immense amount of time—and never more so than in today's composer-saturated environment. We've talked before about the hundreds—literally hundreds—of demos that land on the desks of filmmakers for each and every gig. Giving all those demos legitimate consideration really is a full-time job.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eOf course, if you have a pre-existing relationship with those other gatekeepers, you can communicate with them directly, regardless of whether a music supervisor is in the picture. And if you're friendly with the music supervisor, you may find yourself with a powerful ally in the competition to land a gig. You might even get a call out of the blue from a music supervisor, letting you know he or she is recommending you for a job!\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eSo, while this additional layer of 'triage' does nudge composers a little further from the heart of the creative team, it also presents opportunities for you to enlist other Industry professionals as your evangelists. It's all in making those connections.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003e3. The Music Library House.\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIf you're writing any sort of library music, this is your dilemma. The production companies that make these shows are less and less inclined to speak with individual composers. It's just too much of a hassle to pick up a few dozen cues here and there. They simply don't have the time… or the budget to pay someone to hassle with the phone calls and e-mails and downloads and paperwork.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eReality shows today deal more and more frequently with massive music library corporations, who can 'represent' tens of thousands of tracks. The task of \"filling out\" the music for a show becomes a one-stop shopping kind of event—a single phone call from a line producer to a music library sales rep (though they typically give themselves a more high-flung title, that's really what they are) can make it happen.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eSo if you want to compete in the music library biz under your own banner (i.e., not writing for an existing library company or composer), the bare minimum number of cues you'd better have ready to go is 500. Your mileage may vary, but in my experience that's where it seems to be shaking out these days.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIf you don't have that many cues just lying around (!), one option is to get into business with an existing outfit. You'll be aligning yourself with a company that already has established contacts, in a business where contacts are the coin of the realm. The only thing I'd mention here is that sometimes the composer at the other end of this ridiculous broken-line connection is actually good. And you have to admire the networking skill required to pull off something like this. Don't hate the game… don't even hate the player. As with the temp track, the secret here is: don't assume a rule exists just because a tactic is personally distasteful to you.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003e6. Or any number of other teams that break up and come back together… sometimes precisely at the moment you think you're a lock for the gig! Ouch. It's a tough one to take (believe me—I've experienced it), and yet there's something to be said for loyalty. Even if that loyalty is sporadic.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThere's not much else to say here… if you get edged out by an Old Flame, there's little to be done.
