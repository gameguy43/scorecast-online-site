I’ve been in Los Angeles since early March 2013 on an Australia Council-funded mentorship program, absorbing all things film music and SoCal: The Good, The Bad and The Bellicose. These are my reflections so far.<\/p>\n

Transit Tantrums<\/h2>\n

LA traffic on Thursday afternoon is slower than a George R. R. Martin novel…and in my case on a shuttle ride, just as gob-smacking when something finally happened. Two young girls in front were chatting excitedly about their impending adventure. Now, they weren’t exactly shrill but the conversation was audible to all. 40 minutes in, a baby-boomer businessman had an explosive outburst: “We DON’T CARE about your banal existence!”…stunned silence. A whimpered response. Simmering tension until they were dropped off…at the same hotel as Mr Explosion and his embarrassed wife. Welcome to Hollywood, girls!<\/p>\n

I went to Chattanooga<\/a> for a weekend, as you do. I had a dawn flight out of LAX so stayed at an airport hotel the night before. Passed the concierge as a man was SCREAMING HIS HEAD OFF at a cab driver for apparently ripping him off all of $9. He kept threatening to call the cops but finally backed down.<\/p>\n

On LAX-CHA flight 1, the overhead lockers filled up and a bunch of people had to check their bags through to final destinations, including myself. I didn’t care; all I needed was my iPad and headphones. Others did care<\/em> – airline staff were hammered by well-heeled women with an inflated sense of entitlement, indignant that they wouldn’t have access to their L’Oreal travel kits and Xanax.<\/p>\n

Then there was the 4-hour delay in Charlotte due to the incoming plane having ‘mechanical issues’. Initially it was 1 hour, then 2…etc. Was it annoying? Yes. Did it warrant the ageing rocker with a gig that night having an absolute meltdown at the check-in girl, to the point that once again the police were mentioned? NO. I wasn’t overly happy myself, but would much rather be delayed than fall out of the sky. And for a while there it did feel like the precursor to an episode of ‘Air Crash Investigations’.<\/p>\n

SO – my point is, this was very early into my visit and I was thinking ‘holy crap, is this REALLY what Americans are like?’ NO IT ISN’T. The symposium was FANTASTIC and when I returned to LA, the perpetually warm glow of Angelenos restored my societal faith. I guess travel brings out the best\/worst in anyone and I saw some doozies of the latter kind. 99.9% of Americans are nice, genuinely interested in foreigners and usually mean it when they say “what can I do to help?”. Embrace that percentile.<\/p>\n

Location\/Vehiculation<\/h2>\n

Something anyone new to to town should consider carefully, well before landing. Unlike Melbourne, where I could walk 5 minutes to the Royal Botanic Gardens, Fawkner Park or my local cafe strip, LA isn’t really<\/em> a city. Rather, it’s a vast sprawl of mini-cities connected by a web of freeways, each enclave having a unique flavour that may or may not suit your taste. Santa Monica and Venice are renowned for their beach lifestyle and slightly cooler air, but if most of your stuff’s happening Downtown or in The Valley, you’ll spend an awful lot of time on the road if you’re based out west. I knew I’d be hanging in Studio City\/ Hollywood a lot, so took the time to find and pre-book the perfect AirBNB rental, smack in the middle. Slightly pricey but the location definitely saved me time and money. If AirBNB-style pads are beyond your budget, the ubiquitous CRAIGSLIST is a great place to find sub-lets, especially during summer holidays.<\/p>\n

Mode of transport is a no-brainer. Unless you have incredibly helpful friends going to Point B (which I actually often do) or don’t mind spending hours traversing multi Metro routes, you need a car<\/strong><\/em>. If you have no choice but to get a cab somewhere, KNOW THIS: They’ll take at least 20 minutes to turn up, cost a lot and woe betide the passenger that dares to pay by credit card \u2013 even if your cab-finder app says it’s cool. I had a heated argument with a driver about this very point; in the end he was only placated when I tipped cash. If you don’t want to drive on a night out, a far better option is LYFT<\/a>: a smartphone app that uses your location to find and order a driver in minutes, is 20% cheaper, 100% friendlier and automatically calculates a tip-inclusive fare \u2013 ONLY from your credit card. Ok, so they have weird pink moustaches on the cars, but most drivers also stock water and snack treats : )<\/p>\n

LA Flakers \u2014 A Few Bad Players or a Team Malaise?<\/h2>\n

Much has been said and written about the notoriously slow\/complete lack of follow through here in Tinseltown; one friend even suggested that ‘yes means maybe, maybe means no and people never say no’. Hackneyed phrases are quoted with mirth by the cheerfully cynical: \u201clet’s do lunch\u201d – \u201dwe’ll talk\u201d – etc. Well. I made a conscious decision to ignore the clich\u00e9 and decide for myself what the deal is. Consequently, I’ve been very surprised by the reality:<\/p>\n

Those Who’ve Flaked:<\/h3>\n

– Old Aussie friends now based here, who wanted things from me on a social\/industry\/material level (they got 2 of the 3) but gave nothing back. Even after I said \u201cYou’re not going to flake on me are you, we will catch up?\u201d I tried to follow up. Twice. We never caught up, but I wouldn’t be surprised if I suddenly hear from them just before their next gig.
\n– Potential new LA friends via Bookface; they were going to ‘reach out’ one weekend…back in March. I tried to follow up. Twice. They de-friended me. Maybe my dry humour was misconstrued when I sent a link to that Four Tops track.<\/p>\n

Those Who Haven’t Flaked:<\/h3>\n

– New composer friends, including very busy ones working on high-rating shows.
\n– Everyone in the industry I’ve emailed\/left a message for, including those I don’t know that well or haven’t met yet. I did wait 3 weeks for one reply, but this was a new contact and I time-budgeted accordingly.<\/p>\n

So \u2013 I’ve been mildly affected by flakelenos at social level, but industry\/music folks have treated me with more than a modicum of politeness and respect. Maybe because I haven’t burdened myself with unrealistic expectations or exuded the desperate ‘I want something from you’ vibe that you can smell a mile away from so many young aspirants. If you come here thinking you’ll reach the summit straight away, THAT is flakey. Ed Hilary completed his first major climb 13 YEARS before scaling Everest. Patience, persistence and prudence, my friends…<\/p>\n

Event Etiquette<\/h2>\n

Outside of my mentorship with Jeff Cardoni<\/a> (CSI:Miami, Wilfred) I’ve attended a swag (Australian vernacular) of workshops, screenings, seminars and masterclasses. Each event has featured high calibre composers, orchestrators, conductors, directors, or moderators and has been well conceived. Sadly, I can’t say the same for some of the questions<\/em>\u00a0that inevitably slip through the relevance cordon at these things. IT’S NOT ABOUT YOU! Do you really think that standing up so that everyone can see you when you say “Hi, my name’s [wannabe composer X] and I’ve been working on\/done a lot of [gratuitous promo Y] and I work in [DAW Z],” will make them think “Wow, I’m really interested in this guy and what he does, he must write like me because he uses DAW Z”<\/em>…?\u00a0Truth is, by the time you’ve got to the actual question, half the room has tuned out (or in my case, head in hands) and the A-lister will remember your face for all the wrong reasons when you push your way through the post-event throng. Then there are those that ask questions just to be ‘involved’ and ‘seen’, even if they’re “just a songwriter”…and you seriously want to punch them when they ask Thomas Newman if he “scores to picture”. Or the guy that asked Aaron Zigman if he “hums ideas in the shower”. Come on people! Be enthusiastic, but don’t be a wanker.<\/p>\n

Weeding Out the Tall Poppy<\/h2>\n

Australians and New Zealanders are not known for overt self-promotion. In fact, those that say “I’m a big deal” are more often than not regarded as wankers and ostracised by their peers. This is the Tall Poppy Syndrome<\/em><\/strong> at work \u2013 a ‘cut them down’ attitude towards high achievers that is uniquely endemic to Australasia. When I was younger and just out of film music school, I was brimming with enthusiasm; thrilled with any work I could get and any composer I met. Said enthusiasm combined with the mild hero-worship of a composer I spent time with somehow lead to me being perceived as a ‘name-dropper’ by a high-ranking person in the Australian film music world. Fast forward four years to the Australian Screen Music Awards, which I was invited to by the organisation this person works for. At an industry lunch prior to the ceremony I made a concerted effort to have a pleasant conversation with this person. But when I told her I was looking forward to the awards, she asked \u201cAre you attending legitimately?\u201d<\/p>\n

Happily, my experiences in LA have been the complete opposite. Here, it’s okay to talk about what you’ve done, what you’re doing, who you’ve connected with and what your aims are. I’ve been doing so with complete openness and honesty over the last 3 months and have been treated with more respect than I ever was in Australia. In fact, one delightful Executive Director (who holds a comparable position to my antipodean nemesis) now refers to me as her ‘favourite Kiwi’. And you know what? I can write about my awesome night at Jeff Beal<\/a>‘s place without fear of someone telling everybody I’ve been blowing my own trumpet. I can mention the Hollywood Music in Media Awards<\/a> competition I won – a scoring session with Mr. Beal; helping him record a 25-piece string orchestra. I can say that we exchanged CDs and that he particularly likes my choral stuff.<\/p>\n

Los Angeles certainly takes some getting used to, but it’s so worth it. The top film music people I’ve met genuinely embrace talent, passion and commitment when they see it.<\/p>\n

This, above all, makes me want to stick around.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
