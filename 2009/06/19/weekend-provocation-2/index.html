Weekend Provocation

By Lee Sanders  /   June 19, 2009  /  

Welcome to the brand-new home of the “Provocations” column. We’re still finding the best schedule for all this content we’re creating on SCOREcast, and it seems like the weekend is a good time for mulling over the kinds of issues we tackle here.

Remember, as always, that these provocations aren’t necessarily to be taken literallyâ€”they’re intended to spur discussion and to prompt new perspectives. There is no right or wrong answer, in other words.

Provocation:Â Composers should charge as little as possible for their work.


OK, now we’re getting serious. Hitting where it countsâ€¦ in the pocketbook. Film composers are one of the few groups in Hollywood that aren’t represented by a union. The Musician’s Union (Local 47 in Los Angeles) represents players, orchestrators, conductors, copyists, etc.â€”but not composers. When it comes to setting our fees, we’re on our own. Should we really low-ball ourselves into oblivion? On the face of it, this provocation seems like a really rotten business plan.

For me, though, this provocation brings to mind a piece of wisdom from Richard Bellis: “In this business, there are three things: good, fast and cheap. Don’t ever let your client have more than two out of three.”

Deciding what to charge, and when to score a project for less than our usual rateâ€”these are incredibly personal subjects. So I wouldn’t presume to offer advice here. If you are sacrificing on the budget-side, though, you ought to be getting something substantial in return: more musicians, more equity in the projectâ€¦ something. To quote Bellis again: “Always know why you’re taking the gig.” Saying “yes” to a project just so you can call yourself a professional composer, in other words, shouldn’t really be enough.

At the very least, you should insist on keeping the rights to the music you create! There has to be a balance at some point; otherwise, you’re just being used. And that makes your filmmaking collaborators users.

Taken less literally, “charging as little as possible” could mean asking as little as possible in terms of things other than money. Keeping our personal drama out of the work space. Making sure our communications are focused. Hitting our deadlines.

Thinking of our provocation this way leads me to a terrific metaphor, which I first heard from Mike Post:

“A producer is basically a guy with a big desk. The desk has dozens of drawers, and inside each drawer something is on fire. The producer’s job is to go around putting out all the fires. So we composers want to be that one drawer the producer never has to waste time openingâ€”there’s never a fire there.”

Whatever it takes to give them that confidence, in other words, is worth doing. That’s one way in which this provocation could be trueâ€”but there have to be some others. For once, I’m a little stumped by my own provocation, so jump in with some comments and let me know what you think.
