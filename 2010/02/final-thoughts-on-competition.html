Deane Ogden: Final Thoughts on “Competition”

by Deane Ogden · No Comments · in Uncategorized
I think that this has been one of my favorite topics we’ve discussed so far. The truth is that there is so much to the topic of “competition” that pertains to this gig, we could probably have taken two months to examine it all. Maybe we’ll come back and revisit it again soon. Kudos to everyone on our editorial staff for bringing their A-game this month and kicking in the doors of this topic with both feet.

As we wind down February and head into another month, and with that a new focus, here are some final thoughts I have about this month’s theme of “COMPETITION”….

In his book The Reel World, film composer Jeff Rona illustrates the importance of speed and accuracy in our trade by summing, “It’s not what you can do — it’s what you can do in five minutes.” This couldn’t be closer to the truth, and if you have spent any time at all in the captain’s chair of your own career in any industry, you know that success is merely preparedness colliding with opportunity.

Before I get too much further, let me say something I’ve wanted to put into words here at SCOREcast on many occasions, but have admittedly been afraid to. I’ve been afraid to, not only because I don’t want to come across as some pompous egomaniac who has seen all and done all (that certainly couldn’t be farther from the truth!), but also because I don’t want to dash anyone’s dreams of transcending seemingly unavoidable circumstances such as location, resources, or finances in order to become a successful film composer. However, I feel that I cannot go too much longer without stating what I have learned over the years to be the obvious:

The majority of those who call themselves film composers will never work on a studio feature film, no matter how good their music is.

Now, before you start thinking that I’ve let the “Hollywood curse” of reality get the best of my usual optimism, let me explain what I mean.

As I’ve worked through the various levels of career that I have (and understand, in many ways I feel that I’m just getting started), I’ve discovered that as every realized victory has given me access to a new level of play, it has also forced me to learn to play the game differently at every turn. Each time you bust through the ceiling of a new terrace in this career, you have to relearn how to compete. Every new level means you have to be that much more on the ball, that much more aware, that much more agile, more thoughtful, more available, more attentive, more communicative, and more adaptable to the sliding scales of the way your new peer group does business. The way I play the game at the studio level — dealing with Warner Bros., Disney, Fox, Sony, etc. — is NOT the way I play the game when I’m dealing with Joe Producer and his merry band of independent filmmakers. In fact, it is completely different. I’ve changed the way I do business so drastically since the time that I started out that it often seems those “salad years” happened to somebody else.

The fact is that you might be perfectly happy as an independent film composer. It’s a great place to be; a wonderful level to work at with incredibly talented filmmakers popping up all over the place, and amazing rewards for you as a composer. But even IF you plan on staying at that level, and not pursuing studio level features, you would do yourself a favor by adopting the competitive habits and practices of studio-level composers.

Why? Because. Studio-level composers have a bevy of issues to concern themselves with in regards to staying competitive that you will most likely never even have to think about as an indie filmmaker. Imagine being the independent composer in your area that runs his or her ship like a 5-star Hollywood film studio would expect you to. Imagine the reputation you would build if you were just slightly quicker at responding to production problems, better at communicating with the producers, easier to ask for changes from, and were just generally a better oiled music-making machine that never gave problems and rarely caused a flap for anyone. Not only would you be the most obvious choice — assuming your music kicked all kinds of ass, too — but you would also be well on your way to having handled the hard parts of what kills the careers of most Hollywood film music hopefuls.

WHAT’S THE DIFFERENCE?
Studios do not do things the same way that independent filmmakers do. They can’t. If they did, they’d be out of business tomorrow. The pace of the major studio runs at least ten times faster than that of the independent filmmaker. Studios require certain things of their composers that the independent world couldn’t care less about. Studio execs place expectations on their composers that independent producers frankly don’t ever need to, nor want to, think about.

If you really do desire to make this jump — out of the indie market and into a life of studio features — you’ll first need to take the mental leaps necessary to start training yourself for a different kind of competition.

Again, I’m NOT putting down indie composers! The vast majority of my work is in the independent world. But when I am booked on the Lot of a major motion picture studio and am working on a studio project, my habits and my understanding of the game need to elevate to their level, or I’m in trouble.

Let me run you through a handful of those “understandings”, in an effort to outline the expectations you’ll need to address before you can expect to be at all competitive in the studio realm.

THOUGHT 1: A SENSE OF URGENCY
In my dealings with major studios, certainly one of the most important things I’ve learned is that when the iron is hot, you HAVE to strike. You cannot wait, and you cannot hesitate. When you find out that there is a television show that is looking for a new musical solution, if you have an “in”… you need to get a package through the door, and you need to do it NOW. When you receive knowledge that the studio is unhappy with the way things are going musically with their current composer, and the rumblings of replacement have already leaked to you, it’s time to act and act fast.

In the independent world, you might have the luxury of a few days before a decision is made on who gets the job. Therefore, if you get the demo in today or tomorrow… it’s not going to make that big a difference. However, on a studio project — particularly in TV — you might have a window of as little as a few hours to get something turned in, and if you are not prepared ahead of time to make that happen, you might as well forget it.

Have your demos ready to rock at ALL times. If I called you right this second and told you I needed a demo sent over ASAP, would it take you a day to put a general reel together for me? You need to have general reels ready to go out the door right now, as well as reels containing specific genres of your material in case you get a request for a “Dramatic Reel” or an “Action Reel”, and so forth. Not being caught with your pants down in regards to demo preparedness is sort of “Studio Feature Film Composer 101″, and is a primary reason why people lose out on gigs at studios.

You also need to be able to take meetings when you are called to. Needing to rely on the bus schedule or the Metro to get you to Fox at 4pm on a Friday is going to be a problem if you are coming from the Valley. You need wheels, pure and simple. I recently heard of a guy who is a composer in his mid-30′s and he still doesn’t know how to drive a car. To me, that’s ridiculous. How could you even think you can compete if you cannot even drive yourself to meet with a table of people who want to hand you a job?


THOUGHT 2: CLEAR AND FREQUENT COMMUNICATION
In the independent world, you might not hear from your director or producers for a few days/weeks, as they are most likely wearing several hats: director, picture editor, post-production supervisor, color corrector, sound mixer, title designer… you name it, they’re doing it. By contrast, in the studio world, everyone has a job (usually protected by a union), and everyone does that job, and THAT job only. Therefore, your principals are not running like crazy people as much as they would be if they were handling every minor detail personally. They have a lot more time on their hands to “manage by walking around” then they would if they were producing an independent feature.

Take it upon yourself to lead the way in making calls to check in as a team. Make sure that your principals don’t have to ask you twice if you “spoke with the editor about that change in the picture” or whether or not you uploaded the file you promised to the night before. Don’t make them repeat themselves. Make promises sparingly, but keep them faithfully. If you cannot pull something off in the time you know you have to do it, don’t commit to doing it during that time frame. But no matter what, pick up the phone and communicate.

And another thing: Stop “texting”. There is nothing more unprofessional than receiving a text message from someone that contains information that would be better communicated in person or voice to voice over the telephone. “I’m five minutes away,” is a text. “I’m going to run 5 grand over budget,” is a phone call. :-)

Here is my personal code of conduct when it comes to communicating with higher-ups in the studio realm: Less is more, accuracy is imperative, and person to person is always better. That last part is important for several reasons. First, it gives you the opportunity to either be heard audibly or seen physically — which is always a good thing. Secondly, texts, emails, and voicemails get buried. Voice to voice contact or face to face meetings (if it is time-appropriate) make YOU a priority — you aren’t on some list somewhere… you are standing there fully engaged with the person. Way better.

THOUGHT 3: PROBLEM-FREE PARTICIPATION
Studio executives are some of the busiest people I’ve met in this industry. There is a misconception out there that if you work at a studio, you spend the majority of your time sitting around and watching the clouds roll by.

Not true.

I’ve also heard frustrated composers say things like, “I could do that job — be a studio executive.” Umm… no, you couldn’t. You don’t have it in you. The fact that you are saying that is only the tip of the iceberg as to why you’d never have the chutzpah to work on the Lot. Trust me.

I’ve become close to several studio people in the years I’ve been doing this and let me tell you… there’s no group of people who work harder under more pressure. The Studio Lot is a place of ridiculous intensity where ungodly expectations are being put on people day in and day out. I’m sure that you have at one point or another experienced the pressure of working for an employer who is concerned for his or her “bottom-line”. Studios are no different, except that when the bottom-line isn’t met every quarter, heads will literally roll. It is a pressure-cooker atmosphere that chews up and spits out the very best people in Hollywood on a fairly regular basis.

It is because of this already intense pressure to achieve the numbers that you, as a third-party freelance contractor, should never present a problem without a solution. Studio people simply do not have the time for extra problems, let alone ones that YOU are creating. You want to get in with the people who are the movers and shakers of the industry? Be a Solution. You’ve heard it said before that there really is no such thing as “writer’s block”. I mean, there IS… everyone gets stumped every once in a while, yes. But there truly is, however, no such thing as the excuse of it. You simply cannot show up to an approval session with no cues in hand and blame it on the fact that you couldn’t think of anything to write that day, right?

Well, being a problematic composer on a studio film is the exact same thing. If you are a pain in the ass, you’ll literally never work with them again. That phrase is not just a cliche. I see it all the time.

The opposite approach is best. I remember the first opportunity I had to provide additional music for Disney on a project. At the orchestral sessions, on a break, I overheard one of the executives from the music department asking the music editor if I was “playing ball” with them; meaning, was I being cool, getting my work done, and not causing headaches.

The editor answered with a smile, “Oh yeah! He’s done everything we’ve needed him to do, without fail. He’s great!”

“Good,” said the executive. “I like when it’s easy.”

Hearing that from someone I respect like that studio executive made my decade. To know that I was being regarded as a team player and not someone who was creating drama or friction on what had already a bit of a roller-coaster project for them made me feel like the king of the world. The sessions were incredible, my music sounds great in the film, and both Disney and the director were happy with the job I did. Big BIG win.

And that’s how you want to be. You want to be the person that comes in, does the job, encourages others to be their best, and pushes the production along as best you can with no controversy, drama, politics, or bullshit. No grandstanding, no ego, no crap — just pure, raw, energy that is excited to be there and thankful for the opportunity to prove that you’ve got the goods.

Confident humility. That’s what it’s all about.

THOUGHT 4: LET THE STUDIO SYSTEM WORK
Making the leap from independent films to studio features takes some time. Just as you didn’t work your way from $5k indie features to million dollar indie features overnight, you’re also not going to be handed a theatrically-released studio feature to score right out of the gate. It’s nothing personal, you just aren’t worth the risk yet. You are still an unproven entity to any major studio. With hard work, perseverance, and a small amount of magic, you’ll get your chance, but you have to let the studio system do its work for you. It will, if you let it. And here’s a tip: The sooner you start thinking of the studios as potential “partners” on gigs and not as potential “suppliers” of gigs, the quicker the stars will align for you.

Be patient… and more importantly, be respectful. If you are lucky enough to get in the door so far as to be told “we’ll let you know”, do not call the next day if you still don’t “know”. If you do things like this, you are already considered a problem.

I know a guy who called the studio that had already informed him that he didn’t get a gig for weeks after, with the intention of “keeping his name in circulation”. I’ve heard people refer to these types of folks as “tail waggers”. The moniker sounds silly, but it is accurate. Susie and I had a dog once and this is how he acted when he wanted “treats”. If you come across that way as a composer, imagine how a studio executive would view you as a potential business partner. Studio: 1. You: 0.

Being persistent has its advantages, but being a pain in someone’s ass never does. It takes a lot to run a major studio, and the film you are gunning for is one of hundreds in production. Just because people aren’t falling all over themselves to return your phone calls doesn’t mean they aren’t working to get you on the gig. Let them do their job and be patient.

CONCLUSION
These are only a few thoughts I’ve had this month as we’ve beat this topic senseless around here. If you have some of your own, I want to hear them, and so does the rest of this community. Jump on the COMMENTS below and share your thoughts and solutions on “competition”.

About The Author

Deane Ogden

SCOREcast's founder and editor-in-chief, DEANE OGDEN, is an American film composer, percussionist, author, and speaker who has scored dozens of feature films including The Sensei, Dreams On Spec, The Way Home, Surrogates, and the Cuba Gooding Jr. actioner The Hit List for Sony Pictures Entertainment.
