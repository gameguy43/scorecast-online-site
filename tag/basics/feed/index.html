After all, we were born with this, right? It’s in our DNA.

Arguable, at best. A lot of people say we were born with certain aptitudes toward this gig but I would argue that while that might be true about musical intuition and a predisposition toward cognitive retention of scales Â and other functional repeating mathematical sequences, quality skilled composition only comes out of tireless practice, varied experience and loads of trial and error. It’s not a far-flung theory then to assume that there could be a serious self-esteem issue working against emerging composers, dare I say even veteran ones who are willing to admit the ugly truth. Are we Weenies? Wimps? Candie-asses? I don’t think so. But we are human beings and from time to time every artist, no matter the discipline, needs to check the ‘ol barometer.

 

You’re Not a Bad Composer

… if you can’t seem to write anything today.

Writer’s block sucks. But even though the cool thing to say is “I don’t have time for that”, that still doesn’t mean it never happens. The truth is that it does happen… to everybody. You aren’t a bad composer if you are not able to write for whatever reason. You might be a late composer, but you aren’tÂ a bad one.

How to get rid of it? Studies show that stress is the leading cause of “mental blocking” and that eliminating stress-related activities or events in your life can also lower your chances of stalling out creatively. Want to drill down further? Those same studies show that the number one cause of stress, at least among Americans, is finances. If that means something to you… get on it. Scale back, pare down, save more, spend less, slash and burn, budget hard, charge more â€” whatever you need to do, but get that financial monkey off your back, and fast. It’s a killer. Literally.

… if you don’t understand the theories behind music in film.

It just means you have a few things to learn if you are to be asÂ effective a film music writer as you can be. You might already be an effective composer, but as a film composer you have to intimately understand the marriage of music and drama. If you don’t, then you are missing a huge piece of the puzzle as a talented composer looking to crack this particular medium. Obviously you aren’t a bad composer if this skill still eludes you, but I’ll warn you that without it you are always going to be composing from a defensive posture â€” meaning that whatever is thrown at you, you’ll either need to be lucky enough to intuitively feel it and magically hit that bullseye on the first dart throw or you’ll need to study up every time out, whether that’s through asking seasoned vets or “listening” to seasoned vets via Netflix streaming. Point being, you’ll always be playing “catch up”. That game gets old fast, slows you down, and eventually begins to erode your confidence.

But don’t worry… it’s an easy fix. All you have to do is find a way to absorb the principles and theories behind drama. Extended viewing and note-taking can do it, but so will auditing a few film appreciation courses at the local community college. Or reading a stack of good books on the subject. I’d recommend starting with Earle Hagen’sÂ Advanced Techniques for Film Scoring. Hagen’s book is grossly outdated in its purview of technology, but the score analysis and score/script interaction is second to none. If you really want to steam your head, pick up Fred Karlin’s classic brick of a bookÂ On the Track. Often referred to as “the film scoring bible”, On the Track offers probably the most extensive perspective on various approaches to dramatic writing for the medium of film.

… if you’ve been rejected, fired or ignored. A lot.

That doesn’t mean you suck. Not necessarily, anyway. Although, a case could be made that if you got so far as to pitch for the gig itÂ won’tÂ matter if you suck anyway because you obviously have what it takes to get through the door. Any well-heeled major player knowsÂ that ain’t easy. But being told that “your music just isn’t what we’re looking for” isÂ tough. What’s tougher, though,Â is hearingÂ that time and again. And even still, that doesn’t necessarily mean you are a bad composer. You’ll have to gauge this one for yourself and use your best and most honest evaluations of your output, but there have been plenty of people â€” hundreds â€” who’ve beenÂ turnedÂ down dozens upon dozens of times before they ever scored a note to picture and even more than that who’ve been successful and then been told theyÂ were washedÂ up, irrelevant, too old, too young, too inexperienced, too expensive, too high-profile, notÂ high-profileÂ enough… you name it. Point being: Rejection happens and you have to learn to accept it and deal with it appropriately.

There are many ways to face rejection, accept whatever truths come with it, and then discard the parts that don’t matter. One way isÂ to beÂ sure that you have the most healthy self-esteem you can have outside of your role as a creativeÂ person. IÂ marriedÂ a wonderful woman whom my every heartbeat pounds for. So, I don’t live every momentÂ to beÂ a composer or to even be a musician. It’s certainly a gigantic part of who I am at my core, but there are things and people in my life who are more important than any of this will ever be. I’m not alone in this feeling. Ask anyone who is creative and probably over the age of thirty. The secondary things in life, you realize over time, matter very little. Success takes on a whole new definition when love, children, family, and even the closest friends take root in your life as a middle-aged person. I spend a huge majority of my time trying to hip younger people who are ready to jump off the cliff of career suicide to this fact. When you get rejected, it’s notÂ a rejection ofÂ you as a person, it’s simply that the timing of your artistic expression and that of the source material don’t align. That’s it. It’s the wrinkle of cosmic scheduling. You could bring the same set of demo cues to the next meeting and have the biggest film in history on your hands. Nobody knows. But whatever the case, rejection should only hit your art, not your heart. It’s just not worth it and it never ever lasts anyway, so why devote the emotional bandwidth?

… if you don’t know how to read music.

Clearly, you will be at major and immediate disadvantage if you don’t read music and you are trying to break into the scene. Not only does this hamper your speed and agility as the point person for the music department on a production, but it will also hurt you as a potential candidate for an apprenticeship â€” the avenue by which a lot of film composers get their start. Like it or not, and argue all you want, but the film music business still thrives and depends on people who can read dots on a page. Period. Whether you are a musician at a session, an orchestrator sorting out lines for the composer, a copyist handling last-minute changes, or the composer yourself, you will need to read often. Written music comprehension is to a composer what the interpreting of blueprints is to an architect. Not knowing how to read the architectural drawings on the house you areÂ building will be an unmitigated disaster. Imagine being a non-reading composer on the scoring stage and having to quickly change a lead-line for your director, transpose it in your head, and rattle it off to your 1st chair trumpet or clarinetist. Can you imagine? Well, please do… because that exact scenario is not a rare experience for the in-the-trenches film composer.

Does not reading make you aÂ bad composer? Of course not. Even a few top-tier composers in the game started out just humming their melodies to an orchestrator. But before long, almost all of them learned to read and write at least functionally. Need to brush up? Take some private lessons; there are hundreds of teachers in LA, NYC, and London who will take you on as a private student and bring you up to functional and workable levels in no time. Don’t be embarrassed by your inability to read notes; not all composers who are working today are ace readers, either, and no matter how good you are, I promise you your musicians will always be ten times better anyway. But even still, having that very basic ability in your skill set will remove what is probably the biggest hurdle to self-confidence as a composer. It is your job as the captain of this ship to have a solid grasp of the fundamentals. This is as fundamental as it gets.

… if you don’t live in a music mecca.

There you are… in Delaware. Don’t worry. Geographical misfortune does not a bad composer make. There’s been a lot of talk in the last decade about geography and how it correlates to the level of sophistication of a composer, implying that the farther away you are from a major music center the less “dedicated” you must be to your craft. Obviously that’s bullshit. It doesn’t matter where you live, your talents are your talents. Now, how accessible your talents are to those who need them… well, that’s another matter entirely.

You’re not aÂ bad composer if you live in Cornwall. Still living with the folks in Texarkana? No problem. Never underestimate the powerful combo of a gigabit Internet connection and a fair amount of good old-fashioned hustle. You can accomplish a lot with an IMDbPro account, a well thought-out demo reel and a cell phone. But if you are determined to be in the thick of the film world, the brick and mortar studios are all still standing in Los Angeles. Film and TV seem to be the only industries who haven’t figured out that the world is flat like the video game industry has. They are making strides in that direction, but they aren’t there yet. Post-production on films and shows happens in LA.

… just because somebody says you are.

Human beings are strange creatures. We love to kick our wounded. I don’t know what it is, but people will love you when it’s easy to and hate you when it’s easier. There is nothing that will take the wind out of your sails more than negative feedback from colleagues. Complete strangers? Whatevs. But colleagues… That’s a tough knife in the gut. And there are those out there who are self-loathing enough to let you have it. Believe me. As I’ve just finished a very personal project, the barbs from the people I figured would lob them last have found their mark. And for a while, I internalized every one, taking it apart and searching for where I’d gone wrong.Â But then I realized that those people are not my audience. Do you make music for other musicians? I sure don’t. So what was I worried about?

Here’s the thing: The people who love what you do love it because it does something for them. It fills something in them. It cures what’s ailing, if only for five minutes. That is your audience. The fans. Not the freaks… the fans. The freaks are just freaking. They need something to freak about so that they don’t have to keep staring at their own work. There will be people who “get” what you do and there will be people who just don’t and never will. Just accept that those people aren’t the people you are doing this for and they likely never will be. Don’t read your comment feed. Never punch down. Even the biggest stars in entertainment get railed by the folks who are sitting at home doing nothing. If you happen to convert a non-believer through consistent output that ultimately changes their mind, awesome… You’re lucky. That doesn’t happen often. Take great care of them and don’t abuse their commitment to your art by following up with a piece of crap. But if you never do, remember who you are playing to: Your fans. The dedicated few. Your niche. Your tribe. Your supporters. The people who will join your mailing list, share your content in their social circles, attend your panel discussions, and email occasionally to tell you they “enjoyed this last one”. That is the person you are working for. That is where your mental bandwidth deserves allocation. Not thinking about how toÂ get new fans…. but thinking about how to care for the ones who are here already. That’s an endgame that matters.

According to Wikipedia the word “composer” comes from the Latin compÃ´nere, meaning “one who puts together”. In a sense, composing a piece of music is really about organizingâ€”organizing notes into themes and melodies, melodies into phrases, phrases into coherent sections, and so on.

The Classical forms like Rondo, Sonata, Compound Ternary, etc. all serve as extremely useful road-maps for developing musical material. The Rondo form, for example (ie. ABACA), gives us a blueprint for balancing a main theme with contrasting sections. After the main theme we have a contrasting section, and after every contrasting section we return to the main theme. Thus, the perfect sense of coherence and unity by repetitions of the main theme, balanced by variety and interest from the contrasting sections.

All of this is great to know when you’re sitting down to write a new composition to be performed in a concert hall. But how does this information stay relevant when scoring to picture? What do you do when your entire musical structure depends on the visual?

Picture is king. Even if you are struck with inspiration and would love nothing more than to have your main theme run off into a fugue, you’re not entirely in charge. If it doesn’t support the picture, it doesn’t make the score.

As you have probably already experienced either in your own scores or while watching other films, sometimes it doesn’t take much for the music to be dramatically effective. Even just a slowly thumping bass drum can be enough to support the picture in a compelling way. Where are the balanced sections in that cue? The audience isn’t particularly interested in how you transitioned from the B section back to the main theme, they’re interested in what’s happening on screen.

It would seem that perhaps the classical forms are backwards thinking, while a film cue is forward thinking. The structured forms are about developing the material that you’ve already heard; the contrasting B section isn’t a contrast if you haven’t yet heard A. But film music seems to be more about the present and future moments, building tension and preparing (or in some cases defying) expectations.

If there is still a place for form in film music, perhaps it’s only in those places where the audience is actually paying attention to the music. In the main titles, for example, or a montage that is mostly score driven.

So what do you think? Is musical form relevant when writing to picture? Are the traditional methods of organizing musical material still useful in the world of film scoring?

Well… Not so fast. TV is pretty different from what you might be used to.

 

#1) TV is not film.

Scoring for television is nothing like scoring for film. Not in the least bit. There are some similarities, but not many. First, the schedules on television shows are insane. The hours you have to put in are completely inhumane. A typical season might stretch from September to May, literally with no break, and your weekly schedule often doesn’t allow for any downtime during score production. #2) TV is not cheap.

Television is very transparent. Because of the shorter runtimes that TV shows naturally have to cater to, music is often looked at to carry more of the story than it would during a 90-minute film. In addition, HD and digital cable have upped the ante in regards to audio resolution, and now, even people with hearing issues can hear their favorite shows – and everything in them – better than they’ve ever have been able to before. After all, why would they have hired you in the first place if not for your musical opinion? The pressure is really on them, therefore everyone on their team must perform at an optimum level. There is no room for you to allow your feelings to be hurt over someone on the creative team demanding that you make a change or rewrite a cue to fit the scene differently than you had first interpreted. The time constraints often do not allow for any other communication style other than directness, and you must have the kind of thick skin that can tolerate that.

 

#4) TV is not for the uneducated.

This last point is two-fold. The first part is appropriate for everyone working in TV or film, but I’ll relate it to TV in this way: If you want to work in television, you have to know what works in television. If you have no idea what has come before you, how can you compete? However, in television, some things just do not work. Again, often because of the medium, if you think like a film composer while scoring a TV show, you are going to get yourself into some serious trouble at some point. Very often, motifs and themes that you would develop over time on a film will not work on a TV show because again… there isn’t enough time. If you aren’t a fan of TV or if you are one of those who hasn’t seen a dramatic television show since the 1960’s, forget it. You + TV writing = FAIL. Period. TV has changed over the years, and you have to keep up on trends, current sounds, dramatic chops, just like film or video games or any other medium that you might want to write for. Not everyone can cut the mustard in television. Many people try, and many people die. Be a fan, get on the bus, and start studying. There is no other way. Learn by immersion.

 

What’s the Point?

I want to end this by telling you that my experience with all this is highly subjective. I have never scored a TV show in my life. On the other hand, I have been on teams that have scored plenty. I’ve written additional music for TV and have had my music placed in dozens of primetime TV shows. I’ve seen it done over and over and over and over again as an assistant to several great TV writers. I’ve watched and I’ve learned so that when the time comes for me, I can have somewhat of an idea of what I should be doing. I know I’ll still be scared to death, but I will have the procedure down in a way that maybe others haven’t had the chance to observe. I hope that you get the same out of my little diatribe here.
