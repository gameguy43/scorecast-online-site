Composers Unionization: New Goals, New Focus

Posted by SCO Staff in News | 11 comments

Tuesday night, April 19, 2010, the Association of Media Composers and Lyricists’ (AMCL) presented their second public meeting concerning the effort to unionize composers and lyricists under the umbrella of the Teamsters Local 399.

Held in Beverly Hills at the Writers Guild Theater, the official proceedings began with a welcome message from AMCL spokesman Bruce Broughton, who outlined that the new focus of the AMCL unionization effort would be to secure “benefits only” for composers and lyricists—a message quite different from November’s initial meeting, where the committee announced that “wage minimums and working conditions” would also be included in the negotiations.

Speaking to SCOREcast just prior to the start of the meeting, Bruce Broughton told us, “I’m happy. This is a great turnout and we have a solid plan. I think we are ready now to really get something done. We finally know what we are doing.”

A statement was presented by actor and Screen Actors Guild representative, James Cromwell, indicating SAG’s commitment to support composer unionization. Writers Guild West executive director, David Young, also gave a statement from the WGA West in support of a composers union, vowing that “the WGA West will support you in every way we can”.

In the lobby, a 3×4 panel was on display with the names of 250 composers, lyricists, actors, producers, and directors indicating support of the union effort. Names on the list included composers Hans Zimmer, John Ottman, Marco Beltrami, Carter Burwell, and Thomas Newman, producers Steven Bochco, Aaron Sorkin, and Dick Wolf, and director Edward Zwick.

Several letters of support were read throughout the evening, including one from director/producer Ron Howard.

The AMCL’s recent move to fight for “benefits only” was indicated by many in attendance to be the tipping point for support, and the AMCL Organizing Committee stated several times that the their main focus would be to first protect “working” composers and lyricists in the film and television music industry.

From the dais, AMCL Organizing Committee member and composer Mike Post stated, “I will not be a part of a union that is lead by non-working people.”

The second part of the meeting was opened up to members of the audience to ask questions and gain clarification on several key points raised by the AMCL Committee throughout the evening.

As of yet, no further meetings have been announced by the AMCL, but we will continue to keep you informed of further information here at SCOREcastOnline.com.

  • marknortham

    It is a huge, embarrassing backpedal.

    What is curious to me is that only 6 weeks ago, the WGA Executive Board approved a request that had been negotiated for weeks by the AMCL folks that had the WGA taking a stand against producers asking for “free music” as part of a spec-off or bake-off competition, a tactic that is becoming more common especially during pilot season.

    Why on earth would the AMCL, having just worked very hard to secure the WGA's official support, suddenly backpedal away from this letter as fast as they can? And what does it leave the WGA thinking of composers – a group that apparently is not even ready to take a position against working for free EVEN WITH official support from a brother union (the WGA)?

    They have 200 signatures for a “benefits only” union? They could have 2,000 signatures for a “no free music” union.

    Sure, you have to start somewhere, but let's not be so afraid of speaking up for the value of our music that we dumb down the union before it even gets out of the starting gate. Benefits only negotiations with the producers? Great – a good starting point. But the WGA letter shows that meaningful deals can be struck with other unions as well – unions like music editors who may have considerable influence in the composer hiring process.

    And as far as health insurance goes, who can say what that landscape will look like in 12 or 24 months given the recent legislation?

    No free music. It's a no-brainer, and the AMCL should move forward aggressively to establish deals with other unions like the WGA that begin to reverse the decades of declining composer workplace conditions that have resulted in the destructive situation we have today where score music and those who write it are not treated with a lot of respect or value by many in Hollywood. But if we are to improve things, we must lead the way in establishing reasonable working conditions for composers. Well-established guys like Mike Post are not going to benefit from this union a great deal, but the next generation of composers and today's up and coming composers will. We must make sure that the people who will benefit from a union the most are not overlooked in the process of determining what this union will be and how it will work.

  • http://deaneogden.com Deane Ogden

    I think you are right, just premature. There are only a few ways to get a union off the ground when the industry that it caters to is not exactly “booming” with discretionary profit.

    I think in the future, some of the things you outline will be addressed, IF they are even addressable then. We cannot really compare anything we do as film and television composers, at least in this regard, to what game composers do simply because the time-frames on the projects differ so vastly from one another. It is easier, given the slower rate of scheduled deadlines (read mileposts) during the course of a video game project, to really keep track of and/or determine what constitues writing “a minute of music”. In film and TV, fixes here and there are simply part of the package fee that is negotiated as part of your contract, and anything that falls outside the scope of those terms is either re-negotiated at that time or, preferably, negotiated upfront as a possible foreseeable circumstance.

    All this assuming, of course, you are either a whiz-kid negotiator or you have solid representation. I don't see someone without either of those things in place coming out on top of this, and a union will never provide either for you… which happens to be another *huge* misconception of what unions can and cannot provide.

  • http://deaneogden.com Deane Ogden

    Hey Jesse—
    You bring up an interesting point, and one that I feel they covered pretty well at the meeting. I think we can all agree that health insurance and pension benefits are items of common ground by which all composers/lyricists can basically agree upon. Wage minimums, as well as “working conditions” (whatever that means to a person working from home in a converted guest house or walk-in closet!), are sort of non-starters to this conversation, and have been bones of contention for many people since the AMCL went “public” late last year.

    I've heard the Elfman interview that you are referring to, and I have felt his pain on countless occasions in many of the same situations. However, “redoing everything for the same price” to a composer is like re-roofing a house for a general contractor—not only, if even by tradition, is it part and parcel with the job, but it also is something that is literally immeasurable and/or regulative by any practical means.

    That being said, this all might be beside the point. I don't feel that Broughton, or anyone else, for that matter, needs to “backpedal” on anything. Everyone on the committee was clear that this is a new focus that everyone can agree needs to happen—including the studios. Concentrating on things that even the studios think composers should enjoy like everyone else, is more of a sure-fire way to unionize rather than concentrating on things that can't even be agreed upon by your own constituency.
