June 2010 – The Month of Anarchy

Posted by Deane Ogden on Monday, June 7, 2010 · 18 Comments 

My assistant and I had a great discussion over an 11pm Taco Bell dinner last night (isn’t composing for Hollywood films just the most glamorous thing ever?). Our topic? Rules. We were talking, specifically, about the boxes we paint ourselves into as composers—those ways of doing that are now go-to methods for us. You know… methods and devices that have worked project after project, over and over, again and again.

At first, our conversation was about identifying these rules. Certain progressions that are used as devices for a particular filmic motion. Certain instrument choices that we might prefer over others. Particular ways of writing or orchestrating that we subconsciously move straight to without regard to how we might approach it differently this time around.

Next, we questioned, “Why do we enforce these rules on ourselves?” Is it laziness? Habit? Insecurity? Maybe it is based on historical success. Or maybe a rule still lingers from our educational background. What we were taught in music school or by our mentors; a childhood music teacher, perhaps.

Finally, we decided, “Screw the rules. Who needs them!” There are enough things in place that cannot be negotiated in this gig… why create more rigidity in the routine? Most rules only serve to hinder creativity and don’t provide anything valuable to the process anyway.

So… let’s practice it here first. Typically, we focus on a monthly theme at SCOREcastOnline.com, and our contributing editors follow it pretty well, always giving their own unique perspectives on the topic as professionals in the film music community. This month… it’s a free-for-all! No restrictions. No singular focus. And most importantly… NO RULES.

I’ll take the lead tomorrow morning. I have no idea what I’ll talk about, but it will be some lesson I’ve learned this last month/year/decade about this gig, I can assure you that! Hopefully it will be beneficial to most of you; insightful if I’m lucky!

Most importantly, why not lend YOUR voice to the chorus? Click HERE and send us an article on anything you think would benefit the professional film music community as a whole. Articles are easy to upload from that page, and there is even a place to upload your bio and photo for us to post under what you write. It can be on anything… writing, orchestrating, business, networking, copying, editing, technical issues, what libraries are better than others, budgets, gear, fundamentals, organization, productivity, the union, workflow… whatever you want.

Are you telling yourself that you “don’t have time”? You are “too busy working”?

Yeah… me too. But isn’t that just another rule?

Maybe the most important “rule” of  being your own boss it that YOU get to decide what the rules are.

Hmmm…

  • http://www.adrianelliscomposer.com/AdriansBlog Adrian Ellis

    I think both your, Ogden, and Bellis' points line up.

    During the crunch, use the rules/parameters to create quality work, efficiently.

    During downtime, challenge yourself creatively. Think of new ways of working, moving ahead, progressing, pushing against your comfort zone.

    Both of these can be filtered through 'your voice'. If I may be permitted to use an analogy from martial arts training – during times of extreme stress, you will default to what you trained yourself to do. If were lazy and kept your hands low – that's how you'll defend, and you'll get punched in the face. When the pressure is off, we need to be vigilant and examine our methods and practice – something we tend not to do by nature – instead we relax and turn our brains off :)

  • http://www.adrianelliscomposer.com/AdriansBlog Adrian Ellis

    One thing I'd like to add, riding on Richard Bellis' comment on “creativity coming when we are not in panic mode”.

    Yes, in times of stress you default to 'what you know and have trained for”. What works works, and that's often dictated by historical success.

    There is also the client pressure, that is scared to try new things (they have the gig, the pressure is on, they are not in 'creative mode'). So the common argument is, “they want safe, they want tried and true, they want the cliches that audiences respond to”.

    Here are my humble suggestions for us all to contribute to a high standard in film music, and one that is evolving and progressing:

    1. When not under grueling deadline pressure, think – in what musical way do I think I can raise the bar? Am I being lazy? Am I defaulting to modes of working that are based on cliches and trends? Am I consciously developing my artistic voice? Set some goals. What are you truly passionate about? What do you want to hear in music? It doesn't have to be the craziest, most original thing ever. Just something – your voice, a new direction, a new sound.

    2. Create relationships with filmmakers outside of grueling pressures and deadlines. Talk with them, get them excited, express your passion, make them feel a commitment to raising the bar because it will raise the value of their work.

    3. When we feel pressure to do 'trendy cliched' stuff that 'works', we have to be professional and deliver. However, we can subtly work our ideas in, slowly but surely. Be artistically sneaky! We are always 'selling' – our cues and our value as composers. So, sell your passion! It doesn't have to be outrageous. Just a few elements that raise your game, that make you feel like you made a small difference, that you can be proud of – whatever that means to you. If you can succeed at that, then we are all moving towards something positive, something win-win, where filmmakers, composers, and the audience benefits from your resistance to doing only what's safe.
