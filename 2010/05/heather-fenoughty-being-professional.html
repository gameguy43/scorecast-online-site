Heather Fenoughty: Being Professional

May 25th, 2010 | Category: Articles, Business, Tips

professional [prəˈfɛʃənəl]

3. extremely competent in a job, etc.

A pro is someone who gets paid for what they do, pure and simple.

Compare this with the opposite, the definition of an amateur, who does the same thing only for the love of it (i.e. for free).

As soon as you get paid, you can legitimately call yourself a professional. A ‘Pro’.

But, ultimately, only by being professional can you get to this point and stay there.

A roundup of the qualities of the Pro Film Music Composer (by no means exhaustive!) follows…

1. It’s business, not a hobby
If you’re in this business as a pro, then you’re a businessman/woman. That means working regularly, persistently on not only your music but on your business.

Get your affairs in order: tax, insurance, working environment, legal requirements. You’re in this game to sell your music, to get paid for composing.

2. Be an expert
Educate yourself in composition, musical styles, and methods of collaboration and delivery.

Keep your knowledge up-to-date with new technological innovations, musical trends, media language, etc. Have an informed opinion.

Note: this does not mean jumping on the bandwagon/keeping-up-with-the-Jones’ by buying every new piece of technology that comes out. This does mean knowing what’s new, what it can do, and what it may or may not be able to do for you now and in the future.

3. Be objective
You’re creating raw materials for someone else to manipulate, not a completely finished, final product. Don’t get precious.

Accept criticism graciously. It’s not personal; even when it seems like it is.

4. Pro-actively research and create opportunities
Use downtime between gigs to update your showreel, website and promotional materials — you never know when you’ll get asked for them.

Seek out new contacts and build relationships; plan ahead, refine your business systems, identify potential new opportunities in the making, and then go after them.

5. Invest in your business
Invest time in your education, your experience, and in building relationships with potential clients.

Invest money in equipment — the right tool for the right job — and, again, in your education, both in your skills and in running a business.

Invest energy: this isn’t a job you can just fall into. It takes focus, motivation, persistence and courage. Get on and do it already.

Invest in your team and build support networks for streams of advice and leads on sources of work, and identify potential assistants and colleagues that can bring complementary skills when you might need them. Build your team in advance to save essential writing time when that call finally does come through.

6. Take responsibility

  • Be responsible for your business; you know you’ve no-one to hold accountable but yourself.
  • Act professionally: deliver on time; be courteous, civilised, and amiable to work with, yet focused; be prepared for any and all contingencies you reasonably can be; learn from past mistakes and plan for future successes.

So whilst being paid for a gig defines you in the moment as a ‘Pro’, being a Pro will more importantly get you to that point and give you the best shot at staying Pro!

Based in the North of England in the UK, HEATHER FENOUGHTY is an award-winning freelance composer and sound designer. She has scored several feature films and documentaries for the BBC and ITV, and her credits also include nearly 100 short films including a BAFTA-nominated drama (Nits, 2004). She has composed music and sound designed corporate advertisements for Nissan and other multi-national companies, with clients based all around the world. She also creates soundscapes for cutting-edge contemporary theatre, and her scores have played on the West End and off Broadway.
