Alain Mayrand: Are You Any Good?

by Alain Mayrand · 3 Comments · in Composition

What is the secret to success as a film composer? We all talk about networking, internet promotion, social networks, building your image, people skills, getting press, websites, gear, DAWs, sample libraries—all of these are important, but dependent on one thing: the quality and uniqueness of your work. If your music is mediocre, if it sounds like every other bloke out there with a drive full of VSTs, then all of your marketing efforts won’t matter much.

As you know, for better and for worse, the barrier to entry into film scoring has been lowered. With a few pieces of software you now have all the tools to create a complete film score. The downside is that people no longer need to know how to read or write music, be proficient at their instrument, or have a true knowledge of orchestration. Is that bad? Not in itself, but the result is that the film score landscape has become so homogeneous that an agent recently told me producers can now identify Storm Drum just by hearing it on the demos they are sent!

That’s potentially good for you. This means you have a chance to separate yourself by working harder at your craft and being unique. Don’t just rehash what others have done, it’s not a good career move. Being unique takes hard work and is not an accident. But how do you know if you are good? Can you judge for yourself? To answer this question, I present to you …drum roll please… the Dunning-Kruger effect, a report that states that:

“…an unskilled person makes poor decisions and reaches erroneous conclusions, but their incompetence denies them the meta-cognitive ability to realize their mistakes. The unskilled therefore suffer from illusory superiority, rating their own ability as above average, much higher than it actually is, while the highly skilled underrate their abilities, suffering from illusory inferiority. This leads to the situation in which less competent people rate their own ability higher than more competent people.”

But someone who is in this position can be made to “recognize and acknowledge their own previous lack of skill, if they can be trained to substantially improve.”

In other words: If you think you are great, then you probably aren’t and don’t even have the tools to recognize excellence. If you are plagued with self-doubt, then you are probably doing okay.

I am sure that this brings to mind at least one person to each of you, or perhaps more!

The irony, though, is that the person who suffers from the Dunning-Kruger effect is blissfully unaware of his own shortcomings. So who’s to say it’s not you who is clueless? Based on this study, there is only one solution, one that I completely agree with: reach a high level of musical study. (And no, elementary harmony and voice-leading does not count!) A high level of music study will give you the tools to recognize and reach excellence, to have music that stands above the sea of DAW-Warriors. As a result your music will stand apart. Producers and directors will notice you—the quality of your work will make you an asset to them and they will proudly recommend you to others, because in this business, recommendations and word of mouth are the most potent marketing tools.

Do you think you suffer from the Dunning-Kruger effect?

About The Author

Alain Mayrand

Alain Mayrand is a trained composer, conductor, pianist and guitarist. His symphonic score for the Jackie Chan animated feature The Legend of Silk Boy was released on the Movie Score Media label and has been called “...one the of the year’s most delightful scores”. His film and concert scores have been performed and recorded by The Russian State Film Orchestra, Taiwan's Evergreen Orchestra, the Ramat Gan Orchestra in Israel, the Erie Saxophone Quartet and many more. Alain is composer-in-residence for the Vancouver Metropolitan Orchestra who premiered his piece “Resisting Euphoria” last September. For more information on Alain's career and to listen to his music, visit his official website and his blog at gettingthescore.com.

  • http://www.adrianelliscomposer.com/AdriansBlog Adrian Ellis

    It’s an interesting point.
    “they don’t have the tools to tell brilliance apart from crap either. ”
    Well, that may or may not be true, but it’s also incredibly subjective.
    I agree with Alain that education, and pushing yourself to excel is incredibly important, but not because you will necessarily write ‘better’ music or be more appreciated. Look at what Carpenter did with Halloween. It’s neither the most sophisticated piece of writing and I don’t believe he is the most educated musician – but there’s no arguing that that music just WORKS.

    The fact that you are always improving, learning, and striving is, I believe, what truly matters. You cannot ever know all there is, especially about orchestration and harmony. Just a quick survey of your peers at work at an orchestral recording and masterclass should be enough to put things in perspective. We had one just this week (and were lucky enough to have the incredible Andrew Lockington serve as guest clinician) and it was humbling for everyone.

    But that’s the attitude, not the technical prowess of the music. Indeed, most of the time, when writing to picture, we are writing very simple stuff – by necessity.

    On the other hand, there is something to be said about confidence in your abilities – knowing you can get the job done and instilling trust in your clients. In the end, it really doesn’t matter a damn if you think you are good or not – it comes out in the wash. I think the only way to test yourself is to persistently put your work in the line of fire of trusted advisors and be completely open to their critiques. Sometimes, we sell ourselves short, and could, in fact, be moving forward much more quickly if we stopped beating on ourselves for not reaching an imagined ‘perfection’.

  • http://twitter.com/FilmCompos3r Greg Nicolett

    While I loved being introduced to the formal theory DK theory (an idea I had been dancing around in my own head) I feel that the conclusion of “reach a high level of musical study” is unfortunately erroneous. In pop culture there is no agreed upon standard of excellence. Infact, even in academic music, there are many schools of thought on what music is “good” music.

    I hear a lot of score’s and say to myself, “Yes, I could probably do that.” I feel this way with A-List films. This realization gives me confidence that I am at least near the technical level needed to achieve success. But I am perhaps rather cynical in that I believe the bar for success itself is rather low, from a MUSICAL standpoint, yet rather high in the areas of luck, collaborative spirit, longevity, and social connections.

    Hans Zimmer get’s a lot of flack as a composer for the simplicity of his material. I remember in an interview John Burlingame asked him “Why do so many film makers invite you back?” to which he answered something like “not because of my music, but because of my ability to communicate with them.”

    Elfman can’t read bass clef. Carter Burwell majored in Philosophy. Clint Mansell had never written for strings before writing for Kronos. In each of their cases, a high level of musical study would likely have stunted their individual voices rather than cultivated them.
