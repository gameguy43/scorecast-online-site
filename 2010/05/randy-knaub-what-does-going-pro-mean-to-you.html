Randy Knaub: What Does “Going Pro” Mean to You?

May 20, 2010 Written by Randy Knaub Business

As I thought about this month’s topic on going pro and reading Deane’s article, I began thinking; is being a pro a state of mind, a skill/ability, or even both?
Therefore, this month’s article will not address technology per se, but more of a list of questions that I not only ask myself, but I ask them of you.

What if you are a procrastinator? Deane mentions in his article that you may have a hard time going pro if you procrastinate. Does this eliminate you from making it? We all can change, but what if you can’t?

Is it a degree in music that makes you a pro? What about gear? Is there a certain type of gear you need to have to be a pro? A studio outside of your house, perhaps? Making a full-time living at music or composing? What does it take?

What about your personality type? Are you an Introvert? An Extrovert? Does this matter, even if you can write the best music ever?

I agree that you need drive, determination and definitely being proactive helps. However, what if you are not an A type personality, but you are a great composer and just lack people skills? Does this mean you will fail?

My goal with this article is to spark a response from you, and tell me what you think about yourself that makes you a pro. Does the high-level personality or demeanor affect you? What in your mind makes you a pro?

I believe that being a pro is what you think it means to you. You may not be happy until you have an Oscar on your mantle, or maybe three Grammys for Best Song. You may have high goals, which are good and we all need to have them. However, maybe you are content with just writing jingles, and that you can pay the bills.

Being a pro means a couple of things for myself:

  • Making a full-time living at music, composing, recording, etc.
  • Making a difference with the gifts and skills I posses

This means that I am able to make a full-time living at the thing I love while contributing something of meaning. Whether that is a piece of music that moves someone emotionally, a recording that sounds great, or a mix that “rocks the house”. It could also be a piece of music that makes a picture speak. Having someone appreciate the work is also important. This could be one person, or a thousand.

These things sound lofty at the onset, like saying, “I am wishing for world peace.” Sure, you have to have skills, talents, and abilities at the low levels, but this is what I am getting at. Do you immediately get into the weeds with things like, “I need a Steinway piano,” or, “I need the latest string library,” versus, “I need to focus on organization and priorities?”

I happen to have spent many years in sales. Talking, public speaking and relating to people comes easy for me. I do not have a problem being on stage in front of five thousand people. I can start a conversation in a room full of strangers, but does this help me be any better at becoming a pro? I think it does, but how about you?

Now I want to use all of my training, people skills, education, gear, sample libraries, mic preamps, etc. … to go pro. I want people to want to work with me because I give them what they need at all levels: gear, experience, relationships, knowledge, and friendship.

Most of us understand that we got into this business because we love it, and it is the passion that keeps us coming back, even though the pay could be bad and the hours terrible. It is truly an art form that we all share in common.

Tell us SCOREcasters what makes you a pro, and at what level.

Look forward to the comments!

RANDY KNAUB is an audio engineer, producer, songwriter, keyboardist, and music technologist. He composes music and sound bites at his northern California studio, and works with local artists recording demos and CDs as well as pursuing corporate multimedia clients. Randy also consults for and beta tests for numerous music software and hardware manufacturers. His clients range from Martin Marietta, McDonalds, The United Way, Colorado Lottery, Visual Communications Group, and Jeppesen. You can find his SCOREcast bio (and links to his other sites) here.

    Nice article Randy, thank you.
    Initially when I read the title for Deane's article, ” Going Pro” it immediately drew images of composers in Hollywood, LA and NY who are extremely busy, well compensated for their work and composing brilliant scores, which I believe is true, they are Pro. But after reading his article I calmed down and realized that ” going pro” can be based on exactly what I am striving for.

    What now comes to my mind for “going pro” I think of a film composer with these qualities , efficiency, flexibility, hard worker, disciplined, organized, friendly and personable, devoted to their creativity in music, but not so attached to their music that they are unable to change for the better of a film, someone who has talent and loves what they do.These are what I think makes someone professional and good at film scoring.
