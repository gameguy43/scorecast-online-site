Lee Sanders: Funny Looks and Spotting Jujitsu

March 5, 2010 Written by Lee Sanders Process

People look at me funny all the time (I got that out of the way for you, just to save space in the comments!), but never more so than when I express my general philosophy about spotting films: I want to write the least amount of music possible for the project.

Nowadays our up-front music budget seems to shrink with every new gig that hits our desk… so why would I take a wacky position like this? Why deny myself the (sometimes) glorious royalties that come from a wall-to-wall score? And doesn’t “writing as little music as possible” sound, well, kinda of lazy?

My rationale, and more… after the jump.

It’s simple, really: If my default position is not to score a scene, then including music becomes a conscious choice. In other words, I prefer to build in a decision point, right from the spotting session, in which there must be a argument for music.

Working this way means the music has a better chance of being target-specific, rather than ubiquitous. It has a better chance of heightening the experience by its presence (and absence!), rather than simply serving as what it’s often called: sonic wallpaper.

I say “has a better chance,” because working this way usually leads to conflict with filmmakers. You might be surprised how many filmmakers walk into a spotting sessions with the assumption that everything will be scored.

Once, at a spotting session several years ago, I was even flat-out told: “Just write music for the entire picture, and we’ll throw out all the cues that aren’t working.” Which was both refreshingly honest and casually insulting. When I launched into my whole “concept of spotting” spiel… you guessed it. Funny looks.

Maybe I was being “too meta” for them, talking about a philosophical framework… in fact, I can guarantee it. It’s an understandable thing to want to do, but it’s a telltale sign of inexperience. A rookie mistake.

Here’s why:
Opening the door of talking about process can be counterproductive, even dangerous. Never more so than at a spotting session. It can erode the filmmakers’ confidence in you. It can lead to blanket statements like “I want you to score everything,” out of a desire to cut to the chase. “Process” conversations, I’ve come to believe, are best left to cocktail hour… long after the gig is finished. They’re abstract at a moment when you ought to be dealing in the concrete; they’re speculative and introspective at a moment when you need to be definitive and self-assured. You’re being hired for your opinion, not for out-loud musings about how you reach that opinion.

A spotting session, as filmmaker meetings go, is a long one: it’s at least the length of the picture (…right? Don’t tell me you allow the filmmakers to “skip to the next part where we’re gonna have music”!). Keeping things focused, setting a brisk pace, is an important precedent for your subsequent playback meetings.

All of which is why I’ve adopted a “just (don’t) do it” approach to spotting, in which I simply remain silent until I feel music is required. If, before that moment, the filmmaker asks “…we’re gonna have music here, right?,” I can reply with a question: “Why?”

This little verbal jujitsu move, if done in a friendly, genuinely-curious way, leads into the discussion I want to be having, within the frame in which I want to place it. And if the filmmaker offers up some obviously-just-concocted justification, it doesn’t matter: you’re still having the conversation. You’re still taking part in the creative collaboration. Which means you’re in a position to talk it over. And maybe, just maybe, change your own opinion. None of this should be construed as closed-mindedness.

The spotting process is under fire, in my view, and not just from people like the score-happy filmmaker I mentioned above. Projects for which a music library is used—which, I don’t have to tell you, are more and more prevalent—don’t even have spotting sessions. It’s usually assumed those projects will be wall-to-wall.

On more traditional projects, temp tracks often fall into place long before a composer is set. The spotting decisions made as the temp is assembled become a kind of default unto themselves. If we composers disagree, we have to fight harder than ever even to reach a place where we’re deemed worthy of being allowed to voice an opinion on the subject. It’s a moment in the creative process that, frankly, is in very real danger of falling out of our jurisdiction.

That’s a shame—it results in a shallower collaboration between filmmaker and composer, and in my opinion, a weaker end product. If I’m completely against the idea of placing music in a scene in the first place, how inspired am I really going to be when it comes time to score it? Besides, I’ve spent my whole career honing a dramatic sensitivity… a huge part of which is knowing how and when to enter and exit a scene. It’s part of my identity as a (musical) storyteller. It’s something I bring to the party. I think that deserves a hearing, and I encourage you to be similarly confident and persistent in your own spotting sessions.

And if you still get funny looks from your filmmaking collaborators? Ignore ‘em. If you’re right, they’ll come around.

I want to hear some spotting session success stories… and nightmares… from people this month! The more we all share, the better prepared we are when we walk into our own sessions. So bring it on. The crazier the better. Winner gets respect.
