Making Money from your Music: Part 1

By Emmett Cooke on October 19, 2009

I’m about to embark on an experiment of sorts, and I’d like to share my progress with you as I go. We all know how tough it can be sometimes to make enough money from composing – in particular when starting off. Some of us have a part time job while starting off, some have a full time job – others have no side jobs – just composing.

So… how can we make more money from our music, even after it has been used for its original purpose of a film score, etc.? We sometimes forget that the money making process doesn’t always have to stop there – we can keep licensing our music (depending on the original license we signed with the director/creator) after the original project. It’s not all about the money, but let’s not fool ourselves – we all need money to survive, and the more the merrier. 🙂

I currently sell some of my music on stock music libraries and make between $200-$350 per month – which is nice pocket money for paying the bills, or buying whatever. However, that money is generally only 50% or less, of the net profit that my music makes – the websites I sell on can take from 50% – 75% of the money my music earns, which can often be quite disheartening.

Therefore, I looked into ways to make 100% profit on selling my music – then it hit me – I sell a collection of about 12 piano tracks on a website about 4 or 5 times a month, and it sells for $100 a pop (of which I earn 50%). So…if I could sell a CD for $100, why not sell it on my own website for $100 for licensing purposes or $20 for personal listening, and earn all of the profit? So I contacted some CD duplication companies to see how much it would cost to get 50 CDs created, and started looking at the sums:

Expenditure:
50 CDs = $283 (CDs include colour artwork printed on CD, jewelcases, album cover and back)
Delivery = $24
__________
Total = $307

If we then take a look at the total possible income, the money starts to add up nicely. Lets say I sell 30 CDs for personal listening (I’m sure I could find enough friends and family to buy them! LOL) and then 20 CDs for licensing purposes.

30 x $20 = $600
20 x $100 = $2000
_____________
Net Total = $2600
______________________________
Gross Total Possible Income= $2293
_____________________________

OK – so the above sums may be wishful thinking, but it certainly looks like it could be a profitable venture, so why not try? My CDs are being made this week and should be ready in a few days time. I’ll share my progress with you as I go – I think it will be a worthwhile experiment.

The next questions is – how do I sell them/market them? That’s where things get interesting… I want to make 100% of the profit so I won’t be using services such as CD Baby to sell my CDs for me – at least for now. I’ll be trying a few different marketing ideas and I’ll let you know which work, and which don’t.

In Part 2 of this article (coming soon), I’ll show you how to license music on your website, rather than relying on other sites to do it for you.

Have you looked into CD Duplication services in your area, and have you tried selling CDs of your music? How do they stack up against services like DiscMakers or Oasis? How did you get on? Let me know in the comments!

Emmett Cooke

Emmett Cooke is a professional 'honours ba mus' composer and technologist. His projects have spanned the realms of video games, film, television, library music, and trailers. Emmett hails from Waterford, Ireland and writes music from his personal studio there.
