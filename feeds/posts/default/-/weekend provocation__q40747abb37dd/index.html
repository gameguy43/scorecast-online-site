philosophy about spotting films: I want to write the \u003ci\u003eleast amount of music possible\u003c/i\u003e for the project.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eNowadays our up-front music budget seems Why deny myself the (sometimes) glorious royalties that come from a wall-to-wall score? And doesn't \"writing as little music as possible\" sound, well, kinda of \u003ci\u003elazy\u003c/i\u003e?\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eMy rationale, and more… after the jump.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003ca name\u003d'more'\u003e\u003c/a\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIt's simple, really: If my default position is \u003ci\u003enot\u003c/i\u003e to score a scene, then including music becomes a conscious choice. In other words, I prefer to build in a decision point, right from the spotting session, in which there must be a argument for music.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eWorking this way means the music has a better chance of being target-specific, rather than ubiquitous. It has a better chance of heightening the experience by its presence (and absence!), rather than simply serving as what it's often called: sonic wallpaper.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI say \"has a better chance,\" because working this way usually leads to conflict with filmmakers. You might be surprised how many filmmakers walk into a spotting sessions with the assumption that everything will be scored.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eOnce, at a spotting session several years ago, I was even flat-out told: \"Just write music for the entire picture, and we'll throw out all the cues that aren't working.\"\u0026nbsp;Which was both refreshingly honest and casually insulting. When I launched into my whole \"concept of spotting\" spiel… you guessed it. Funny looks.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eMaybe I was being \"too meta\" for them, talking about a philosophical framework… in fact, I can guarantee it. It's an understandable thing to want to do, but it's a telltale sign of inexperience. A rookie mistake.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eHere's why:\u003cbr /\u003eOpening the door of talking about process can be counterproductive, even dangerous. Never more so than at a spotting session. It can erode the filmmakers' confidence in you. It can lead to blanket statements like \"I want you to score everything,\" out of a desire to cut to the chase. \"Process\" conversations, I've come to believe, are best left to cocktail hour… long after the gig is finished. They're abstract at a moment when you ought to be dealing in the concrete; they're speculative and introspective at a moment when you need to be definitive and self-assured. You're being hired for your opinion, not for out-loud musings about how you reach that opinion.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eA spotting session, as filmmaker meetings go, is a long one: it's at least the length of the picture (…right? Don't tell me you allow the filmmakers to \"skip to the next part where we're gonna have music\"!). Keeping things focused, setting a brisk pace, is an important precedent for your subsequent playback meetings.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eAll of which is why I've adopted a \"just (don't) do it\" approach to spotting, in which I simply remain silent until I feel music is required. If, before that moment, the filmmaker asks \"…we're gonna have music here, right?,\" I can reply with a question: \"Why?\"\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThis little verbal jujitsu move, if done in a friendly, genuinely-curious way, leads into the discussion I want to be having, within the frame in which I want to place it. And if the filmmaker offers up some obviously-just-concocted justification, it doesn't matter: you're still having the conversation. You're still taking part in the creative collaboration. Which means you're in a position to talk it over. And maybe, just maybe, change your own opinion. None of this should be construed as closed-mindedness.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThe spotting process is under fire, in my view, and not just from people like the score-happy filmmaker I mentioned above. Projects for which a music library is used—which, I don't have to tell you, are more and more prevalent—don't even \u003ci\u003ehave\u003c/i\u003e spotting sessions. It's usually assumed those projects will be wall-to-wall.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eOn more traditional projects, temp tracks often fall into place long before a composer is set. The spotting decisions made as the temp is assembled become a kind of default unto themselves. If we composers disagree, we have to fight harder than ever even to reach a place where we're deemed worthy of being allowed to voice an opinion on the subject. It's a moment in the creative process that, frankly, is in very real danger of falling out of our jurisdiction.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThat's a shame—it results in a shallower collaboration between filmmaker and composer, and in my opinion, a weaker end product. If I'm completely against the idea of placing music in a scene in the first place, how inspired am I really going to be when it comes time to score it? Besides, I've spent my whole career honing a dramatic sensitivity… a huge part of which is knowing how and when to enter and exit a scene. It's part of my identity as a (musical) storyteller. It's something I bring to the party. I think that deserves a hearing, and I encourage you to be similarly confident and persistent in your own spotting sessions.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eAnd if you \u003ci\u003estill\u003c/i\u003e get funny looks from your filmmaking collaborators? Ignore 'em. If you're right, they'll come around.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI want to hear some spotting session success stories… and nightmares… from people this month! The more we all share, the better prepared we are when we walk into our own sessions. So bring it on. The crazier the better. It's a turn-off. You're making me feel like I'm just a sub-boss monster in the video game of your life. And you're gonna blast me with your spam cannon and get onto the next level. I'm not that easy… and neither are most filmmakers.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI like meeting new people, especially music people, and even more especially music people who are \u003ci\u003eon fire\u003c/i\u003e to get their careers going. But a little unclenching in this particular department works wonders. Building relationships takes time… at its best, it happens organically. And trying to force those relationships—looking at them too much like they're something to be shooting your way through, rather than nurturing and enjoying—is, to my way of thinking, over-competitive.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eNext up: gear.\u003c/b\u003e We've talked about GAS (Gear Acquisition Syndrome) around here before, and I freely admit to succumbing from time to time. What I sometimes find, though, is film music pros snapping up a whole new array of toys merely out of a desire to race in the Gear Derby. Again, it's competing in the wrong event.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI'm not suggesting that it's not important to stay up on the latest stuff; it definitely is. But it's a means to an end. And there's a pacing to the thing: it ought to be at a level commensurate with the gigs you're actually getting (and if you simply \u003ci\u003emust\u003c/i\u003e compare and compete, do so with those peers who are at your own level).\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI'd love to have a brand spanking new Euphonix or SSL console; who wouldn't? But I don't need it. It's way out of profile with where I am in my career. And the fact that (insert A-lister here) just bought one, or five, or whatever, shouldn't really bear on my decision. If I let it, I'm allowing my urge to compete over my head put the rest of my finances in jeopardy. And that's dumb. I don't need any more machinery to legitimize what I do.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eMy take on this: put the blinders on, run your own race, and unclench. When you need gear, get the best you can afford and no more. (it's enough to have to spend just doing that!)\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eLast thought for now: \u003c/b\u003eThere are all sorts of aspects of this biz in which thinking of the struggle as a competition can be healthy. It gives us definitive, often-humbling feedback about how we're progressing.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eBut competing over who can go the longest without sleeping, or who has neglected their family and friends the longest, or even who can write the craziest number of minutes of music in a day? Somewhere in there is that oft-mentioned line between hard-ass and dumb-ass. Tread carefully. Run the right race. Don't let your hardcore composer status become an end unto itself.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eLurkers—here's a great chance to pitch in your own thoughts. This career comes with high risk and \u003ci\u003ezero\u003c/i\u003e promise of reward. So the fact that you're interested in composing at all is a pretty reliable sign that you're aware of the gamble. People who are risk-takers generally enjoy tough competition, as well. Generally… but not always.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eRisk and competition are different. Competition implies effort. A lottery ticket is a risk, not a competition—there's nothing you can do, besides buying more tickets, to improve your chances of hitting the right combination of numbers. Whereas, if you fail to land a gig, there's always a nagging guilt, or doubt, that remains: What could I be doing better? Why didn't they like me? Why do I have to do this 'schmoozing and hustling' part—why can't writing incredible be enough?\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eToday's column is all about these \u003ci\u003einner-game \u003c/i\u003easpects of the career. It's all about improving what's between our ears, rather than what's coming out of our speakers. It's a subject I want to tackle even more here on SCOREcast, if you're interested. So let me know.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eFor now, here's an orientation: some basic techniques for getting through the competitive aspects of the biz, even (especially) if you find them distasteful.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eFirst, \u003cb\u003ediscipline\u003c/b\u003e yourself to a positive attitude. Being dejected and cynical is easy. Finding reasons to stay in the game, and to keep a smile on your face as you do it, is much tougher. It is, in fact, a job unto itself. It requires discipline—one of the great unsung commodities of the age we're in. I think discipline is a finite quantity, with each of us possessing a certain intrinsic maximum capacity that is expressed to a greater or lesser extent depending on how we prioritize its usage and constant development. Just like building muscle at the gym, in other words.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIt's only a theory, but it seems to line up with my experience, and I definitely feel the burn when I've had to force a little more discipline onto \u003ci\u003emyself\u003c/i\u003e from time to time (usually around deadlines!).\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eSecond, \u003cb\u003eexpose\u003c/b\u003e yourself to competitive experiences more frequently… and not just in film music. Desensitize yourself through increased exposure, a little at a time. So that un-returned phone call didn't make you feel so great? Make ten more… twenty more. Once you realize that any one failure, any one defeat, isn't the end of the road, you should (should!) be able to breathe a little easier next time.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThird, \u003cb\u003estudy\u003c/b\u003e resources that are proven for other competitive environments. There are books, websites, DVDs that use the same fundamental mental-toughness techniques that top trainers preach day in and day out to the peak-performers in every human endeavor. This technology has actually been developed to a fairly astonishing level—take advantage, be skeptical as required when confronted with some of the ridiculous claims that are out there, and find what works for you. Make a proactive effort to improve, just like you do when you sit down to compose. Work at it.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eFinally, \u003cb\u003eunclench\u003c/b\u003e. Put in perspective, though, they're actually relatively minor when you compare them to the fact that, even at a middling level of success, millions of people around the world will be hearing the music you create. You're taking your place as one of the people who shapes the artistic reality of the present moment. So the number of people targeting that $5,000 indie film is actually greater than the number gunning for the $200 million blockbuster. The major pictures are, of course, a much higher-stakes game, but the players at the table are fewer… especially considering that many of the filmmakers who work at that level already have their composer. That's something you need to know.\u003cbr /\u003eUse Google, social media of all sorts (searching Twitter is a great way to make some progress here)—seek out a couple dozen of your peers, whether you've ever made their acquaintance or not, and take stock. A-B. Back and forth. Be critical: Are you doing as well or better in terms of finished musical product? Chances are you're at least close. Congratulations. But don't get too cocky. Focus on the stuff others are doing better.\u003cb\u003e\u0026nbsp;\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003ePart Two\u003c/b\u003e of this strategy is more painful, more humbling, but crucial. Producers don't care that you don't have rooms and rooms full of gear, with small armies of studio underlings, to make everything sparkle. They just want their score to KILL. So A-B yourself with the people you think are absolutely the best in terms of writing chops, production quality, dramatic sensitivity. And draw the appropriate conclusions about where you stand. Just be sure to do it in a way that conveys your personality. Filmmakers can smell a Thomas Newman-alike or a John Williams-alike a mile off, and they'll wonder if you have anything original to contribute to their project.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eBut creating amazing music, as we always emphasize around here, is only a part of the equation. Far, far better to have some social interaction prior to that meeting in which you have to sell yourself.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eEither way, though, one thing I recommend avoiding at all costs is \u003cb\u003etalking too much\u003c/b\u003e. Especially if it's a gig I really want, my tendency is to try to seize control of the situation with words… words… words.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThat's a bad idea, kids.
