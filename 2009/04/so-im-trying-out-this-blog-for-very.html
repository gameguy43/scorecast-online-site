To Flit or Not to Flit?

Hi, folks—I'm trying out this blog for the very first time today, and I thought I'd bring up something for discussion—juggling.

I have a bunch of projects going just now (too few of them paying… sigh), and I find that the most fun way to work isn't necessarily the most efficient way—bouncing back and forth between sequences and shows, writing a bit here and a bit there until I'm stumped (or bored, or sleepy) and then switching gears.

Composing this way definitely takes the edge off of grinding through a particular tedious cue, but I suspect I'm never quite achieving the focus necessary to do my best work.

So what do you think, SCOREcasters? Is it better to power through one thing at a time, or is flitting around (I hesitate to call it multitasking) something we can call a SCOREcast-approved work strategy? Hop into the mix here and let me know what you think.

Houston Haynes said...

I'm most definitely a "flit"er, and quite honestly I think it has to do with allowing the *other* part of the brain time enough to chew on the primary cue. I was married to a writer for a while, and noticed the same thing for her - where she'd be in the middle of a feature-length screenplay, but would divert to work on a short story or give someone feedback on their work instead of continuously drilling on the big project in front of her. I think there's a non-verbal part of the brain that's "chewing" on all of the options in a non-or-semi-conscious way, and the pattern of surfing from one thing to another is a way to allow that part of the mind to work out all of the options.

There *are* times when you have to consciously discipline yourself to hit a deadline, but in most cases I "follow my nose" or more accurately my "ears" to those things that are in the front of my mind. It's a great way to unblock, and many times those "other" projects, whether for paying gigs or for building library cues, are for solving problems or experimenting with options that affect the cue that was put on the back shelf to begin with.

Once I finally accepted the non-linear way I process this kind of stuff, I hardly ever encountered writer's/composer's block. That, more than "flit"ing, is the worst enemy of any creative person. Whatever keeps the wheels turning, that's what works for me.

Jim Well said...

I'm probably the worst of the worst when it comes to flitting. Flit, flit, flit. See, I just can't stop ... flit! Flit!

I'm also better at prescribing a solution than I am at taking my own advice, but anyway, here's a thought.

Flit if you must. But counter-balance the flitting by putting in place something that closes the loop and keeps you accountable as you flit.

Example. At the beginning of each day, give yourself 5 minutes where you do nothing except to review and write down all the things you've got going. Think about which of them you really need to be doing the most -- looming deadline, nasty problem, whatever. Then make a quick but honest prioritization. Well, if not a set of priorities, then at least an order in which you plan to attack the items on your list. Actually write "1" next to the task you've decided to do first, or next to the item to which you've assigned the highest priority. Then "2", "3", and so on. That's your "accountability contract" with yourself. Now, put the list somewhere where it's sure to grab your attention, like -- on the cookie jar?

Last, but not least, check in with your list as the day progresses. If you're knocking off the things on your list, don't sweat too much about flitting or not flitting. But if you're not getting any of these things done, or you've paid absolutely no attention to your own plan, well, eat another cookie ...
