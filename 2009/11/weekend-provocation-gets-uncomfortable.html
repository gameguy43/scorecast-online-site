Lee Sanders: Getting Uncomfortable

November 6, 2009 Written by Lee Sanders Workflow

As we settle into our month on creativity, I’m going to return (at least for one weekend!) to the old-school Weekend Provocation format: I’ll throw a statement out there that may or may not seem, on its surface, to be odd, incoherent or just plain wrong. Then you think it over and see if your brain can make some valid connections that might not have been there before.

This weekend’s Provocation:
Creativity=discomfort.
Strange, when in the past few months we’ve talked about how to craft the most perfectly comfortable work environment, the most efficient (and, in that way, comfortable) workflow, and the smoothest (again, a form of comfort) collaborative relationships.
On the other hand, we always talk about “getting outside your comfort zone” as though it’s a good thing. That’s so obvious as to be a cliché—so let’s see if we can take it a bit further.
What new idea does this provocation stir up with you? The goal here is to get somewhere your normal thought patterns wouldn’t take you. So help us out by sharing where you arrived.

  • http://deaneogden.com Deane Ogden

    “Creativity = Discomfort” means something deeply personal to me, and is something I am very passionate about passing onto younger composers.

    It goes something like this: Unless your heart is miserable when you are not making music, unless you feel sick to your stomach when you are not composing the music you hear in your everyday life meanderings, please, I beg you… go do something else.

    This business is too tough, too hard, and too unforgiving for those that are in it half-assed. You pretty much have to be willing to die everyday to the pleasures of what would be considered a “normal life” in order to make it as a Creative.

    We've all heard it said that making a living as a Creative is often a feast or famine endeavor. In the film music business, that's putting it nicely. If you have a significant other and/or you have children, those “famine” times can be really tight and can be extremely uncomfortable. Often, it is too much to ask for you to expect someone else to walk through that with you.

    I am super fortunate that my girl is also in the film industry as a costumer, otherwise she wouldn't understand completely the political, social, and economic reasons for why I don't take certain jobs that pay a lot of money or why I take certain jobs that don't. All of those decisions affect our financial situation, and if you are linked with someone who cannot understand the career positioning behind such decisions, and instead is just concerned with how much is in the budget every month, you are in trouble already.

    It takes a very together and strong person to be able to live with a Creative. As a film composer, more often than not, you don't know where your next job is coming from. Therefore you have to be able to navigate the thin times as a team and have enough faith in each other that no matter what, things always work out fine. These can be difficult waters to sail if your significant other is more of a “worrier” than a “believer”.

    Before you launch into this career, ask yourself the very serious and probably most important question of “Will my significant other be able to withstand the harsh realities of this gig” and based on that answer, “Am I willing to put THAT theory to the test?”

  • http://www.titanlineaudio.com Houston Haynes

    I believe that you can't really find your voice as a creative until you're willing to really reveal something about yourself that makes you somewhat uncomfortable on some level – or at a minimum, a bit vulnerable. If you *don't* feel any sense of loss if a particular work is rejected, then I don't believe that the right investment was made in the cue (and might tacitly be one of the reasons *why* that take was rejected to begin with).

    Another take on this is that I feel like you must have sacrificed something in order to create the music you're putting forth. It seems silly to say/type out loud, but “safe” music doesn't interest me, and I think a lot of people – even total musical neophytes – pick up on that intuitively. There was a time back in my school days when I played for a raggae band that had a pretty loyal following – but it wasn't raggae fans per se as much as it was the extended social circle that the band leaders enjoyed because they were more “trustafarian” than true Rastafarians. They were good musicians and *loved* raggae, but there was a certain level of authenticity that just wasn't there.

    I think it boils down to the need for *true* music to come from the heart – banal as that sounds. It takes an act of courage to “speak” from a place of truth, and the audience can sense that immediately, and when you get it right, the response is most definitely gratifying. Film music doesn't always allow for that type of outlet, but when the opportunity comes up, the composer has to be absolutely fearless in embracing it. It's hardly ever comfortable, but almost always worthy of the effort.

    Anything else is piling artifice upon artifice. And an audience can smell that, even through the popcorn.
