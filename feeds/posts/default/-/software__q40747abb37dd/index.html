The majority of competing orchestral virtual instrument products are based on direct instrument waveform sampling technology and often involve huge and relatively inflexible sample libraries.\u0026nbsp; In place of fixed samples, WIVIs utilize a highly-parameterized instrument waveform model based on a technique similar to additive synthesis.\u0026nbsp; Most of these parameters can be varied continuously via MIDI, allowing nuances of playing technique far beyond what is typically possible with samples.\u0026nbsp; In addition, WIVIs utilize various types of randomization that not only can mimic variations characteristic of real playing but also counteract \"phasing\" effects that otherwise occur when virtual instruments are combined into ensembles.\u0026nbsp; WIVI plugins (VST, RTAS, and AU) take very little RAM or disk space and, for this reason, help complex projects to load extremely quickly in a DAW.\u0026nbsp; They run happily in either 32- or 64-bit environments under either Windows or Mac operating systems.\u0026nbsp; WIVIs are set up using a unique and powerful visual user interface that excels in both parametric flexibility and the ability to configure ensembles.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eCurrent WIVIs include many types of flutes, oboes, bassoons, clarinets, saxophones, and brass instruments (muted as well as unmuted) and are sold in bundles, working out to an average cost of about $25 per instrument.\u0026nbsp; A comprehensive set of WIVIs costs about $1500-$1600, street price.\u0026nbsp; Additional WIVIs are believed to be in development.\u0026nbsp; Strings, however, are notably missing from Wallander's offering and may or may not appear in the future.\u0026nbsp; Since I wrote about WIVIs in my July 15, 2009 SCOREcast column article, Wallander Instruments has released version 2.30, which makes many improvements to not only the WIVI interactive player but also to the sound and playability of all of the WIVI instruments.\u0026nbsp; Overall, I'm very enthusiastic about the expressiveness, creative possibilities, and sound quality WIVIs deliver and the degree to which they free me from many of the serious limitations of sample-based technology.\u0026nbsp; I just can't say enough about them.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cb\u003eLA Scoring Strings\u003c/b\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIntroduced in July, 2009, \u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.audiobro.com/\"\u003eLA Scoring Strings\u003c/a\u003e (LASS) is a sample-based string virtual instrument product that I think is representative of a trend toward increasing realism and playability in this sector of the composer's arsenal.\u0026nbsp; An intense battle among several competitors in this product area is easy to understand given the central importance of strings in traditional orchestra.\u0026nbsp; For what it delivers, LASS is well worth the $1200 cost.\u0026nbsp; LASS is at once notable for its beautiful, expressive sound and for what it does to simplify creation of convincing MIDI mockups of divisi string passages.\u0026nbsp; LASS approaches divisi by offering, in effect, 5 separately-recorded sample libraries rolled into one:\u0026nbsp; first chair (solo), two different 1/4 divisi sections, a 1/2 divisi section, and a full section -- for each of Violin 1, Violin 2, Viola, Cello, and Bass.\u0026nbsp; Samples from the different section types can be combined in any way desired, either to implement divisi or to create a full orchestra with maximum sonic diversity that maximizes the impression of realism.\u0026nbsp; LASS is also equipped with scripts (which run in the included Kontakt 3.5 playback engine environment) that make it relatively easy to orchestrate with samples of real legato, portamento, and glissando playing under MIDI control.\u0026nbsp; Additional scripts support trills, patterns of short articulations, tuning, and other features.\u0026nbsp; However, while LASS supports the string articulations one is most likely to need, some articulations, such as col legno, are not provided and may require you to combine LASS with another string library.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eNot surprisingly, considering it is a sample library, LASS uses a lot of RAM.\u0026nbsp;
