SCOREcast Poll Discussion

by SCOREcast Team · 9 Comments · in Business

In the spirit of this month’s focus on branding and marketing, we wanted to give you all a chance to voice your opinion on what method of marketing you like best. So once you’ve taken our poll in the right side bar, tell us in the comments why you chose your answer. Why is that particular method the easiest for you? What method of marketing do you like the least? Do you prefer to let your personality shine at networking events over sending out demos? Do you loathe having to keep up with social networks like twitter instead of updating your website’s portfolio? Or do you hate attending social events when you could be spreading your online presence with social networks and blogs? Sound off in the comments!

  • http://realstrings.wordpress.com/ pete whitfield

    I voted for web marketing which currently has the least votes! I guess your method of staying in employment is quite a personal thing, but I reckon the old cliche 'it's not what you know, it's who you know' still has some truth about it, though, of course, you have to be able to deliver the goods. As it's a global market now, personal interactions are difficult; most of my work does not come from local sources. I believe blogs and social networks achieve what personal interactions do – they project your identity, beliefs, character and values. Interesting poll!

  • http://twitter.com/hethfen Heather Fenoughty

    I too voted for web marketing, for the same reasons as Pete (Hi Pete!) though it was a close call between that and website/portfolio. I find those two easier to maintain consistently – probably because I actually enjoy them – and also for me they're inextricably linked. Social networking and blogging hopefully draws attention to my online portfolio, which can then 'talk for itself'.

  • David Pencil

    I voted for Personal Interaction. While the other stuff is important too, to me, the best bang for your buck is actual face-time with the people who will hire you. It's an industry of relationships, and there's a big difference between being another disk in the pile or another friend on Facebook versus being the person sitting across the table. It's also a great way for them to feel you out, what you're personality and temperament are like, and vice versa.

  • EmmettCooke

    Portfolio driven myself – far easier when you are so far from the main “hubs” (London/LA etc.) in little old Ireland :)

  • http://www.joelciulla.com Joel Gregory Ciulla

    Web marketing. Easy and to the point with info and music which will “speak” for itself. Too much info seems to be an over-kill. We live in the generation of ” I want it now! “.

  • http://www.nanavant.com/ Nan Avant

    I picked the portfolio driven but would agree with Heather that online social networks draw people to you and then they can look at your web site. I think all three are necessary to market yourself and you almost have to use all three to get recognized.

  • http://twitter.com/hethfen Heather Fenoughty

    I think the wording of the poll is very interesting: which do you find easiest, rather than which do find you get the best ROI (or ‘bang for buck’).

    I find I’d agree with David P that face-to-face networking is on average often the most effective for the amount of time spent doing it… but I don’t find it very easy. So it’s a huge effort, whereas online/social networking and portfolio work is soooo much easier in comparison, (and really rather fun) so very little effort at all, with a reasonable if long term (and often difficult to quantify) return on that investment.

    Sorry to argue semantics ;-)

    I agree with Nan – you have to use all three and other forms to market yourself. It’s wisest to use any and all weapons in your arsenal to get noticed!

  • John Maestri

    A healthy balance of all three is necessary. Anyone who completely ignores online marketing opportunities is not up to snuff in a constantly changing field of technological advancement. Alas, I picked the old fashioned Personal Interactions because we're in the people business where good communication can organize itself into positive working relationships.

  • http://twitter.com/DeaneOgdenMusic Deane Ogden

    I, too, believe that each of these is important. However, I chose “personal interactions” because I think often times it is my personality that sells me. My music speaks for itself, and it does what it does—I cannot control it. It is going to hit some people right and it is going to hit some people wrong. That's just the nature of our biz.

    But… You get me in a room with someone and nine times out of ten, I'll walk out with the job. My personality communicates that I am easy to work with, I bring ZERO drama to the table, I can achieve when facing even the toughest deadlines, I can solidly lead a team in the midst of utter chaos, and nothing ruffles my feathers when its time to stand and deliver.

    I cannot communicate those strengths on a webpage or in a demo package. Face-to-face speaks volumes… sometimes without saying a word.
