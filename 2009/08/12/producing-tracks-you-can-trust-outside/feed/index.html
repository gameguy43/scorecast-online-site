Some may not go “as far down the rabbit hole” as those of us with hard-core engineering chops, but as I said before, the 80/20 rule applies. Focus first on learning to write good music. My two cents.

]]> By: Houston Haynes ../#comment-761 Wed, 12 Aug 2009 20:26:03 +0000 ../../../../../?p=109#comment-761 Hey James. Thanks for the kudos – your article “Love Boat in 7.1?” was part of the inspiration for the deep dive.

Gear listing in this area is a very slippery slope. A lot of it has to do with budget, and with speaker monitors it can vary so widely that a solution that corresponds to one budget level wouldn't make sense at another. Even in home/independent studios the band is wide enough that you can run into giving good advice for one situation, and the same thing would be bad advice for another. Then there's the whole host-dependent aspect. Some solutions [even free ones] might be VST-only, and still others might be great for AU or RTAS based hosts. Then there's the hardware solutions, where certain studios hang an analog meter right off the main output buss, and simply run their studio speakers through dedicated monitor buss control hardware. I do the same thing “inside the box” with Nuendo's Control Room, and there are some really nice pro-sumer level monitor controllers, but most times they are coupled to a speaker vendor's product line.

You can start with some of the pointers I link to in the article. I think Bob Katz's chapter on speaker calibration from “Mastering Audio” might be published on his site as a web article. You can also find links to free metering plugins on KVRAudio.com that I've never tried, but some of them look pretty cool. Then there are the vendor sites, which can have a lot of good information about speaker selection and placement. Here's one for Genelec that is really well done – much more concise than I can be. 🙂

http://www.genelec.com/learning-center/speaker-…

And of course, the final factor is just plain appetite – do you, as the composer, have enough interest in this subject to do things piece by piece, or are you more of the type to pay for tools/consulting that does most of the heavy lifting for you? For better or worse, it's a relatively open set of factors, and cheat sheets don't have a lot of pass-around value.

]]> By: Houston Haynes ../#comment-765 Wed, 12 Aug 2009 10:52:07 +0000 ../../../../../?p=109#comment-765 Hey Jai – you're welcome! FWIW, you could very well be in the correct power band for the music you're making and still end up with ear fatigue. Some music demands to be played loud, since that's the style in which the audience hears it. Oddly enough, it was Bob Moog that pressed that point to me during college. When you see a gray-haired old man shouting, “Dammit, it's the medium that matters! If it's music that meant to be played loud – PLAY IT LOUD!” you tend to listen.

The key is to understand what you give and what you get in those scenarios – and to rest your ears afterward. There are actual physiological changes in the ear when it's been subjected to loud sounds for a long time. I'm not talking about hearing loss, but micro-changes in the supporting structures of the ear mechanism that can change perception – particularly at low frequencies. That's one of the reasons why I rely on good metering to “see me through” and try to keep the results consistent. It can be like adjusting your perception to compensate for a pair of speakers that you *know* has deficiencies (hello, NS-10M). Once you know the effect is there and could be at play, you can limit the effects.

]]> By: Houston Haynes ../#comment-763 Wed, 12 Aug 2009 10:04:35 +0000 ../../../../../?p=109#comment-763 Thanks for the kudos, Lee. There's a ton of useful info coming out this month. (Props to Deane's article “Scoring Through the Droughts” for an incredibly well-written piece on motivation) I kind of ran a little bit long with my article than originally planned, but felt like it was worth the stretch to put things in context.

I think mixing on headphones is largely a mistake. Even if you're just tracking, you tend to make instrumentation and arrangement decisions based on an inaccurate view of how much the voice fills the mix. That's just inviting more work down the road.

Also, the stereo image ends up being pinched – since the speakers are now at 90 degrees to your head, you tend to drive things to the middle in order for things to “sound right”. Then when you go back to speakers everything has collapsed to the center and you can even get into issues of getting in the way of things that *should* be in the middle that you're covering dialog (or a lead singer, as the case may be).

You can get a pair of head worn monitors that more closely approximates the way in which speakers reach the head naturally, but they're very expensive and I'm always suspicious of their acoustic efficacy. It's bad enough trying to do big room mixes in a small studio – there's no sense in blowing the dough on something the increases the odds of a mistake.

Then again, I suppose that if someone listens to enough reference material on headphones and makes the same kind of mix decisions that match those references, I suppose they can come close to something that will transfer. I've read articles about how Europeans (and in particular, Germans) have a very headphone-friendly audiophile base. If that's the primary medium in which the material is to be heard, then I cannot argue against using them.

But as a substitute for proper studio monitoring, that's a stretch that I can't make.
