First, the cost of higher-speed CPUs increases more rapidly than the extra benefit they deliver – i.e., you get more bang for the buck at middle-level speeds. Second, a faster CPU may require the other system components (e.g., RAM) to be faster as well – increasing the cost impact. Third, higher clock rates can mean higher power dissipation, such that your computer runs hotter – and that reduces reliability. So, I stuck my finger in the wind and chose the middle ground. My choice has turned out to be adequate.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThe motherboard also has quite a few slots for PCI expansion cards. Experience has shown that having enough of these slots is crucial – I’m using all the slots I have, and would use more if I had them.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eOne last detail: I chose an \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRME 9652 \u003c/span\u003ecard as my computer’s audio interface. This choice was made because RME was early in providing a driver that supports 64-bit operation. The 9652 also has three ADAT input and 3 ADAT output ports, collectively capable of moving up to 24 channels of audio at 48 KHz sample rate. This is how I move digital audio streams between my computer and my outboard gear, including my A/D-D/A converter. Most of the time, I use no more than 4 of the 24 audio channels for the type of work I’ve chosen to do – i.e., I’m not trying to create a full-blown recording studio, per se, but rather an environment for MIDI-enabled composing.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003eThe Software. \u003c/span\u003e Once I determined that a 64-bit computer was the way I’d go, I was in dangerous territory, because to use such a machine in a way that takes full advantage of RAM above the 4- Gbyte boundary, you have to have software that runs in 64-bit mode. This includes your operating system, your DAW software, your drivers (such as the driver for your digital audio card and any other piece of hardware integrated into your computer), and as many of your VIs as possible. At the time I was figuring all of this out (18 months ago), there wasn’t much available that worked in 64-bit mode, and it was unclear how long it would be before the situation improved significantly.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThe first decision to be made was which 64-bit operating system to use. Should it be \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eWindows\u003c/span\u003e \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eXP\u003c/span\u003e, with which I was familiar, or \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eWindows Vista\u003c/span\u003e, which implemented more leading-edge technology but about which I’d heard lots of bad press and had even had my own negative experiences? I was advised to go with \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eVista Ultimate 64-bit\u003c/span\u003e, as XP was seen as a dead-end product and software vendors were designing their products to work under Vista. Once again, I decided to take a calculated risk – in this case, that things would work out best over time if I went with Vista. I made this decision, as well as my choice of computer hardware configuration, in combination with the availability of a convincing level of technical support from the computer’s vendor, \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ePC Audio Labs\u003c/span\u003e. I’ve paid extra for that support, but I figured my shot at business success was conditioned upon having a computer that worked well and had minimum downtime, and the extra cost seemed worth it. Experience has proven me right.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI decided to use Steinberg’s \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eCubase \u003c/span\u003eas my DAW, for the following reasons: 1) I was familiar with Cubase, liked the way it worked, and didn’t want to be tied to dedicated hardware as would have been the case had I chosen a product such as \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eProTools\u003c/span\u003e; 2) I knew Cubase was a relatively mature product but that a 64-bit product was under development; and 3) I felt that Steinberg’s pioneering role as developer of the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eVST \u003c/span\u003e(\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003eV\u003c/span\u003eirtual \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003eS\u003c/span\u003etudio \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003eT\u003c/span\u003eechnology) plug-in standard meant that most VI vendors would be sure their plug-ins worked with Cubase. At the time of my decision, Steinberg was offering only a “preview version” of their 64-bit software (which was later to be introduced on a fully-supported basis with the release of Cubase 5). This preview version was said to be for those brave souls who needed to be at the bleeding edge of technology. I figured that while Steinberg was readying a fully-supported 64-bit version, I’d be coming up the learning curve on a lot of other things. This conjecture proved to be true, and the timing worked out about right. I’m now running 64-bit Cubase 5.1.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eAs for virtual instruments from which I can build a complete orchestra, I’m currently using a combination of products from different vendors. You hear this combination in the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRite Of Spring\u003c/span\u003e examples.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eAs I described back in July, I’m using WIVIs for all of my woodwinds and brass. Since July, I’ve only grown happier with the WIVI approach. I expect my use of WIVIs will expand over the next year or two as new instruments are introduced and existing ones are improved. One of the biggest positive impacts of the WIVIs is that they use very little RAM, because they’re based on modeling rather than sampling. In principle, they also use little CPU. However, current WIVIs operate only in 32-bit mode, whereas I have a 64-bit environment. I’m able to use them through the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eVST Bridge\u003c/span\u003e feature of Cubase. This works by creating a software wrapper around any 32-bit VST plug-in instance; the wrapper makes the VST instance appear to Cubase as if it’s a 64-bit plug-in, and makes Cubase appear to the VST instance as if it’s a 32-bit DAW. That’s great except that VST Bridge adds a surprisingly large amount to the total CPU workload, and the amount added is roughly proportional to the number of 32-bit plug-in instances. If you have a large number of WIVIs, this can easily add up to the single largest component of CPU usage. Although I find I can live with the actual amount of CPU load I have in the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRite Of Spring\u003c/span\u003e, I’ve decided to reduce the load by taking advantage of the ability of a WIVI plug-in instance to incorporate more than one virtual instrument. I’ve used this technique to reduce the number of WIVI plug-ins by a factor of about 5, relative to the number I’d have if I put only one instrument in each plug-in instance. That has brought my \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRite Of Spring\u003c/span\u003e CPU usage down to about 60% of what it previously was. More about these numbers later.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eFor strings, I’m using \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eAudiobro\u003c/span\u003e’s recently-introduced \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eLA Scoring Strings\u003c/span\u003e (LASS) library. This is a sample-based library and, depending on how you use it, is quite capable of filling 16 Gbytes of RAM – which is what I have. It’s a good thing my WIVIs don’t use much RAM! My standard LASS setup deliberately creates a great deal of flexibility so that I usually have at my disposal what I need without having to pause in the middle of composing to add something that’s not there. The downside of this strategy is that it requires a lot of RAM. If I use LASS’s 24-bit samples, the amount of RAM used is uncomfortably close to 16 Gbytes. Thus, for most of my work, I use the 16-bit samples, which cuts several Gbytes from my RAM usage (the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRite Of Spring\u003c/span\u003e MP3s were made using LASS’s 16-bit samples).\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eLASS samples are hosted by \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eKontakt 3.5\u003c/span\u003e, which is a recently-introduced, 64-bit version of the Kontakt sampler. I use the full version of the Kontakt 3.5 sampler, but you can also use the Kontakt 3.5 player that’s supplied by Audiobro, along with LASS.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eLASS, by the way, is a fantastic string library and one of the best acquisitions I’ve made. Still, I’ve only scratched the surface of what LASS can do. But as LASS’s many positive attributes are not the main focus of this article, I’ll leave further discussion of those to another day. There are a few shortcomings. For those, I used samples from the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eSonic Implants String Collection\u003c/span\u003e, with good result. I addressed all of those needs with samples from EWQLSO, delivered by EastWest’s 64-bit Play engine.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIn addition to all of the instruments I’ve described, there are currently 15 reverb instances, one of which is a convolution reverb, and a few equalizers. The lion’s share of these (217) are string instruments from the LASS library. This seemingly large imbalance can be explained by the fact that the LASS instruments are sample-based and have separate samples (which I’m counting as separate instruments) for the various articulations that are supported. On the wind side, in contrast, all of the instruments are WIVIs, which, by nature, don’t have multiple articulations. This suggests that I might be able to reduce the amount of RAM I’m using by reducing the value of the RAM pre-load size parameter in Kontakt 3.5. This would demand more from the disk, but the disk seems to have a lot more to give. We’ll see.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eCPU load and its distribution across the 8 CPU cores can be effectively monitored using the Performance tab of the Task Manager built into Windows Vista. Given all the blood, sweat, and tears that have gone into getting this far, that’s welcome news indeed. And if you’ve ever wondered whether these big systems really work – you have your answer.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eCould I have gotten by with less? Perhaps. When sequencing, it’s easy to create a pattern that sounds cool but would be difficult or impossible for a real guitarist to play – and therefore, you’d be unlikely to hear such a pattern in real guitar work. In real playing, each hand must carefully respect what the other is doing in order to produce a clean, continuous sound that avoids buzzing strings and dull thuds, even at chord changes. A finger of the left hand (which forms chords) can change position only when doing so will not prematurely terminate a tone sounding on any affected string. Conversely, a finger of the right hand can pluck a string only when a finger of the left hand isn’t about to move either onto or off of it. (Exceptions are made for deliberate hammer-ons and pull-offs.) The resulting choreography is a little like running underneath a herd of stampeding giraffes – it greatly constrains what the guitarist does, and thus puts a certain imprint on a picking and chording pattern that says: \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ethis sounds real\u003c/span\u003e.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eTo make sure my MIDI sequence had this imprint, I used my own playing as a guide. In other words, the note pattern of the entire 3-1/2 minute sequence captures the way I’d actually play the song if I could do so reliably enough to meet my quality standard.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI entered the notes one by one using the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003e\u003ca href\u003d\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubase\"\u003eCubase\u003c/a\u003e \u003c/span\u003epiano roll and my mouse, carefully studying my actual playing as I went along. However, before resorting to this tedious method, I tried to find a faster way. I happen to own a \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003e\u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.godinguitars.com/godinmultnylonsap.htm\"\u003eGodin\u003c/a\u003e \u003c/span\u003eMIDI guitar. A \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003e\u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.roland.com/products/en/GR-20/index.html\"\u003eRoland\u003c/a\u003e \u003c/span\u003eguitar synth attached to this guitar is capable, in principle, of transforming each string pluck into the corresponding MIDI note and velocity. I thought this might make it easy for me to get the notes into \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eCubase \u003c/span\u003eas I picked away naturally – figuring that I’d clean everything up afterward. But I found that this setup was incredibly finicky and made so many errors that it cost me more time than it saved. I also tried using \u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.musiclab.com/products/realgtr_info.htm\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRealGuitar 2.2L\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/a\u003e, a virtual instrument created by \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eMusicLab\u003c/span\u003e. In the hands of a keyboardist skilled in its use – which I’m \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003enot \u003c/span\u003e– it can produce some pretty convincing acoustic guitar parts. There is also an ability to create custom picking patterns which can then be drag-n-dropped onto a MIDI track in varying orders to create a complete guitar part. The resulting MIDI tracks, however, are in a special format designed to work only with the guitar samples built into \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRealGuitar \u003c/span\u003eand not with another instrument such as the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eI’m A Blackbird Guitar\u003c/span\u003e I had selected. Had I more time to set it up properly, \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRealGuitar \u003c/span\u003emight have produced acceptable results – but there was no time, and I also recognized that \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRealGuitar\u003c/span\u003e’s way of doing things would make it awkward for me to match my own playing style without a lot of contortions. Yet another option I considered was \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ePG Music\u003c/span\u003e’s product, \u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.pgmusic.com/\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eBand-in-a-Box\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/a\u003e. It lets you come up with a guitar part by any of a number of methods, and, unlike \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRealGuitar\u003c/span\u003e, can output a straightforward, easily-edited MIDI sequence. But \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eBand-in-a-Box\u003c/span\u003e is a very complex product and, like \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRealGuitar\u003c/span\u003e, functions in a way that makes recreating one’s personal playing style more work than I could deal with in a short amount of time. So, fairly quickly, I concluded that the fastest way to get the result I needed was also the simplest and most direct: simply entering all the notes by hand.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eWith practice, notes can be hand-entered into a piano roll more quickly than you’d think. However, the initial result isn’t likely to sound very realistic, mainly because the note \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003evelocities\u003c/span\u003e will be constant unless you make them otherwise. Once again, I analyzed my own playing and found that an emphasis on certain notes was an essential part of the finger-picking style I was sequencing. To first order, the style had an eighth-note rhythm, but often the stress pattern was \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003e\u003ca href\u003d\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syncopation\"\u003esyncopated\u003c/a\u003e \u003c/span\u003ein one of several ways. Subdividing a 4/4 measure into eight equal parts numbered from 1 to 8, I found, for example, that in one characteristic pattern, subdivisions 4 and 8 were stressed. In another lick, the stress was on 1, 4, and 7. And so on. And then, there were additional variations reflecting the flow of the song and particularly leading into transitions. And there was an instrumental break in the middle of the song, which required more emphasis. So, yes, I went through and edited all the velocities by hand, taking into account all of these factors.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eTiming nuances also affected the perception of realism. For example, I noted that when I play in this particular style, there are times when I pluck two strings, \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003enominally \u003c/span\u003eat the same time. Generally this is accomplished by a \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003epinching \u003c/span\u003emotion, in which my thumb plucks one of the lower-pitched strings while another finger moves in the opposite direction to pluck a higher string. However, I usually put a slight \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003edelay \u003c/span\u003ebetween the two notes. When sequencing, and especially if quantization is turned on, the tendency will be for these two notes to be precisely simultaneous – which doesn’t sound realistic. You have to put in the delay. Thus, I searched the song for instances that warranted making deliberate timing adjustments like these, and edited note start and end times accordingly.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI should point out that I had quantization turned \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eoff \u003c/span\u003ewhen entering most of the notes, relying only on the visual grid to guide me. This practice introduces a certain amount of timing randomness that can help eliminate a machine-like sound. And on top of my \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003edeliberate \u003c/span\u003evelocity and timing edits, I used \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eCubase \u003c/span\u003eto apply a small amount of randomness to both of these attributes. But I don’t think that applying randomness, by itself, is a good strategy for obtaining a realistic feel. It's still necessary to study the nuances of real playing and emulate those deliberately.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI did some tempo editing as well, though probably less than I should have. \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eCubase\u003c/span\u003e, like most \u003ca href\u003d\"http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Audio_Workstation\"\u003eDAW\u003c/a\u003e applications, makes graphical editing of the tempo contour pretty easy. I used this mostly in the last verse to provide dramatic rubato. Although I wanted the tempo to be fairly steady for the most part, I could have improved the interpretation and humanization of the song by judiciously adding rubato in other places. On the other hand, recording the guitar first meant that in my vocal, I would have had to be very careful to follow the guitar’s timing – the reverse of the leader-follower relationship I’d expect in a real performance. Having a lot of tempo variation would have added unwanted complication in the face of a short time schedule.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eIn this particular project, guitar note timings and velocities were the primary things that needed tweaking to get a realistic sound. However, there were some additional things I could have done that might have provided further improvement. For example, like many guitar virtual instruments these days, \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eI’m A Blackbird Guitar\u003c/span\u003e offers string noises (those \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eeeek \u003c/span\u003esounds you get when sliding along a wrapped string) that can be deliberately invoked by the MIDI sequence. However, inserting the right type of noise in the appropriate place requires much more careful study, for which I had no time. There is also an ability to adjust the envelope of each note (attack/hold/delay/sustain/release), which could probably have improved the quality of each note pluck, had I had the time. And so on.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eBy the way, as a result of selecting an \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eEastWest \u003c/span\u003eguitar for this project, the guitar track had in it a substantial amount of ambience which is characteristic of all of \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eEastWest\u003c/span\u003e’s sample libraries. I did not use the reverb built into \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eEastWest\u003c/span\u003e’s playback engine (\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003ePlay\u003c/span\u003e) add more to this ambience. In fact, I might have preferred a dryer guitar, given that my voice was to be recorded dry.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003eCapturing the Vocal\u003c/span\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eOnce I had the guitar part nailed down in MIDI, I was ready to record my vocal. This involved using \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eCubase \u003c/span\u003eto play back the guitar part while simultaneously recording my voice on a separate track, while I monitored the guitar by headphone. I used a \u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.rodemic.com/microphone.php?product\u003dK2\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eRode K2\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/a\u003e vacuum tube condenser microphone with a cardioid pickup pattern, running the mike into an \u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.avalondesign.com/vt737sp.html\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eAvalon Vt-737sp\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/a\u003e channel strip primarily to provide soft compression and a little extra warmth. For the most part, I set the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eVt-737sp\u003c/span\u003e according to \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eAvalon\u003c/span\u003e’s recommendations for vocals: about 6dB of treble boost following the compressor, 4:1 compression, -5 dB threshold, attack and release each at 10 o’clock. (It’s difficult to know exactly what the latter two settings amount to in terms of times.)\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eWorking at a sample rate of 44.1 KHz, I recorded five takes of the vocal, figuring that none was perfect but that I could pick the best version of each phrase and make a decent composite. To do this, I lined up the five takes side by side in the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eCubase \u003c/span\u003emain window and went through the song a phrase at a time, each time choosing what I felt was the best take for that phrase. For each phrase so found, I extracted a chunk of waveform from the relevant take and glued it into the composite I was creating. This produced \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003emuch \u003c/span\u003ebetter results than if I had simply used any of the five raw takes without compositing – and didn’t require any entire take to be flawless.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eNext I used \u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.celemony.com/cms/index.php?id\u003dproducts_studio\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eMelodyne Studio\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/a\u003e to correct my pitch, working manually on each note. I’ve never been a great singer, and hadn’t sung at all for about four years before making this recording, so you can imagine my pitch control wasn’t so hot. From a pitch standpoint, the corrected result was very good. However, a noticeable degree of vocal \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eexpressiveness \u003c/span\u003ewas lost in the process of cleaning up the pitch. I didn’t recognize the extent of this loss until several hours later, when I was able to make a global assessment of the pitch-corrected vocal. With hindsight, if I had time to redo the correction, I’d strive for a better balance between pitch correction and preservation of expression.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003ePutting It All Together\u003c/span\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThe last step was to mix the vocal and guitar part into single stereo track. Before mixing, I processed the composite vocal through an instance of the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003e\u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.celemony.com/cms/index.php?id\u003dproducts_studio\"\u003eTrackPlug\u003c/a\u003e \u003c/span\u003eplug-in from \u003ca href\u003d\"http://wavearts.com/\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eWaveArts\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/a\u003e. The plug-in was set to roll off the frequencies above 7.6 kHz at -12 dB/octave and apply compression over and above what was done by the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eVt-737sp\u003c/span\u003e. The compression parameters at this stage were 4:1 ratio, -17 dB threshold, 0 dB gain, 2 ms attack and 100 ms release, Vintage mode, medium knee. To balance the guitar with the voice, I dropped the level of the guitar track by 5.6 dB. No other processing was applied to the guitar before mixing it with the vocal.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eI applied reverb to the result of mixing. For this I used the \u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.voxengo.com/product/pspace/\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eVoxengo Pristine Space\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/a\u003e convolution reverb plug-in loaded with the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eSmall Hall\u003c/span\u003e impulse response from the \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eBree Casedy\u003c/span\u003e impulse response library available from \u003ca href\u003d\"http://www.studiodevices.com/\"\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eStudio Devices\u003c/span\u003e\u003c/a\u003e. In my opinion, this is a very good impulse response library. Wanting a very close-miked sound, I mixed in only a very small amount of reverb, setting the wet level 26 dB below the dry level.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThe last stage of processing was an instance of \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003eWaveArts\u003c/span\u003e’ \u003cspan style\u003d\"font-style: italic;\"\u003e\u003ca href\u003d\"http://wavearts.com/products/plugins/finalplug/\"\u003eFinalPlug\u003c/a\u003e \u003c/span\u003eplug-in, which applies peak limiting and an adjustable degree of compression for volume maximization. I set the compression threshold of this plug-in at -6 dB.\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cspan style\u003d\"font-weight: bold;\"\u003ePerspective\u003c/span\u003e\u003cbr /\u003e\u003cbr /\u003eThis article is intended to give you some idea what’s involved in a project that’s simple yet aims to achieve a high quality result.
