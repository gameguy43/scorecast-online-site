Open Forum Friday: How Do You Define “Team”?

October 29, 2010 Written by SCO Staff Industry , Process

The concept of a “team” means different things to different people in our business.

For some, the team is the group of people doing everything that would keep you from writing so that you can concentrate on the all-consuming task of… uh, writing. Said another way: they do the things you could be doing, but shouldn’t. Examples of people on this kind of team could include an assistant, an intern, a studio manger, etc.

Quite another definition of team illustrates a group of  people that are actually doing the writing… so that the person that is supposed to be doing the writing can be doing other things… instead of… uh, writing. Examples of this could include ghost writers, additional music personnel, or in a more extreme sense, some of these organizations that are popping up that take on a film with the strategy of piecing it out amongst dozens of their “member” composers to get the score finished, while possibly (or possibly not) giving credits to the participating composers.

Some of these definitions of the concept of “team” are a little looser than others, obviously. But we are curious what YOU think of these and whether you agree or disagree with any of them as models of getting this gig done. In addition to your opinions on those particular examples, what are some of the ways that you incorporate your team in your process?

The COMMENTS are wide open, so Lurkers—it’s time to come out and get loud! And… should any ghosters want to come out and spill the beans—you don’t have to register to leave a comment. We’re just sayin’. ;-)

  • Felix Weber

    For twelve years I’ve been working successfully with a team of 3 where everybody played a specific role. I was solely responsible for the writing, my partner for studio business matters and my manager for business in general. That was great until the team fell apart and I realized that I was only good in the musical part of my business, but that I was almost illiterate in the other important areas of my business such as business itself.

    For the last eight years I’m working within a team of 4, but now, even though everyone has still a specific task and skill, we all work together as a cross functional team. So that the right hand knows what the left hand does and that in case someone isn’t capable of doing his or her task anymore the whole thing doesn’t just fall apart, but someone else can substitute the missing part. A very transparent team which also formed great relationships.

    Of the two “team models” you mentioned, I would prefer the first one over the second one. I don’t think my ego would allow me to take the credit of someone else’s work.

  • Jason

    Interesting topic. I’m not sure which organization you guys are referring to when you mention the piecing out of scores to member composers, but I was involved in something like that called the Composer Collective and it was a horribly exploitative situation. Despite having written several cues on one of their films I have never received credit nor payment for the work. In fact, the guy that runs it is mysteriously the only person who received any credit for all of the members’ work. If you are a struggling composer with no large credits don’t do what I did and let yourself be suckered by these immoral people. I regret having done so.

  • bellis

    I’ve worked with one contractor since we went to high school together (40 some years ago). Many of the musicians I call have been working with me for over 30 years and I think I’ve only had two or three steady scoring mixers in that time.
    A “team” differs from any other group of individuals because they actually care about each other’s success. I take pains to make their job a little easier and they play or mix or contract with my success and well-being in mind. We are interdependent. Money is never the issue.
