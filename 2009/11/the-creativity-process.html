James Olszewski: The CreativITY Process

by SCOREcast Team · No Comments · in Uncategorized

November’s SCO theme is The Creative Process. Most of the articles this month this will likely focus on various aspects of film or TV processes. But since DIY Studio focuses more on starting up, I’d like to broaden the scope of the definition, recognizing how diverse we may all be in where we are in our journeys. As such, this month’s DIY Studio edition is more about The CreativITY Process. The great thing about the Creative Process is that, at its most fundamental, it’s free.

The Creative Process is a process- a method to get something done. It’s a thought process. There are of course tools, techniques, classes, theory, etc, to help you “be creative”, and many people take advantage of these. But doesn’t the very nature of creativity lie in people thinking in ways no one has before? For this edition of DIY Studio, let’s disregard all the workshops, classes, technical aspects, and maybe even theory. Let’s instead discuss the Creativity Process in terms of things everyone can do, even as we go about our day. I’ll tell you some things I do to get (or keep) my creativity flowing, and I hope to hear other thoughts from the SCOREcast community.

Writing Without Writing
For me, composing starts minutes or hours before I play my first note. It’s tempting to just dive into my studio when I get a new, load up some patches, and start playing. And sometimes I do that, as you’ll see below. But sometimes I just find a silent place and listen to the music in my head. Back in the 80’s I used to love this painting show on PBS, with Bill Alexander. He always painted such awesome landscapes. One of the most fascinating things about watching this show is that before he even touched his brush to the canvas, he would fully describe the painting as he imagined it. He had the scene fixed in his head before he even began; the act of painting was just a matter of attempting to replicate in the physical world, the image in his head. I often do this when I write- I just sit and listen to the concert in my head. Once I’ve listened to it and I have it firmly fixed in my mind, it’s then just a matter of “copying” it into the physical world. To use a film term I’ll call this technique “previsualization”.
Jai Meghan discussed in an article earlier this month the technique of writing an Overture. For me, the mental technique of previsualizing dovetails perfectly into the Overture concept. Using previsualization and Overtures in tandem lets you almost “cookbook” the cues from that point on, giving you the opportunity to be creative when you’re at your best, instead of trying to come up with an effective and memorable cue in the middle of the night when you’ve been working for 16 hours.
When Inspiration Attacks!
Besides those times when you’re sitting at your computer, piano, or other instrument, what do you do to be ready for inspiration to strike? I’m very curious to hear from you about this! For me, I have a few standbys.First, I carry around staff paper. I have some in my backpack with my “day job” stuff, or sometimes I keep a few sheet in the car. If I get caught without any staff paper, I hit http://www.blanksheetmusic.net/ and print off a sheet or two. More than a couple times this web site meant the difference between my capturing a motif or passage, and letting it get away. Alternatively (or additionally), I’ve also recorded myself, actually singing melodies to capture them. If you have a phone or PDA, I recommend you get familiar with your voice recorder. Some of my best ideas occurred to me in traffic, on the drive to and from work.Next, I always have a project. If it’s not a paying gig or something for anyone in particular, I make up projects for myself. This forces me to continue honing my writing, orchestrating, and technical skills, but it also gives me an opportunity to practice writing in different styles and develops my portfolio, without pressure. As a composer still getting my feet wet, I do have plenty of non-project time; this is a great way to keep myself sharp.One of my favorite creativity activities is to just sit down, open my DAW, hit record, and just play. I turn off the metronome, I don’t worry about key sig, playing well, tempo, anything. I just play. Go for at least 10 minutes, just messing around. From this method, I’ve come up with jazz pieces, classical pieces, I’ve discovered new chords / chord progressions, and one cartoony piece my daughter loves. I keep all of these performances in a special directory I call a “sketchbook”. Just as artists and writers keep little sketchbooks with them to scribble ideas, so should composers. It’s amazing the inspiration you can give yourself by pulling up your sketchbook when you have writer’s block.

I have a few more thoughts on creativity, maybe I’ll continue in the comments, but I think I’ll wrap up for now and just let the discussion begin. What is your CreativITY Process?
