James Olszewski: Bad Cues and the People Who Write Them

August 18, 2009 Written by SCO Staff Announcements

I was writing a cue last night, not for any particular project mind you, but just to write something. Who knows, maybe I’ll need it someday, right? It was pretty clear in my head, but not as crystal clear as other times. Sometimes I feel like I’m just regurgitating what I hear in my head! Not so last night, it was like trying to hold sand with my hands.

And it showed in the cue- it was not good. But it still sounded okay in my head, although it was still not totally clear.

Times like this make me wonder whether I should “abort mission” and walk away from the cue, or whether I should continue to hammer away at it, like some sort of blacksmith. I wonder how the SCOREcasters deal with such situations? Or is there some in-between ground, e.g., work on a different cue for a few days then return to it. What sometimes works for me is to just take my hands off the keys, close my eyes, and listen to the piece in my head, let it finish developing a bit more, bring it out of my subconscious.

Sometimes that works.

What do you do? Salvage your cue? Your time? Or some of both? I figured I’d throw it out there for you to chew on.

    Great question for Scorecasters, me included. ;) Personally I really don’t find myself writing cues that have no sync or video reference (perhaps no time). So in my case I guess usually if I feel like a cue isn’t working or cutting it musically I always sit back, try to mix the audio reference as best as I can and play through it video full screen audio and all. That usually pretty quickly let’s me know ‘where’ the cue is lacking. And then I ask why. Etc. sometimes just select all regions of the cue and hitting the delete button and starting over helps too.
    I know time constrains are tough. But I do everything I possibly can to have all cues leave the studio as best sounding and best working as I can in the time given. I’m sure we most do.

  • http://deaneogden.com Deane Ogden

    +1 with Marcelo. I believe that the problem probably originates with James’ approach to what it is he is actually doing. He’s writing a piece of music that he himself admits is “not for any particular project mind you, but just to write something.”

    That’s not a “cue”. A “cue”, in both traditional and current definitions, is music to picture. I would submit that what he’s *really* doing here is just simply writing to write, which is “composing”. I don’t use that word as all-encompassing—obviously “composing” can be much more than that—but only to illustrate this point:

    How we view what we write and why we are writing it directly affects the outcome of the piece. When I write a cue, I approach it differently than I do a concert work, and I approach a concert work differently than I do a library track, and I approach a library track differently than I do a game bed, and that differently than a song, and that differently than a…..

    You can’t think about a composition—if that’s all it is—as a “cue”, because it isn’t. I question whether you are forcing something that will never be there, because as Marcelo suggested, the other piece is missing.
