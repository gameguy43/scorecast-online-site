Heather Fenoughty: Putting Your Gear to Work

December 23, 2009 Written by Heather Fenoughty Uncategorized

I am not the most tech-minded of people.

I thought Logic 9 was 64-bit until it was pointed out to me on Twitter that it wasn’t. I didn’t care though. It still runs lovely and fast. And on Snow Leopard too, which is a shock because I thought I was being a little risky upgrading when I did… but so far so good. In fact better than good, to be fair.

Just so long as the equipment serves the higher function to create music as easily, quickly, efficiently and intuitively as possible, then I’m a happy bunny.

Let me explain… right after the jump…
I just wish I had an interface like Tom Cruise had in “Minority Report”. Imagine how much fun that would be. And you’d get a workout at the same time, none of this sore-arse-fat-belly-tired-eyes that sitting staring at an itty-bitty monitor does to one who chooses to follow our particular line of work.

Just an aside – that UI technology now exists, courtesy of Oblong Industries Inc.

If there’s one tip I can give aspiring composers that may actually cause a ruckus (because it has done before when I’ve aired opinions on this matter) is that whatever technology you have at your disposal, however low or high-end the set-up that’s in front of you…

Work to the strengths of your gear
Never mind you want an oboe solo but your sample sounds shite and you can’t get a live player round to record it before your deadline at 10 am tomorrow. Don’t have a oboe solo. Simple as that.

I don’t know what strengths and weaknesses your assembly has – that’s for you to work out through exploration and experimentation – the fun part!

Make time to experiment
…and remember to love the process. Being creative musically isn’t about being right and correct and perfect first time. It’s about hearing things from a new angle, smashing two genres together, blending sounds to create new and interesting breeds, digging deep into the available parameters to see if tweaking this particular fader will give birth to something wonderful. Or hideous. It doesn’t matter. That’s the process. And you never know when that bizzarro-world custom preset will be the perfect element for a score.

Make a wishlist
… of bits of gear or effects or samples that you want. Be specific. Man, I’m so psyched for flutter-tongued flutes at the moment. Ever since I heard it in Giacchino’s score for Star Trek (track 5 on OST, och, fires me up — ready for anything every time!).

So if your oboe sample is yukky and quacky, and you didn’t rethink that admittedly questionable decision to insert an oboe solo, find a sample library with a decent one. Or try out WIVIs as recommended by Jim Well. Research your options, ask advice, get opinions, listen listen listen.

Maybe the best thing to do is to sample one of those goddamn oboes yourself. Or just scrap it. They do sound pretty quacky at the best of times you know. And there’s no way you can hear the dialogue over the top so you know the sound mixer’s just gonna turn the volume on your track waaaay down…

Get minimalist
This might be a contentious one. I blogged about this recently, my new quest to get minimalist with my working as a way of sparking creativity. In response, someone commented on email that when composer guys get new toys (read: technological equipment) it ups their testosterone ‘cos they get all excited about it and that fires their creativity.

Man, is this for real? Men are weird.

Aw, just kidding, love you all really ;-)

But back to the point: strip away the bits and bobs of gear, software and hardware that you never use because they’ve been usurped in your setup by newer, better stuff. Do you really need 15 different reverb modules? Do you really use them all?

And don’t get me wrong, I love a new module or software gadget as much as the next guy, but only as a means to creating better and more appropriate music – for the potential for creating something that you couldn’t have made without it.

What’s gathering dust in the corner of your workstation? Box it up, put it in storage, sell it, give it away. Your new minimalist workspace and system will free the inner creative with the space of a streamlined playground.

In summary…

Music Technology is your servant.
A means to an end. It is there solely as a tool to create music. You may enjoy using it, but the end goal is the music itself. Don’t be a slave to it, to the latest bit of gear, just to keep up with the Jones’. Or the Zimmers.

Get what you need, work it to its strengths, find updates and alternatives for its weaknesses, remove what you no longer need or no longer serves you.

Good advice for life really.

Based in the North of England in the UK, HEATHER FENOUGHTY is an award-winning freelance composer and sound designer. She has scored several feature films and documentaries for the BBC and ITV, and her credits also include nearly 100 short films including a BAFTA-nominated drama (Nits, 2004). She has composed music and sound designed corporate advertisements for Nissan and other multi-national companies, with clients based all around the world. She also creates soundscapes for cutting-edge contemporary theatre, and her scores have played on the West End and off Broadway. You can find her SCOREcast bio (and links to her other sites) here.
