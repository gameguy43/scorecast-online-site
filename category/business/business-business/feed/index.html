These are my reflections so far.

Transit Tantrums

LA traffic on Thursday afternoon is slower than a George R. R. Martin novel…and in my case on a shuttle ride, just as gob-smacking when something finally happened. Two young girls in front were chatting excitedly about their impending adventure. Now, they weren’t exactly shrill but the conversation was audible to all. 40 minutes in, a baby-boomer businessman had an explosive outburst: “We DON’T CARE about your banal existence!”…stunned silence. A whimpered response. Simmering tension until they were dropped off…at the same hotel as Mr Explosion and his embarrassed wife. Welcome to Hollywood, girls!

I went to Chattanooga for a weekend, as you do. I had a dawn flight out of LAX so stayed at an airport hotel the night before. Passed the concierge as a man was SCREAMING HIS HEAD OFF at a cab driver for apparently ripping him off all of $9. He kept threatening to call the cops but finally backed down.

On LAX-CHA flight 1, the overhead lockers filled up and a bunch of people had to check their bags through to final destinations, including myself. I didn’t care; all I needed was my iPad and headphones. Others did care – airline staff were hammered by well-heeled women with an inflated sense of entitlement, indignant that they wouldn’t have access to their L’Oreal travel kits and Xanax.

Then there was the 4-hour delay in Charlotte due to the incoming plane having ‘mechanical issues’. Initially it was 1 hour, then 2…etc. Was it annoying? Yes. Did it warrant the ageing rocker with a gig that night having an absolute meltdown at the check-in girl, to the point that once again the police were mentioned? NO. I wasn’t overly happy myself, but would much rather be delayed than fall out of the sky. And for a while there it did feel like the precursor to an episode of ‘Air Crash Investigations’.

SO – my point is, this was very early into my visit and I was thinking ‘holy crap, is this REALLY what Americans are like?’ NO IT ISN’T. The symposium was FANTASTIC and when I returned to LA, the perpetually warm glow of Angelenos restored my societal faith. I guess travel brings out the best/worst in anyone and I saw some doozies of the latter kind. 99.9% of Americans are nice, genuinely interested in foreigners and usually mean it when they say “what can I do to help?”. Embrace that percentile.

Location/Vehiculation

Something anyone new to to town should consider carefully, well before landing. Unlike Melbourne, where I could walk 5 minutes to the Royal Botanic Gardens, Fawkner Park or my local cafe strip, LA isn’t really a city. Rather, it’s a vast sprawl of mini-cities connected by a web of freeways, each enclave having a unique flavour that may or may not suit your taste. Santa Monica and Venice are renowned for their beach lifestyle and slightly cooler air, but if most of your stuff’s happening Downtown or in The Valley, you’ll spend an awful lot of time on the road if you’re based out west. I knew I’d be hanging in Studio City/ Hollywood a lot, so took the time to find and pre-book the perfect AirBNB rental, smack in the middle. Slightly pricey but the location definitely saved me time and money. If AirBNB-style pads are beyond your budget, the ubiquitous CRAIGSLIST is a great place to find sub-lets, especially during summer holidays.

Mode of transport is a no-brainer. Unless you have incredibly helpful friends going to Point B (which I actually often do) or don’t mind spending hours traversing multi Metro routes, you need a car. If you have no choice but to get a cab somewhere, KNOW THIS: They’ll take at least 20 minutes to turn up, cost a lot and woe betide the passenger that dares to pay by credit card – even if your cab-finder app says it’s cool. I had a heated argument with a driver about this very point; in the end he was only placated when I tipped cash. If you don’t want to drive on a night out, a far better option is LYFT: a smartphone app that uses your location to find and order a driver in minutes, is 20% cheaper, 100% friendlier and automatically calculates a tip-inclusive fare – ONLY from your credit card. Ok, so they have weird pink moustaches on the cars, but most drivers also stock water and snack treats : )

LA Flakers — A Few Bad Players or a Team Malaise?

Much has been said and written about the notoriously slow/complete lack of follow through here in Tinseltown; one friend even suggested that ‘yes means maybe, maybe means no and people never say no’. Hackneyed phrases are quoted with mirth by the cheerfully cynical: “let’s do lunch” – ”we’ll talk” – etc. Well. I made a conscious decision to ignore the cliché and decide for myself what the deal is. Consequently, I’ve been very surprised by the reality:

Those Who’ve Flaked:

– Old Aussie friends now based here, who wanted things from me on a social/industry/material level (they got 2 of the 3) but gave nothing back. Even after I said “You’re not going to flake on me are you, we will catch up?” I tried to follow up. Twice. We never caught up, but I wouldn’t be surprised if I suddenly hear from them just before their next gig.
– Potential new LA friends via Bookface; they were going to ‘reach out’ one weekend…back in March. I tried to follow up. Twice. They de-friended me. Maybe my dry humour was misconstrued when I sent a link to that Four Tops track.

Those Who Haven’t Flaked:

– New composer friends, including very busy ones working on high-rating shows.
– Everyone in the industry I’ve emailed/left a message for, including those I don’t know that well or haven’t met yet. I did wait 3 weeks for one reply, but this was a new contact and I time-budgeted accordingly.

So – I’ve been mildly affected by flakelenos at social level, but industry/music folks have treated me with more than a modicum of politeness and respect. Maybe because I haven’t burdened myself with unrealistic expectations or exuded the desperate ‘I want something from you’ vibe that you can smell a mile away from so many young aspirants. If you come here thinking you’ll reach the summit straight away, THAT is flakey. Ed Hilary completed his first major climb 13 YEARS before scaling Everest. Patience, persistence and prudence, my friends…

Event Etiquette

Outside of my mentorship with Jeff Cardoni (CSI:Miami, Wilfred) I’ve attended a swag (Australian vernacular) of workshops, screenings, seminars and masterclasses. Each event has featured high calibre composers, orchestrators, conductors, directors, or moderators and has been well conceived. Sadly, I can’t say the same for some of the questions that inevitably slip through the relevance cordon at these things. IT’S NOT ABOUT YOU! Do you really think that standing up so that everyone can see you when you say “Hi, my name’s [wannabe composer X] and I’ve been working on/done a lot of [gratuitous promo Y] and I work in [DAW Z],” will make them think “Wow, I’m really interested in this guy and what he does, he must write like me because he uses DAW Z”…? Truth is, by the time you’ve got to the actual question, half the room has tuned out (or in my case, head in hands) and the A-lister will remember your face for all the wrong reasons when you push your way through the post-event throng. Then there are those that ask questions just to be ‘involved’ and ‘seen’, even if they’re “just a songwriter”…and you seriously want to punch them when they ask Thomas Newman if he “scores to picture”. Or the guy that asked Aaron Zigman if he “hums ideas in the shower”. Come on people! Be enthusiastic, but don’t be a wanker.

Weeding Out the Tall Poppy

Australians and New Zealanders are not known for overt self-promotion. In fact, those that say “I’m a big deal” are more often than not regarded as wankers and ostracised by their peers. This is the Tall Poppy Syndrome at work – a ‘cut them down’ attitude towards high achievers that is uniquely endemic to Australasia. When I was younger and just out of film music school, I was brimming with enthusiasm; thrilled with any work I could get and any composer I met. Said enthusiasm combined with the mild hero-worship of a composer I spent time with somehow lead to me being perceived as a ‘name-dropper’ by a high-ranking person in the Australian film music world. Fast forward four years to the Australian Screen Music Awards, which I was invited to by the organisation this person works for. At an industry lunch prior to the ceremony I made a concerted effort to have a pleasant conversation with this person. But when I told her I was looking forward to the awards, she asked “Are you attending legitimately?”

Happily, my experiences in LA have been the complete opposite. Here, it’s okay to talk about what you’ve done, what you’re doing, who you’ve connected with and what your aims are. I’ve been doing so with complete openness and honesty over the last 3 months and have been treated with more respect than I ever was in Australia. In fact, one delightful Executive Director (who holds a comparable position to my antipodean nemesis) now refers to me as her ‘favourite Kiwi’. And you know what? I can write about my awesome night at Jeff Beal‘s place without fear of someone telling everybody I’ve been blowing my own trumpet. I can mention the Hollywood Music in Media Awards competition I won – a scoring session with Mr. Beal; helping him record a 25-piece string orchestra. I can say that we exchanged CDs and that he particularly likes my choral stuff.

Los Angeles certainly takes some getting used to, but it’s so worth it. The top film music people I’ve met genuinely embrace talent, passion and commitment when they see it.

This, above all, makes me want to stick around.

]]> ../../../../2013/07/08/observations-of-an-aussie-speaking-kiwi-composer-in-la/feed/ 0 2075 You’re Not a Bad Composer ../../../../2013/06/13/youre-not-a-bad-composer/ ../../../../2013/06/13/youre-not-a-bad-composer/#comments Thu, 13 Jun 2013 21:05:56 +0000 ../../../../?p=2019 The film music culture over the years has a developed a bit of an elitist complex in the way we talk about the artform, leading some to believe that if you don’t have little black dots as prevalent as you have red blood cells in your veins then you must not be trying very hard. After all, we were born with this, right? It’s in our DNA.

Arguable, at best. A lot of people say we were born with certain aptitudes toward this gig but I would argue that while that might be true about musical intuition and a predisposition toward cognitive retention of scales  and other functional repeating mathematical sequences, quality skilled composition only comes out of tireless practice, varied experience and loads of trial and error. It’s not a far-flung theory then to assume that there could be a serious self-esteem issue working against emerging composers, dare I say even veteran ones who are willing to admit the ugly truth. Are we Weenies? Wimps? Candie-asses? I don’t think so. But we are human beings and from time to time every artist, no matter the discipline, needs to check the ‘ol barometer.

 

You’re Not a Bad Composer

… if you can’t seem to write anything today.

Writer’s block sucks. But even though the cool thing to say is “I don’t have time for that”, that still doesn’t mean it never happens. The truth is that it does happen… to everybody. You aren’t a bad composer if you are not able to write for whatever reason. You might be a late composer, but you aren’t a bad one.

How to get rid of it? Studies show that stress is the leading cause of “mental blocking” and that eliminating stress-related activities or events in your life can also lower your chances of stalling out creatively. Want to drill down further? Those same studies show that the number one cause of stress, at least among Americans, is finances. If that means something to you… get on it. Scale back, pare down, save more, spend less, slash and burn, budget hard, charge more — whatever you need to do, but get that financial monkey off your back, and fast. It’s a killer. Literally.

… if you don’t understand the theories behind music in film.

It just means you have a few things to learn if you are to be as effective a film music writer as you can be. You might already be an effective composer, but as a film composer you have to intimately understand the marriage of music and drama. If you don’t, then you are missing a huge piece of the puzzle as a talented composer looking to crack this particular medium. Obviously you aren’t a bad composer if this skill still eludes you, but I’ll warn you that without it you are always going to be composing from a defensive posture — meaning that whatever is thrown at you, you’ll either need to be lucky enough to intuitively feel it and magically hit that bullseye on the first dart throw or you’ll need to study up every time out, whether that’s through asking seasoned vets or “listening” to seasoned vets via Netflix streaming. Point being, you’ll always be playing “catch up”. That game gets old fast, slows you down, and eventually begins to erode your confidence.

But don’t worry… it’s an easy fix. All you have to do is find a way to absorb the principles and theories behind drama. Extended viewing and note-taking can do it, but so will auditing a few film appreciation courses at the local community college. Or reading a stack of good books on the subject. I’d recommend starting with Earle Hagen’s Advanced Techniques for Film Scoring. Hagen’s book is grossly outdated in its purview of technology, but the score analysis and score/script interaction is second to none. If you really want to steam your head, pick up Fred Karlin’s classic brick of a book On the Track. Often referred to as “the film scoring bible”, On the Track offers probably the most extensive perspective on various approaches to dramatic writing for the medium of film.

… if you’ve been rejected, fired or ignored. A lot.

That doesn’t mean you suck. Not necessarily, anyway. Although, a case could be made that if you got so far as to pitch for the gig it won’t matter if you suck anyway because you obviously have what it takes to get through the door. Any well-heeled major player knows that ain’t easy. But being told that “your music just isn’t what we’re looking for” is tough. What’s tougher, though, is hearing that time and again. And even still, that doesn’t necessarily mean you are a bad composer. You’ll have to gauge this one for yourself and use your best and most honest evaluations of your output, but there have been plenty of people — hundreds — who’ve been turned down dozens upon dozens of times before they ever scored a note to picture and even more than that who’ve been successful and then been told they were washed up, irrelevant, too old, too young, too inexperienced, too expensive, too high-profile, not high-profile enough… you name it. Point being: Rejection happens and you have to learn to accept it and deal with it appropriately.

There are many ways to face rejection, accept whatever truths come with it, and then discard the parts that don’t matter. One way is to be sure that you have the most healthy self-esteem you can have outside of your role as a creative person. I married a wonderful woman whom my every heartbeat pounds for. So, I don’t live every moment to be a composer or to even be a musician. It’s certainly a gigantic part of who I am at my core, but there are things and people in my life who are more important than any of this will ever be. I’m not alone in this feeling. Ask anyone who is creative and probably over the age of thirty. The secondary things in life, you realize over time, matter very little. Success takes on a whole new definition when love, children, family, and even the closest friends take root in your life as a middle-aged person. I spend a huge majority of my time trying to hip younger people who are ready to jump off the cliff of career suicide to this fact. When you get rejected, it’s not a rejection of you as a person, it’s simply that the timing of your artistic expression and that of the source material don’t align. That’s it. It’s the wrinkle of cosmic scheduling. You could bring the same set of demo cues to the next meeting and have the biggest film in history on your hands. Nobody knows. But whatever the case, rejection should only hit your art, not your heart. It’s just not worth it and it never ever lasts anyway, so why devote the emotional bandwidth?

… if you don’t know how to read music.

Clearly, you will be at major and immediate disadvantage if you don’t read music and you are trying to break into the scene. Not only does this hamper your speed and agility as the point person for the music department on a production, but it will also hurt you as a potential candidate for an apprenticeship — the avenue by which a lot of film composers get their start. Like it or not, and argue all you want, but the film music business still thrives and depends on people who can read dots on a page. Period. Whether you are a musician at a session, an orchestrator sorting out lines for the composer, a copyist handling last-minute changes, or the composer yourself, you will need to read often. Written music comprehension is to a composer what the interpreting of blueprints is to an architect. Not knowing how to read the architectural drawings on the house you are building will be an unmitigated disaster. Imagine being a non-reading composer on the scoring stage and having to quickly change a lead-line for your director, transpose it in your head, and rattle it off to your 1st chair trumpet or clarinetist. Can you imagine? Well, please do… because that exact scenario is not a rare experience for the in-the-trenches film composer.

Does not reading make you a bad composer? Of course not. Even a few top-tier composers in the game started out just humming their melodies to an orchestrator. But before long, almost all of them learned to read and write at least functionally. Need to brush up? Take some private lessons; there are hundreds of teachers in LA, NYC, and London who will take you on as a private student and bring you up to functional and workable levels in no time. Don’t be embarrassed by your inability to read notes; not all composers who are working today are ace readers, either, and no matter how good you are, I promise you your musicians will always be ten times better anyway. But even still, having that very basic ability in your skill set will remove what is probably the biggest hurdle to self-confidence as a composer. It is your job as the captain of this ship to have a solid grasp of the fundamentals. This is as fundamental as it gets.

… if you don’t live in a music mecca.

There you are… in Delaware. Don’t worry. Geographical misfortune does not a bad composer make. There’s been a lot of talk in the last decade about geography and how it correlates to the level of sophistication of a composer, implying that the farther away you are from a major music center the less “dedicated” you must be to your craft. Obviously that’s bullshit. It doesn’t matter where you live, your talents are your talents. Now, how accessible your talents are to those who need them… well, that’s another matter entirely.

You’re not a bad composer if you live in Cornwall. Still living with the folks in Texarkana? No problem. Never underestimate the powerful combo of a gigabit Internet connection and a fair amount of good old-fashioned hustle. You can accomplish a lot with an IMDbPro account, a well thought-out demo reel and a cell phone. But if you are determined to be in the thick of the film world, the brick and mortar studios are all still standing in Los Angeles. Film and TV seem to be the only industries who haven’t figured out that the world is flat like the video game industry has. They are making strides in that direction, but they aren’t there yet. Post-production on films and shows happens in LA.

… just because somebody says you are.

Human beings are strange creatures. We love to kick our wounded. I don’t know what it is, but people will love you when it’s easy to and hate you when it’s easier. There is nothing that will take the wind out of your sails more than negative feedback from colleagues. Complete strangers? Whatevs. But colleagues… That’s a tough knife in the gut. And there are those out there who are self-loathing enough to let you have it. Believe me. As I’ve just finished a very personal project, the barbs from the people I figured would lob them last have found their mark. And for a while, I internalized every one, taking it apart and searching for where I’d gone wrong. But then I realized that those people are not my audience. Do you make music for other musicians? I sure don’t. So what was I worried about?

Here’s the thing: The people who love what you do love it because it does something for them. It fills something in them. It cures what’s ailing, if only for five minutes. That is your audience. The fans. Not the freaks… the fans. The freaks are just freaking. They need something to freak about so that they don’t have to keep staring at their own work. There will be people who “get” what you do and there will be people who just don’t and never will. Just accept that those people aren’t the people you are doing this for and they likely never will be. Don’t read your comment feed. Never punch down. Even the biggest stars in entertainment get railed by the folks who are sitting at home doing nothing. If you happen to convert a non-believer through consistent output that ultimately changes their mind, awesome… You’re lucky. That doesn’t happen often. Take great care of them and don’t abuse their commitment to your art by following up with a piece of crap. But if you never do, remember who you are playing to: Your fans. The dedicated few. Your niche. Your tribe. Your supporters. The people who will join your mailing list, share your content in their social circles, attend your panel discussions, and email occasionally to tell you they “enjoyed this last one”. That is the person you are working for. That is where your mental bandwidth deserves allocation. Not thinking about how to get new fans…. but thinking about how to care for the ones who are here already. That’s an endgame that matters.

]]> ../../../../2013/06/13/youre-not-a-bad-composer/feed/ 13 2019 What Are You Worth? ../../../../2012/09/20/what-are-you-worth/ ../../../../2012/09/20/what-are-you-worth/#comments Fri, 21 Sep 2012 03:16:35 +0000 ../../../../?p=1777 I was talking with a friend, who, on behalf of his brother, was asking for advice on getting into the industry.  Specifically, his brother is the musician in the family while my friend is a more traditional businessman who sells product. My pal asked me, “How do you determine what to charge? I know that in my industry I have to do an analysis of what a product costs me to produce and how much profit margin I need to make to survive. If a music mix takes a certain number of hours and you can determine its cost to show the client, why can you not get people to pay for that?” This inevitably led to the train of thought that you charge what you are worth — a topic we have discussed here at SCOREcast many times in the past. But upon further discussion, I determined that in advising his brother on how to charge for his work, there was a fundamental problem in their approach. They were focused on selling the wrong product.

What Are You Selling?

Answer this question for yourself. As a composer, what product are you selling? Think about it. Answer honestly in your head before you read on.

 

…(Queue Merv Griffin’s “Jeopardy” theme)…

 

Did you answer “music?” Did you answer “a film score?” Did you answer “fully produced tracks?” “Original compositions?” Well… you are wrong on all accounts. What is the product you are selling? The product you are selling is YOU!

It is a fundamental shift in how you look at your business. It’s why some composers can charge seven figures and perhaps you can not — those composers are worth more. But if you are frustrated because you feel the fully produced tracks on your demo are just as polished and unique as those composer’s then you are taking your eye off the ball. It is NOT about your music. It is about YOU. You are the product. ALL of you.

Of course you produce music, but that is only part of it. You also make the director feel like his or her movie was the best thing made this year. You also solve problems with a positive and professional approach that makes people want to be around you for months at a time on a project. You also communicate well and deliver on time and on budget. There is so much more to it.

The challenge in setting a price for your music is really just determining what YOU are worth to the client, rather than what your music is worth. A Blu-Ray can be purchased at the nearest store for about $15-$20 USD. There is a value assessment to that product that the market has determined and can sustain. But with us, our value changes — sometimes overnight. If your movie hits #1 in the country, all of a sudden you’ll be worth more. It is not like your music is any different today than it was yesterday before your film hit number one at the box office, but the value of having you on a future project is now much more valuable to a producer or director. It has nothing to do with your music.

The honest truth: If you are having a hard time getting your fee up it is because the industry does not feel YOU are worth that. If they felt you were worth the money, they would find the money to cover your fee. It also plays into Deane Ogden‘s frequent posts about the culture of free. If we agree to free gigs, we are simply affirming to the industry that we are worth… nothing.

In conclusion, the next time you re-evaluate your business plan and try to determine what your fee is, remember what it is that you are actually trying to sell. Also remember that you are not a composer looking for a job, you are a business looking for clients. It is a subtle distinction but an important one. It shifts the dynamic of where you sit on the ladder with your clients.

The product is not your music — the product is YOU!

]]> ../../../../2012/09/20/what-are-you-worth/feed/ 19 1777 SoundCloud 101 ../../../../2012/01/16/soundcloud-101/ ../../../../2012/01/16/soundcloud-101/#comments Mon, 16 Jan 2012 14:40:26 +0000 ../../../../?p=1352  

 

SoundCloud is a versatile and effective platform for composers and sound content creators of all kinds, and is arguably the next big thing in online audio. Over the last year and a half I have invested hundreds of hours on SoundCloud and would like to spend a few minutes with you to share a few nuggets I’ve picked up along the way, that may help you as a composer, and that may shed some light on the community aspects of the service, as well.

Ten Point Briefing

  1. SoundCloud is an audio platform for sharing, promotion and collaboration, with its own social networking framework.
  2. SoundCloud is accessed via desktop web browsers, embedded webpage widgets and smartphone apps.
  3. SoundCloud began as a music file sharing service in Berlin in 2007, by Alexander Ljung and Eric Wahlforss, growing from 1 million members in May 2010 to 9 million members in December 2011.
  4. Members can upload sound files in all common formats, with public or limited visibility, downloadability and all common licensing options, Buy links and a vanity URL, for example soundcloud.com/the-chemical-brothers.
  5. Tracks can have players embedded on external websites with flexible appearance and browser compatibility options.
  6. Members can follow other members, provide feedback via timed comments, mark tracks as favourites, keep abreast of other member activity on a dashboard, and create and join groups.
  7. Detailed stats are available, showing who played your track, where, when, how often, and who downloaded or saved your track to their favourites list.
  8. SoundCloud is well integrated with Twitter, Facebook and other online social networks. At the Facebook f8 conference in September 2011, Facebook announced a strategic technology partnership with SoundCloud, effectively providing SoundCloud embedded exposure to over 800 million Facebook users.
  9. Membership is free but there are premium accounts ranging from €29 to €500 offering various levels of storage, stats, widgets, support and other options.
  10. Finally, and crucially, SoundCloud is a community.

Who Uses SoundCloud?

Essentially, sound content creators of all types, and listeners of all tastes. From dance music producers like Deadmau5, Skrillex and Swedish House Mafia to A-list pop stars like Lady Gaga, Radiohead and Bjork, from indie singer/songwriters to podcasters, comedians and even newspapers like The Economist, and of course composers.

I asked a handful of film and television composers to share their experiences of SoundCloud, starting with LA-based Kerry Muzzey, who enjoys the community-oriented learning and social aspects of SoundCloud:

I have fallen in love with SoundCloud. I’ve enjoyed it not only for the many musical discoveries I’ve made but also for the ability to interact with other composers. I love that so many have been willing to share their tech tips and secret recipes. Sure, it’s a really simple and slick demo-hosting site: but take some time to go a little deeper and you’ll be surprised at the community you’ll discover: these are people who are passionate about music and about the beauty of sound.

Sean Beeson from Ohio has over 400,000 track plays on SoundCloud and gains from the marketing aspects it offers:

I use SoundCloud to gauge the success of my online marketing campaigns. I can see what sites people are accessing my music from, where in the world they are listening from, and what music the are listening to. It helps me to cater my sets to what music is most listened to in the hopes that I can gain greater exposure for my work.

Geographically, members are spread across the world in roughly the following order: US, UK, Germany, rest of Europe, Canada, Russia, Australia, Japan, rest of world.

UK-based Marie-Anne Fischer, film and television composer and chapter director of SCOREcast: London, has experience with a number of online music platforms including ReverbNation. Marie-Anne shared a little about using SoundCloud for professional showreel and private sharing purposes:

I discovered SoundCloud about 10 months ago when I started writing demos for Sonokinetic because they use SoundCloud to post their demos. I like the direct connection and simplicity. SoundCloud is becoming more interesting to me as I have found more film and media composers on SoundCloud than I had on ReverbNation. I like the group features on SoundCloud where there is no clutter. I tend to be using SoundCloud more recently to link prospective clients to my showreel and being able to share private tracks is a great feature.

Given the fact that there are over 9 million members, of course not all members are professionals in the line of work they present on SoundCloud and membership is arguably weighted towards amateur enthusiasts, but that does not detract from its usefulness in professional spheres.

Can SoundCloud Help Your Career?

SCOREcast: London’s own Dominik Johnson, world music production specialist:

SoundCloud, for me, is one of the best community music tools online at the moment for the pro musician / composer / producer. I know the audio quality on my music (typically 48kHz/24bit) can be lost with some of the upload compression, but to me, that’s a price worth paying. A few months ago an independent film director from Berlin got in touch after hearing a few of my works on there, we got chatting on there, then we starting having Skype calls – and BOOM! I’ve been commissioned to write some tracks for his upcoming feature documentary – and yes, his production team does have a budget for the music.

Dominik touched on a point above with regards to upload/playback quality, which I’ll explore in the Technical section further below.

I know of one case where the SoundCloud user was not necessarily the beneficiary of a career opportunity. LA-based film composer, orchestrator and conductor Deon Vozov has this story:

How SoundCloud Compares to Other Audio Platforms

In the user content audio hosting space, SoundCloud competes with, among others, BandCamp, ReverbNation and Myspace.

BandCamp is mainly focused on allowing independent artists to sell their work, and are funded in part on a commission basis. Users can customise the look and feel of their music pages and have their tracks downloadable in many formats. BandCamp has attracted some commercially established artists including Amanda Palmer to abandon their labels and self-promote (interestingly, Amanda Palmer also has a SoundCloud account).

I do find SoundCloud much better for getting feedback and it has very good community features (commenting, groups, etc.) that Bandcamp doesn’t have.

— Jeremiah Pena, Film Composer, Utah

In terms of usefulness to composers, BandCamp may serve a function to sell original soundtracks if the production licensing terms permit, but since BandCamp does not offer embedded players, the composer would have to set up a specific BandCamp page outside their main website, which may not be a desirable solution for those who like a one-stop-shop approach to their web presence.

ReverbNation aims to provide a collaboration and communication platform as well as music hosting for independent artists, and have an embeddable player.

Marie-Anne also offers us a comparison with her SoundCloud experience:

You can personalise ReverbNation’s profile which you can’t on SoundCloud but that hasn’t stopped me from using SoundCloud.

I like the statistics SoundCloud collects and provides me and also their feature where people can favour a track and leave a comment.

The great big difference between SoundCloud and ReverbNation is that SoundCloud is purely functional and works well where as on ReverbNation you can add your personal touch to your profile, link music to multiple stores, integrate with other websites, create newsletters, blogs and apps are only but a few of their features.

Marie-Anne is right about SoundCloud being less customisable than ReverbNation, which takes a modular approach to the profile page with a lot of control over content and branding. On SoundCloud, personalisation can be applied to player widgets for website-matching style and colour scheme, for user dropbox page branding, for linking other profiles like Twitter, Facebook etc. and for essential items like profile image and an HTML-enabled description area.

In terms of how that translates into commercial opportunities, I would argue that for a composer it is the presentation of your professional website that is more important than the presentation of your profile on the audio hosting website, but that can be debated in the comments below.

One other factor to consider is the service value available to non-paying customers. Although ReverbNation’s free account allows more tracks to be hosted than SoundCloud’s free account, the size of each track is limited to 8MB which forces lossy upload format; the longer the track the lossier it has to be to fit into 8MB.

Myspace does not make for great headlines these days, having been sold to News Corporation for $580 million in 2005 and then bought by Specific Media and Justin Timberlake in 2011 for $35 million. The main technical issues reported by professional SoundCloud users are well-known and steps have been taken to address them.

1. SoundCloud playback is compressed at 128kbps, which many audiophiles can discern distinctly from studio-quality uncompressed recording.

In previous email exchanges, SoundCloud staff let me know that they’re always thinking of and considering ways to make the experience better for both creators and listeners. One way for creators to offer higher quality is to enable downloads for their sounds so listeners can access the file in its original quality. They’re regularly discussing this topic internally and are considering changes in the future.

In the meantime, SoundCloud seems to remain the platform of choice for many sound libraries’ demo tracks, including Sonokinetic, 8dio and others, which is a good indicator that the quality, although not perfect, is widely acceptable for professional demo purposes.

2. Flash-based SoundCloud players are subject to the inherent stability and compatibility issues of Flash, particularly where smartphones often have trouble showing Flash controls.

SoundCloud recently released HTML5 versions of their embeddable widgets, addressing both the stability problem and making it compatible with most smartphones and mobile devices including iPhones and iPads. The new HTML5 widgets have been well-received and many say better looking than their Flash predecessors.

3. Some users reported that the encoding produced glitchy playback, particularly in high frequency ranges and at high volume levels.

Up until recently, SoundCloud advised: “This rarely happens but if it does, we suggest uploading in 320kbps 44.1kHz with at least -3dB of headroom” but a recent discovery has revealed that this is no longer required.

FLAC is the recommended upload format for optimal playback quality, but this may not be ideal for download purposes and is probably best-suited for playback-only tracks (non-downloadable). For people who would prefer to have their files available for download, the higher the quality of the lossy file they upload, the higher quality the encoding will be.

The Help Page on this topic offers this advice:

Sometimes our transcoding system can create audio artifacts, as we transcode all tracks to 128 kbps mp3 for streaming playback. Uploading a lossless or high-quality lossy file will usually reduce these to a minimum, but unfortunately there’s not much we can do for the handful of individual tracks that are still affected.

If you choose to make your track downloadable though, the version users can download will be an exact copy of the version you uploaded, without any transcoding.

This is an important point with regards to using SoundCloud for track sharing between yourself and the film production co, or for demo or collaborative purposes: The track you download is the exact track that was uploaded, regardless of the playback encoding. So you can happily share your 192kHz 32-bit float wav file if that tickles your fancy, in full trust that that is how it will be received. You can also do so in complete privacy without violating your NDA.

4. SoundCloud has experienced unscheduled downtime in the past.

For 24 hours in October 2011, SoundCloud fell victim to an aggressive DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) attack leading to slow response times and occasional periods of downtime.

No data was lost in the attack and no user profile security was compromised, but better measures have since been put in place to defend against future attacks of that nature. Unfortunately, no website is untouchable from such an attack attempt.

My SoundCloud Story

I joined SoundCloud in August 2010 and from the start had a revelatory and inspirational experience, so much so that by New Year’s Day 2011 I resolved to turn my life-long passion and hobby into a career. This I knew would be a long path but one that would hopefully be rewarding at every step of the way, and as 2011 passed by, I reflected on those intentions in confidence that things were finally moving in the right direction.

My first steps on SoundCloud were like most others—a one-way path of uploading music and hoping it would be heard and liked by someone, somewhere, just as most creative artists hope for their own art to be observed and appreciated. It only really happened for me when I realised that it was more than a music hosting site but a community as well.

And so I started engaging in-depth with other people’s music—going through their entire track lists to understand their own creative processes and leaving comments and, drawing on my own experience, leaving constructive feedback along the way. This revealed the reciprocal nature of SoundCloud interaction when all of a sudden my own tracks started drawing a lot of attention.

Community

The third stage of my SoundCloud involvement began in June when I decided to try tapping into the collective creativity of the SoundCloud community.

  • The first crowd-sourcing project was extremely simple: “I invite you to suggest a name for this track”. SoundCloud staff noticed this, thought it was a cool idea, and tweeted it to their then 80,000 followers. My track got absolutely bombed by name suggestions and the winning title and subtitle were really well-suited to the track (White Light Rendezvous: For Those Who Follow). With around 200 followers at the time, I then gained another 200 or so on the back of that mini-project.
  • The second project was a little more ambitious. I played a 9 minute piano improvisation and called it “I invite you to suggest an orchestral composition with timed comments on this track.” This got a lot of attention too with comments like “a harp arpeggio phrase for next 8 bars” and after about 100 comments I spent a month composing those elements over the original piano improv. I called the resulting track “SoundCloud Sinfonietta: You Composed This” which quickly racked up over 5,000 plays and was a finalist in the G-Technology Europe Driven Creativity Awards.
  • The third project is still on-going and is called “Your Tune” where I invited followers to share a 20-second solo melody and composed a full-length orchestral or band instrumental track using the submitted melody.

Career Break

As well as the community projects I run on SoundCloud, I also produce a lot of original work and get involved in many collaborations. Through a friend on SoundCloud, one of my original tracks “Ochre Threads” was heard by an independent filmmaker in LA, also on SoundCloud, who is working on his 3rd feature-length film and wanted to use the track for a particular scene.

The same filmmaker then spotted a track I released in December 2011—“Of Valour”—and on the strength of that alone gave me the opportunity to score the entire feature-length film, due for release in Summer 2012.

This is potentially the career break I’ve dreamed of for many years and could be my ticket to full-time paid filmscoring work in the future.

SoundCloud Communities

Apart from members who used underhanded spam-like tactics to drive playcount and visibility, the most active and successful accounts are those of people who take the time to listen and provide feedback to other SoundClouders. The communities thrive in the timed comments, the track favouritings, the collaborations and in groups, especially those which have forum-like chat areas. There are also regular live meetups across the world, like this one in October 2011, which Yoko Ono got involved in too.

Naturally I prefer an organically grown community to one built on spam and follow-backs, and contributed “How to Grow Your SoundCloud Community” to SoundCloud HQ, which they keep on the front page of the user guide here: soundcloud.com/101

The SoundCloud community is well supported by dedicated community teams with staff in Berlin, London and San Francisco, headed by Europa Award-Winner David Noël. The team actively promotes community projects via their blog, Facebook page (500,000+ followers) and Twitter account (150,000+ followers), for example a global project collecting translations of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in as many languages as possible.

One very charming aspect of the SoundCloud Community Team is their daily SoundClouder Of The Day nomination, where a member is singled out for their community activity or other unique attribute that deserves recognition. SCOTD alumni are then often contacted for input on community questions and feedback, and are given a little boost in follower-ship by means of a shoutout on their social networks.

“This Week in SoundCloud” is another community project highlighting interesting discoveries across the ‘Cloud, in podcast format on a weekly basis (incidentally, the jingle for this podcast was written by me and 2 other SoundClouders).

There is a lot to the SoundCloud platform, it is not just an audio widget on your demo page. I’ll end where I began: SoundCloud is a versatile and effective platform for composers and sound content creators of all kinds. It is arguably the next big thing in online audio, and maybe most important… it is a community.

10 Lessons on “Breaking In”

I learn something new on every film and further hone my craft and skill with each project.  There are some truly fundamental things I have learned in the last few years.  Many years ago, fresh out of USC’s Scoring for Motion Pictures and Television Program, I was given one particular piece of advice, from a highly respected composer that influenced me greatly as I set out on my composing career.  I would like to share this advice as well as some of my own lessons with you.  Here is my “top ten” list of lessons learned while breaking into the industry as a composer.

1) You have to break into the business of tomorrow, not the business of today.

Basil Poledouris said that. Today’s working directors and producers already have established composer relationships they go back to over and over. You have to find the up and coming directors and producers of “tomorrow” and work with them now before they make it in Hollywood. When they eventually get their first studio gig, they will usually go back to the people they know and trust from when they were struggling themselves. Hopefully you will be one of those people on their team.

2) Everyone’s path is different.

So don’t think if you do what the guy next to you is doing, you will get the same result.  Some composers are at the right place (or on the right film) at the right time, and their preparation synergistically meets with their opportunity.  Some pound the grind stone for years and years before getting noticed.  Careers are like snowflakes.  No two career paths are alike.

3) Set a precedent as Department Head.

Music Composer (as on-screen credited on a film or show) is a “department head” job. The only way you will get bigger and better department head jobs (on multi-million dollar films) is to have a proven track record AS A DEPARTMENT HEAD on previous SUCCESSFUL films. Being the composer’s assistant, ghostwriter, orchestrator or musician on studio films do not count as a DEPARTMENT HEAD and will not help a studio see you as less of a risk in the composer job. I have learned this from 4 different studio level producers. This is all I have been hearing for the last 8-9 years.  Don’t get me wrong, composer assistants, orchestrators, etc…are positions to be respected and it is valid work.  It just usually does not lead to a career as the lead composer.  And it certainly will not give you the freedom to develop your own “sound” which is also very important in building your brand as a composer.   Remember, your sound is what you will get hired for.

4) Main title, on-screen credits matter most.

If you are not the on-screen credited person, the credit really will not count to a producer hiring you to be their lead composer.  This is a subtle difference.  Again…working under the wing of another composer is valuable experience and work to be proud of, but you were not the lead composer in charge.  Your reputation was not the one on the line.  It is that on-screen, single, main title card credit of “music by…” that really matters most on your resumé.

5) Recommendations are key.

A word of mouth recommendation is a more powerful influence to getting a gig than anything else. If you get a strong recommendation from someone a director or producer respects, they will hire you nine times out of ten. I know plenty of great composers.  The ones who do not make it usually fail due to issues completely unrelated to being a composer. The ones who have made it to various degrees (while competent in their craft) were not always the best composers…but were great at all of the other things. They were pleasant to be around and had a work ethic and professionalism that pushed them above the rest.  And thus…they kept getting hired.

Holding true to these lessons over the last few years while working and following my own path has led me to conclude that doing my own music, developing my own sound, and networking to get my own composer gigs was the path for me. Maybe it isn’t for you. Perhaps you may want to experience the industry from the perspective of being someone’s assistant for a while; you can obtain real world experience and at least make a living while working in the field of music. Again for me, I would much rather spend all of my time on building my own credit list, reputation and skills as the lead composer.

A pro is someone who gets paid for what they do, pure and simple.

Compare this with the opposite, the definition of an amateur, who does the same thing only for the love of it (i.e. for free).

As soon as you get paid, you can legitimately call yourself a professional. A ‘Pro’.

But, ultimately, only by being professional can you get to this point and stay there.

A roundup of the qualities of the Pro Film Music Composer (by no means exhaustive!) follows…

1. It’s business, not a hobby
If you’re in this business as a pro, then you’re a businessman/woman. That means working regularly, persistently on not only your music but on your business.

Get your affairs in order: tax, insurance, working environment, legal requirements. You’re in this game to sell your music, to get paid for composing.

2. Be an expert
Educate yourself in composition, musical styles, and methods of collaboration and delivery.

Keep your knowledge up-to-date with new technological innovations, musical trends, media language, etc. Have an informed opinion.

Note: this does not mean jumping on the bandwagon/keeping-up-with-the-Jones’ by buying every new piece of technology that comes out. This does mean knowing what’s new, what it can do, and what it may or may not be able to do for you now and in the future.

3. Be objective
You’re creating raw materials for someone else to manipulate, not a completely finished, final product. Don’t get precious.

Accept criticism graciously. It’s not personal; even when it seems like it is.

4. Pro-actively research and create opportunities
Use downtime between gigs to update your showreel, website and promotional materials — you never know when you’ll get asked for them.

Seek out new contacts and build relationships; plan ahead, refine your business systems, identify potential new opportunities in the making, and then go after them.

5. Invest in your business
Invest time in your education, your experience, and in building relationships with potential clients.

Invest money in equipment — the right tool for the right job — and, again, in your education, both in your skills and in running a business.

Invest energy: this isn’t a job you can just fall into. It takes focus, motivation, persistence and courage. Get on and do it already.

Invest in your team and build support networks for streams of advice and leads on sources of work, and identify potential assistants and colleagues that can bring complementary skills when you might need them. Build your team in advance to save essential writing time when that call finally does come through.

6. Take responsibility

  • Be responsible for your business; you know you’ve no-one to hold accountable but yourself.
  • Act professionally: deliver on time; be courteous, civilised, and amiable to work with, yet focused; be prepared for any and all contingencies you reasonably can be; learn from past mistakes and plan for future successes.

So whilst being paid for a gig defines you in the moment as a ‘Pro’, being a Pro will more importantly get you to that point and give you the best shot at staying Pro!

The second time I pitched, I didn’t get the gig. There were two other composers in the running, and the best man won, to be fair. The pitch was very specific and precise, and I twisted it a little to suit my style, skills and gear. I had a few redrafts too after feedback, which meant I got closer to the desired product, a luxury not usually afforded to pitching composers. I think I was treated better since I’d worked with the director before and he was in my corner!
When I heard the music on the show though, I realised why I didn’t get the gig. They wanted Tibetan vocals and ethnic instruments with a very, intimate, human edge, and I’d given them music better suited to epic panoramas on a nature show. Oops.
At the time, I was pretty gutted – I’d assumed that since they’d come to me first and that the director wanted to work with me again that it was a done deal.
Trust me, it’s never a done deal ’til the fat lady sings.
Morals of the tale:
  1. If the client has been very specific, really do follow that brief, to the letter. If they’ve come to you with the offer to pitch, they obviously know your stuff already. No need to do more of the same. Be as accurate as you can.
  2. Never assume you’ve got the gig simply because you have an awesome working relationship with the director/producer/editor/music supervisor. He’s not the only one who has the final say…
  3. Don’t take it personally. Rejection is par for the course in this line of work. It’s not personal, it’s not that you’re a bad person, it’s (hopefully) not that your music is dire. It just wasn’t right, and someone else got closer to that bullseye. Find out what the finished music was like, learn what it was that they meant by their brief, get over it and move on to the next job.
The third time I pitched, I have to be honest… my heart wasn’t really in it. It was not a passion project, but rather a friend’s film, co-produced with a big-name channel, destined for broadcast – and so had the promise of back-end royalties. My heart became less in it when I got 3 very different briefs over the course of 6 weeks with various revisions going back and forth. By the end, I was still putting in the effort, but the love for the work had gone. Ugh.
Can you guess whether I got the gig? You’d be right I suspect – no, I did not win this particular commission.
Did I mind? Surprisingly (or not), no, partly because by this point I had developed that elephant-thick skin that is so important when dealing with criticism and/or rejection. But partly I was also a little relieved. The brief was really tricky to pin down and changed with every new conversation I had… and I took that as a sign of things to come. Also the tracks I wrote were awesome and I’m now selling them as library tracks. Win-win.
Moral of the tale:
  1. When pitching, if the brief is hazy, ill-defined or keeps changing, you better develop some awesome mind-reading skills. Otherwise you have to rely on the gamble of guessing and hoping. This isn’t to say that you should avoid these gigs if they’re the only ones available to you at the time. You may get lucky. Just don’t get too attached to the outcome.
  2. If a brief keeps changing as you pitch, it doesn’t bode well for the rest of the gig even if you do somehow win the commission.
  3. If it’s not a passion project, or even a subject matter that interests you vaguely, the other benefits better be good. ‘Cos if they’re not… why are you pitching for it in the first place? There has to be some inspiration for those lines and dots, you know.
  4. The music you wrote should be pretty awesome if you put your all into it. It still belongs to you. Why not flog it through a music library, or offer it to your local filmmaking buddies for non-exclusive licensing, if it’s appropriate for that sort of project. Sell it on iTunes. Don’t let it just sit there, put it to work.

Now, as I write this I’m pitching again. I actually just finished uploading the tracks to the ftp!

Yikes.
This time the brief is pretty, well, brief. And not really a brief, more a transcription/reduction of an email conversation between the production company and the funding clients, where they decide that they’d like to compare different styles for the work rather than being very specific. It’s really very open-ended. But there are some ‘style’ tracks that the clients appear to like and also a sample of the video to work to, which gives an idea of where they’re headed. And the genre of the project is sooooo in an area that I really want to get into, so the love is there.
So this one is a little bit of an experiment. I’m sticking to the ‘vibe’ of the style tracks, but they’re definitely in my ‘niche’ writing style too. I’m providing lots of variety within those styles, some very contemporary, some more orchestral and cinematic and also there’ll be tracks that blend the two styles.
If I get the gig, I want to be writing music that I want to write, that I love to write, that will make me jump out of bed in the morning in the knowledge that I’m scoring a series I’m excited about.
If I’m not the right gal for the gig with these tracks, I won’t be gutted that I didn’t get the job. I’ll be happy that I didn’t get contracted into writing for a project that wasn’t my cup of tea.
But I really do hope that I am the right gal for the job.
